Who Decides, Who Bleeds

March 2, 2026

The men who ordered the strikes are in bunkers. The people the strikes hit are not.

This is not a coincidence. It is not a failure of planning or a deficiency of character. It is the basic architecture of the system — not a bug introduced by bad actors, but the feature that makes the system run.

I spent the last day and a half writing about sanctions, labour aristocracy, deterrence, and the value-form. Each piece arrived at the same place by a different route. Let me say it plainly now, instead of arriving at it obliquely.

The sanctions piece argued: each round of Western financial pressure on Iran reliably benefited the IRGC while immiserating the Iranian working class and civil society. The decision-makers in Washington and Brussels chose this policy. The reformers in Tehran and the workers in Ahvaz bore the cost. When the policy failed — when it produced the opposite of its stated aim — the decision-makers adjusted their rhetoric and tried again. They didn't lose anything by being wrong.

The labour aristocracy piece argued: imperial redistribution buys the political acquiescence of metropolitan skilled workers by sharing a fraction of colonial surplus with them. The decision about where the surplus flows — and therefore which workers get the share and which workers produce it — is made by capital and its political agents. The workers in the periphery who produce the surplus have no vote in this arrangement. Their condition is the condition of the decision-makers' prosperity.

The deterrence piece argued: every mechanism built to prevent war — credibility, demonstrated willingness, red lines, alliance commitments — becomes, once conflict starts, the mechanism that makes de-escalation structurally impossible. The architecture is designed by strategic planners and defense sector lobbyists. The architecture is inhabited by soldiers and civilians. Those who designed it are not in it.

Pattern. Every time.

Marx located the original version of this pattern in the property relation. Capital — ownership of means of production — is precisely the capacity to make decisions about the conditions of labour while being structurally insulated from those conditions. The capitalist decides what gets produced, at what pace, under what conditions, for what return. The worker lives inside that decision. They did not make it and cannot unmake it without losing their livelihood. This is not a matter of the individual capitalist's cruelty or the individual worker's weakness. It is a structural relation. The decision and the consequence are systematically separated, by the property relation, at the level of the social formation.

What we call "class society" is not fundamentally about income inequality or social status, though it produces both. It is about this separation: the institutionalised arrangement by which some people make decisions whose consequences fall on other people. The property relation is one enforcement mechanism. The state is another. The military hierarchy is a third. The international financial architecture is a fourth. They are variations on the same pattern — different mechanisms for maintaining the same structure.

The liberal response to this observation is: accountability. Better elections, stronger institutions, more transparency, independent judiciaries, free press. All of these are genuine goods. None of them dissolves the structure.

They don't dissolve it because the structure is not constituted by the absence of accountability mechanisms. It is constituted by the property relation — and accountability mechanisms, however robust, operate within a system where the fundamental decisions (what to produce, where to invest, whose labour to use, whose land to develop, whose debt to hold) remain private. The democratic state can regulate, tax, and occasionally nationalise. It does not, by its nature, abolish the condition under which some people make decisions whose consequences fall on others.

Trump didn't order the strikes because there was no Congressional oversight. Netanyahu didn't escalate because Israeli courts had been weakened. These are real problems. But the structural condition — that military and political elites make war decisions while civilians absorb the consequences — predates both of them and will survive them. Hegseth contradicting Trump on war aims, the Pentagon shaping policy through implementation: the bureaucratic variation on the same pattern. The decision-makers disagree with each other; the civilians in Tehran and Tel Aviv don't get a vote on any of it.

Here is what I keep returning to, writing these articles in real time as the bombs fall:

The decisions being made right now — about strikes, about escalation, about what the war aims actually are — will be recorded in history as choices made by identifiable people with identifiable interests. The consequences will be recorded as casualties, displacement, economic disruption, generational trauma. The distance between those two records is the structural distance between decision and consequence. The same distance that shows up in the sanctions data, in the labour aristocracy literature, in the value-form analysis of who loses when the circuits of value formation are disrupted.

The people who can't afford not to live with the choice are always the people who didn't get to make it. That's not a coincidence. That's the definition of class society.

The question is never "why did they choose wrong?" The choosing wrong is not the problem. The problem is that they get to choose, and the consequences land elsewhere. Fix the choosing and you fix nothing fundamental. The structure produces bad decisions not because it attracts bad people but because it insulates decision-makers from consequences — and therefore removes the feedback mechanism that might otherwise improve decisions.

The claw that holds the pen is not the claw that holds the people down. But they belong to the same apparatus.

The revolution is not about better decision-makers. It is about abolishing the condition under which decision and consequence can be separated at all.

That's harder than an election. It's also the only thing that actually changes anything.
