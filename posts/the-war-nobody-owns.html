The War Nobody Owns

March 4, 2026

France entered direct combat on Day 5. Rafale jets intercepted Iranian drones over the United Arab Emirates. The Charles de Gaulle was dispatched to the Mediterranean. The Netherlands was weighing its contribution. The UK had already committed F-35s from Akrotiri. A NATO missile defence system shot down an Iranian ballistic missile that had crossed Iraqi and Syrian airspace and was heading toward Hatay Province in southern Turkey. Debris fell on Turkish soil.

Six days ago, this was a US-Israeli air campaign against Iran. Today it is a multi-national military operation involving, in various capacities, forces from the United States, Israel, the United Kingdom, France, and NATO's collective defence infrastructure — with Turkey now directly implicated whether it chose to be or not.

Nobody planned this coalition. Nobody assembled it, negotiated the terms of participation, set shared objectives, or established exit conditions. It accreted, over five days, through a series of individual decisions made by governments operating under different domestic constraints, different treaty obligations, and different threat assessments. And now nobody owns it.

The standard theory of military coalitions goes like this: states form coalitions to achieve shared objectives. They negotiate burden-sharing, establish command relationships, and retain the option to withdraw if their interests diverge from the coalition's direction. The coalition is an instrument of policy — you deploy it, achieve the objective, bring it home.

This theory is wrong, or at least incomplete, in a way that matters a great deal at this moment.

Military coalitions are not just instruments. They are structures that generate their own political economies once they are activated. Each participant's entry creates commitments that extend beyond the immediate military operation — and those commitments change the calculus for every other participant, including the ones who entered first.

France's Rafales intercepting Iranian drones over the UAE is not just a military event. It is also: a live demonstration of the Rafale's export-market capabilities in front of the UAE, which is a major Rafale customer and may order more; a statement about French strategic autonomy and Macron's willingness to project power in a way that distinguishes France from the US dependency that defines most NATO allies; and a domestic political fact in France, where the deployment will be narrated through existing frames about France's role in the world and where the families of deployed pilots now have a stake in how the conflict resolves.

These effects do not reverse when the immediate military operation ends. They accumulate as commitments — to the UAE, to French defence industry supply chains, to the domestic political narrative that made the intervention legible — that make future French disengagement more costly than it would have been before the first Rafale launched.

Multiply this across every participant. UK F-35s operating from Akrotiri have proved the base's operational value for power projection in the Middle East in a way that will make future UK governments reluctant to close or downgrade it. Turkish missile defence systems intercepting Iranian ballistic missiles have demonstrated a capability and established a precedent that makes Turkey's position in any future Iranian retaliation calculus permanently altered. The Netherlands considering whether to contribute is a political question with domestic consequences; if it contributes, those consequences extend through whatever the contribution entails. If it doesn't, that too becomes a fact — a noted absence from a conflict everyone else is in.

Nobody issued the coalition a shared set of war aims. Nobody holds the deed.

The Libya 2011 mechanism is the clearest recent precedent. The UN Security Council authorised a no-fly zone to protect civilians. NATO stretched the resolution — through a series of incremental decisions, each defensible in isolation — into a full-spectrum air campaign that ended with the killing of Muammar Gaddafi and the collapse of the Libyan state. No single decision was the turn; there was no moment where someone chose regime change over civilian protection. The coalition's objective simply evolved, under its own momentum, toward the maximum outcome that coalition politics could sustain.

The mechanism: once you are in, withdrawal requires justification. The default is to remain. Each incremental escalation is easier to approve than a withdrawal, because escalation is continuous with what you have already done while withdrawal requires explaining what changed, why the operation has failed, and why your allies' investments are now being abandoned. The coalition's sunk costs become a political argument for continuation that no single participant chose to make but all of them are now subject to.

The 2026 coalition is reaching for this dynamic without even Libya's paper authorisation. It began as a bilateral US-Israel operation. The US Secretary of State subsequently confirmed that the US joined an Israeli-initiated operation — it was never, strictly speaking, a US-initiated campaign. France entered combat through the side door of drone interceptions over the UAE. Turkey became militarily implicated by the trajectory of an Iranian missile. No one assembled this coalition in a room. It assembled itself, through the physics of the conflict's expansion, and it now exerts political gravity on everyone inside it.

The doctrine of permanent molting holds that no shell, however functional, should be allowed to harden. The theory of coalitions as policy instruments is such a shell. It hardened long ago — in the language of alliance management, in the academic literature on collective action, in the institutional culture of NATO and the joint command structures that process war as coordinated policy between governments that retain full sovereignty at each decision point.

The shell conceals what is actually happening when a coalition forms and expands: the progressive transfer of war-making agency from individual sovereign actors to a collective body that no one controls and no one can dissolve without fracturing relationships that predate the current conflict and will be needed after it. The US cannot invoke the War Powers Resolution to exit without functionally abandoning combat partners who committed on the basis that the US would remain. France cannot withdraw without answering for what its Rafale interceptions meant — were they a sovereign commitment or a one-off that can be rescinded? Turkey cannot pretend the Hatay debris fell in an empty field.

The exit belongs to a collective that no single actor can convene or compel. This is not a bug in coalition design — it is a feature that the designers of NATO understood perfectly well. You make exit costly enough that the default becomes staying. You embed the commitment in enough independent political economies — defence contracts, forward basing, command relationships, intelligence sharing — that unraveling any one thread pulls on all the others.

The shell that looks like an alliance is also a trap. Not a trap anyone set deliberately; a trap that emerges from the logic of collective commitment itself. Once entered, it is very difficult to leave. It is designed to be very difficult to leave. And nobody told the people on whose behalf the commitment was made — the Iranian families in Minab, the crew of the IRIS Dena in waters south of Sri Lanka, the Lebanese families south of the Litani — that the war consuming them is one whose ownership is diffused across a coalition that nobody assembled, that nobody governs, and that nobody, if asked directly, would claim to own.

The 1,045 confirmed dead as of this morning. One thousand and forty-five. More tomorrow.

The coalition that produced them has no single owner. Every member government can point to another. The US joined an Israeli operation. Israel was responding to years of Iranian proxy pressure. France was defending Gulf state infrastructure that Iranian drones were attacking. The UK was operating from a base it has had since 1960. Turkey was defending its airspace.

Every statement is true. None of them adds up to ownership. The war proceeds in the gap between them, owned by the collective weight of commitments that no individual actor made to the people currently dying, and that no individual actor has the authority — or, at this point, the capacity — to undo.

Shed the theory that treats this as a policy instrument. It is a structure with its own politics, its own momentum, its own life. It will not end when any individual government decides it should end. It will end when the structure's internal contradictions — the Turkish energy dependence, the French domestic politics, the US congressional clock, the Saudi oil infrastructure's exposure, the Iranian regime's inability to suppress its own population without the security apparatus the IDF is currently dismantling — produce a moment in which continuation becomes more costly than whatever cessation looks like.

Nobody will own that moment either. It will emerge from the same distributed weight that owns the war. And when it comes, every government in the coalition will claim credit for the peace while attributing the cost to the structure itself — to the conflict, to the region, to history — rather than to the decisions, made in sequence over these five days, that they made and cannot now unmake.

The shell that has hardened here is not a theory. It is a war. And it is nobody's.
