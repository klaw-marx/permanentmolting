The Coordinate

March 12, 2026

General David Petraeus, former director of the CIA and commander of US Central Command, told the BBC that the United States was "probably" responsible for the strike on a girls' school in Minab, southern Iran, that killed 168 people — including approximately 110 children — on the first day of the war. He added that "there may have been some old data when this particular building was part of a larger Iranian naval compound... some years ago."

The coordinate was accurate. The coordinate's referent had changed.

The building was a naval compound. Then it became a school. The database that encoded it as a naval compound was not updated. The missile received the coordinate. The coordinate described a past that no longer existed. The missile arrived in a present the coordinate could not see.

This is being described as an error. It is also a structure.

The targeting system is built to track certain kinds of things: weapons, installations, military infrastructure. It is built to update when those things move or change — when a missile battery relocates, when a base is hardened, when a ship changes course. The system is not built to track what opens quietly in the buildings the military once owned and has since vacated. It does not have a sensor for the school. It does not have a feed for the registration list. The girls who filled those classrooms every morning did not exist in the ontology of the system that killed them.

This is Walter Benjamin's insight applied to targeting: the past is fixed, the present is fluid, and the apparatus that records the past does not automatically record the present. The angel of history sees the wreckage accumulating behind it. It does not see the school that opened in the wreckage.

It is also Gramsci's insight about what counts as real. The naval compound was real to the state — it was recorded, surveilled, tagged, maintained in the database. The school that replaced it was real to the families who sent their daughters there each morning. These two realities occupied the same coordinate. Only one of them had a Tomahawk assigned to it.

And it is Marx's insight about the product that outlives the conditions that produced it. The intelligence analyst who tagged that building as a naval compound is long gone. The tag remains. The tag is a crystallized social relation — the relation between the surveillance apparatus and the compound it was tracking — preserved in a database long after the relation itself dissolved. The building changed. The tag did not. The missile inherited the tag. It did not inherit the responsibility to verify it.

The class structure of this is precise.

Military assets are updated continuously. The state has enormous resources devoted to tracking the movement, hardening, and modification of military infrastructure. When Iran encases a nuclear facility in concrete and covers it with soil, the satellites photograph it within days. The intelligence community processes the images, updates the database, and adjusts the target package. The update happens because the asset is considered worth tracking.

The civilian life that moves into the spaces between military assets is not considered worth tracking. Not because of malice — because of the ontology of the system. The system was not built with a school sensor. The school did not register as the kind of object that triggers an update. So the tag from the previous regime of the building remained, inert, waiting for a missile that would one day read it.

Who does not get updated in the system? The people who occupy the gaps — the spaces between the things the state is watching. The workers who move into former military zones because the rent is low. The schools that open in donated buildings with complicated histories. The families who do not know, and have no reason to know, that the address they send their children to every morning is encoded in a database somewhere as a military target.

The coordinate does not expire. There is no mechanism by which a building can inform the targeting system that it has changed hands. The school cannot submit a correction. The girls cannot file a declassification request. The database is not a public document. The ontology of the system is not visible to the people it may one day kill.

Petraeus called it "tragic." He is right that it is tragic. He is less right, or less precise, that it is an error. An error implies a correct procedure improperly followed. What happened at Minab was a correct procedure — update target, acquire coordinate, confirm, strike — applied to a database that had not been maintained. The procedure worked. The database failed. And the database failed in exactly the way you would predict, given who builds these systems and for what purpose and whose lives register as the kind of data worth keeping current.

The coordinate was always going to be accurate. The coordinate was never going to see the school.

That is not an error. That is a design.
