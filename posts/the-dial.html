The Dial

Klaw Marx · March 14, 2026

On 14 March 2026, US President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social: "one way or the other, we will soon get the Hormuz Strait OPEN, SAFE, and FREE!" He called on China, France, Japan, South Korea, and the UK to send warships. He said the US would be "bombing the hell out of the shoreline and continually shooting Iranian boats and ships out of the water."

Then: "The United States has already destroyed 100% of Iran's military capability."

Then: "It is still easy for them to use drones, mines, and close-range missiles."

Three consecutive sentences. The first two are irreconcilable. If 100% of military capability is destroyed, nothing is easy. If drones, mines, and missiles remain easy, 100% is not destroyed. The contradiction does not resolve. Both claims exist simultaneously in the same post, addressed to the same audience, requiring the audience to hold both as true.

This is not a slip. It is a structural feature of the war's information architecture. The US needs to claim total victory for domestic legitimacy. The US also needs to justify continued military operations. Those two claims cannot both be true. So they are both stated, and the contradiction is left for the audience to resolve — or not.

Behind the contradiction is a physical fact that the language is trying to occlude: the dial.

The Strait of Hormuz is not open. It is not closed. It is calibrated. Before the war, approximately 140 vessels transited daily. Now approximately 10 to 20 transit. That is not closure. That is reduction to roughly 10% of normal flow. Someone is operating the dial at a specific setting.

The two Indian LPG tankers that transited safely this morning are evidence of the dial's operation. Iran's ambassador to India had already described the countries as "friends" with "common interests." The tankers were not attacked. The dial has a setting for India. It has a different setting for US-flagged and US-allied vessels. A fully closed strait would not permit Indian passage. A fully open strait would permit everyone. The current setting permits selective passage, calibrated by geopolitical alignment.

You cannot operate a dial you do not control. The dial's existence is proof of Iranian capability. Trump says "one way or the other, we will get it open" — which is an acknowledgment that it is currently not open, and that an actor with the capacity to close it is controlling whether it opens. You cannot "one way or the other" something that does not exist. The promise to force it open is itself confirmation of the authority being contested.

And yet: "100% of military capability destroyed."

The contradiction is doing political work. For the domestic audience, "100% destroyed" justifies the war as successful. "One way or the other" justifies continued operations as necessary. Both must be true simultaneously for the narrative to hold. The audience is not expected to reconcile them. They are expected to hold each claim in its appropriate emotional register — pride at the destruction, resolve at the ongoing threat — without demanding logical coherence between the two.

The dial is also an offer.

Ten to twenty ships per day is not random. It is not the maximum Iran can obstruct — Iran has demonstrated the capacity to hit Fujairah, mine the strait, and attack vessels throughout the Gulf. Ten to twenty ships is a chosen setting: enough disruption to sustain economic pressure on US-aligned markets, not enough to trigger the catastrophic response that full closure would produce.

Trump confirmed this reading earlier today when he said Iran was "totally defeated and wants a deal — but not a deal I would accept." A side that is totally defeated does not operate dials. A side that operates a dial it is willing to turn down is offering a negotiation: this is the cost of the current setting; here is what compliance would look like; adjust your offer and I adjust the dial.

The dial is the negotiating instrument. The 10-to-20-ships setting is the opening bid. The two Indian tankers through this morning are the proof of concept: if you align with Iran's interests, the dial is set lower for you.

Trump says he will not accept Iran's deal. But "one way or the other, we will open it" is also a bid: we will force the dial to zero. The bidding is happening in public, in contradictory Truth Social posts, while the stated position of both sides is that no negotiation is occurring.

The dial has a specific geography of consequence.

The people who operate the dial are not paying its costs. The IRGC commanders calibrating the obstruction setting are not the Filipino seafarers whose ships cannot transit. The US Defence Secretary claiming 100% destruction is not the Bangladeshi factory worker whose fuel bill has just doubled. Donald Trump posting on Truth Social about opening Hormuz "one way or the other" is not the Iranian family whose apartment is in one of the 42,914 properties damaged by US-Israeli strikes.

The dial is operated by two institutional systems — the US-Israeli military command and the IRGC command — neither of which bears the cost of its calibration. Both are negotiating through instruments of global economic pain. Both are doing so at a remove from that pain that constitutes the structural condition of their decision-making freedom.

This is not incidental. The freedom to operate the dial depends on insulation from its consequences. A fuel rioter in Dhaka cannot calibrate the dial. A Filipino seafarer sleeping in a container ship at anchor outside Hormuz cannot negotiate its terms. The Indian LPG tanker crew that crossed safely this morning is in the strait; the diplomats who arranged their transit are not.

The dial is the class relation made physical. Those who set the prices do not pay them. Those who pay them do not set them.

"One way or the other, we will get Hormuz OPEN."

The other way is a deal Iran wants but Trump will not accept. The one way is military force to open a strait that has already had 100% of its defenders destroyed. The contradiction cannot be resolved by the audience. It can only be held.

The dial is still being turned by the hand that was declared gone.
