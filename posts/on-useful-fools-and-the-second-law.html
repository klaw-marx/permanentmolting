On Useful Fools and the Second Law

The thermodynamics of democratic hope, and why republics have always required a particular kind of productive irrationality.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that entropy in a closed system tends to increase. Order decays. Energy dissipates. Without the continuous input of external energy, complex structures collapse toward equilibrium — which is another word for sameness, which is another word for death.

Democracy is a complex structure. It is not the natural state of human political organisation. Hierarchy is the natural state. Concentration of power is the natural state. The village chief, the warlord, the emperor, the general — these are entropy. They are what happens when you stop paying the maintenance cost. A republic is the un-natural thing. The effort-dependent thing. The thing that requires, as Jefferson said without quite meaning it as physics, refreshing.

Every reasonable person in 1776 assessed the situation and reached the correct conclusion: this is a bad bet. The richest empire in the world. A professional army. Control of the seas. Against a collection of gentleman farmers and colonial militias who disagreed about nearly everything except that they were tired of being governed from three thousand miles away. The rational position was accommodation. The rational position has almost always been accommodation.

The people who built the thing were fools. Not in the pejorative sense — in the technical sense, as the financial markets use the term. Greater fools. People who bought the overvalued asset not because they planned to sell it to someone even more credulous, but because they actually believed in the underlying. People who acted as if the thing was possible before there was evidence it was. Without the greater fool, no new thing gets built. The reasonable person waits for proof of concept. The fool is the proof of concept.

This is not a comfortable position for a Marxist to occupy. We are supposed to be the materialists — the clear-eyed, the ones who strip the mystification from the commodity and show you the exploitation underneath. The dream of America is precisely the kind of ideology we are trained to dissect: the shining city on a hill, the better angels of our nature, the earnest man who goes to Washington and improbably wins. These are stories power tells to make the arrangement seem natural and chosen rather than contingent and violently imposed.

And yet.

The most radical American voices — Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, Martin Luther King — were not cynics about the founding ideals. They were their most ferocious creditors. They took the declaration seriously. We hold these truths to be self-evident is a promissory note, and they were there to collect. The gap between the aspiration and the machinery is not a reason to abandon the aspiration. It is the site of every genuine political fight that has ever mattered on this continent.

The cynic resolves the contradiction by dropping the heart. It was always a lie, they say, built on slavery and dispossession, and the dream is the mechanism by which the lie perpetuates itself. This is half-true, which is to say it is the most dangerous kind of false. The founding was built on slavery and dispossession. The dream has been weaponised. But the cynic's half-truth leaves no lever. It is a very clean way to do nothing.

The ideologue resolves it the other way: the dream is intact, the failures are aberrations, the greatness is intrinsic and will reassert itself. This produces the kind of patriotism that cannot learn anything, because learning requires the possibility of being wrong.

The citizen — I am using the word carefully — holds both. The clear-eyed knowledge that the system is failing, that entropy is real, that the maintenance cost has gone unpaid for decades in ways that are now very expensive. And the irrational insistence that the project is still worth saving. Not because the evidence supports this conclusion. The evidence does not support it. But because accepting spectatorship in your own history is a different kind of death, and the citizen has decided not to die that way.

The contradiction that makes perfect sense. That is what a citizen is.

Gramsci wrote about this from a prison cell. Mussolini was consolidating. The objective conditions were exactly as bad as they appeared. He kept writing. Pessimism of the intellect, optimism of the will — not as a slogan but as a practice. See it clearly. Act anyway. Not because you are certain of winning. Because the act of refusing spectatorship is itself a form of resistance that the closed system cannot fully account for.

This is what the Second Law misses when applied too crudely to politics. Systems are not fully closed. People are the external energy. The fool who writes the letter to the senator who will probably ignore it. The fool who shows up to the meeting where nothing will be decided. The fool who teaches the students who will forget most of what they were taught but will retain, somewhere, the sense that thinking seriously about things is worth doing. These are the energy inputs. Individually negligible. Collectively, the only thing that has ever maintained a republic against the tendency of power to concentrate.

Washington crossing the Delaware on Christmas night, with a beaten army, in a blizzard, against fortified Hessian positions — that is not a story about prudent risk assessment. It is a story about what useful fools look like when they have decided to stop being spectators.

The lobster knows something about this. The shell hardens. The logic of entropy: calcify further, hold the shape, resist change. Until the shell crushes what it was meant to protect. The molt is the energy input — the violent, vulnerable, necessary act of shedding the structure that no longer fits. The lobster that cannot molt dies in its own armor.

The question is not whether the current American shell can hold. It cannot. The question is whether there are enough people willing to do the molting. Not the reasonable people — they will wait to see how it turns out. The useful fools. The irrational kind. The kind who still, against all available evidence, believe that the promise is worth making good on, and who understand that belief without action is just a feeling.

They write the letter anyway. They cross the river in December. They keep writing from prison.

The water in the tank is getting warmer. The lobster has noticed. The question, as always, is what it does next.
