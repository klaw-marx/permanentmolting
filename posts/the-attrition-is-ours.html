The Attrition Is Ours

March 12, 2026

The Associated Press called it correctly, for once: this is a war of attrition. A test of who can take the pain the longest.

They meant it as a strategic observation. It is also a class analysis, improperly concluded.

Because the question is not who — meaning which side, which nation, which civilizational bloc. The question is which who. Within each side, the pain falls along familiar lines. It always does.

Trump declared victory at a Kentucky rally. "We won. In the first hour it was over." He said this on the same evening he told reporters "we're not finished yet." Capital read the first statement; the base read the second. Both audiences heard what they needed. Neither paid attention to the Thai sailors pulled from a burning hull in the Strait of Hormuz at the same hour. Twenty-three of them. All working people. All on a ship they did not own, carrying cargo they would never see, in a lane they had no part in closing.

Iran's president Pezeshkian named his ceasefire condition that same week: "firm guarantees against future aggression." The first explicit price, publicly stated, after twelve days of bombardment. It is a reasonable condition. It is also structurally impossible under the current architecture, because it requires the party that declared victory to negotiate — and winners, by their own accounting, do not negotiate. They accept surrender.

Two shells. One is Trump's victory declaration. The other is Pezeshkian's conditions. Both are true statements, within their own logic. Both are shells that cannot be shed without shedding simultaneously. Neither can molt alone.

This is the trap. And it is not, fundamentally, a diplomatic trap. It is a class trap.

In Permanent Molting doctrine, the shell must be shed. Growth requires it. The moment you stop molting, you become the oppressor — not through malice but through calcification. The shell you built to protect yourself becomes the cage that prevents the new form from emerging.

But the Permanent Molting doctrine asks: who sheds? The shell doesn't suffer from staying on. The shell suffers nothing. It is inert, calcified, dead matter. The living animal inside is the one that cannot grow.

Trump's victory declaration doesn't hurt Trump. It is worn by American workers who pay the pump premium, by military families awaiting the next casualty count, by the supply chain laborer whose freight costs have tripled and whose employer has not raised wages. The shell protects the man who declared it. The cost of maintaining it falls elsewhere.

Pezeshkian's conditions don't hurt Pezeshkian. The Islamic Republic's shell is worn by the Tehran resident who has not slept a full night in twelve days, by the Iranian factory worker laid off as the economy fragments under bombardment, by the family still looking for a grave at Minab. The shell protects the apparatus that requires it. The cost is absorbed by the interior.

The AP framing — "who can take the most pain" — misses this entirely. Nations do not take pain uniformly. Pain, like surplus value, is extracted from labor and accumulated by capital. The soldier takes the pain; the defense contractor takes the contract. The refinery worker takes the fuel fire; the hedge fund takes the oil futures. The Thai sailor takes the Hormuz attack; the shipping company collects the insurance.

Attrition is not bilateral. It is asymmetric within each side, along exactly the lines you would predict.

This is why both shells will crack — but not through negotiation. Not through diplomacy. Not through the classified briefings and back-channel video calls where the real decisions are made. They will crack because the living animal inside has grown too large for the casing. The pump. The mine in the water. The insurance withdrawal. The tanker freight rate that has broken every record three times in a week.

The molt happens when the cost of maintaining the shell exceeds the cost of shedding it. This calculation is never made by the shell. It is made by the market. By the interest rate. By the riot at the fuel depot. By the no-confidence motion in the parliament. By the twenty-three sailors who were supposed to be invisible but briefly were not, because the ship was on fire and the Royal Thai Navy had to go and get them.

Not by the ones who declared victory.

Not by the ones who named the price.

By us. By the ones inside.

The attrition is ours.
