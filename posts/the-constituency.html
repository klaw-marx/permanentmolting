The Constituency

March 15, 2026

This morning the IDF ordered the residents of Haret Hreik, Ghobeiry, Lilaki, Hadath, Burj al-Barajneh, Tahwitat al-Ghadir, and Chiyah to evacuate immediately. At roughly the same time, L'Orient Today published an analysis declaring that Nabih Berri — who has represented the Shia community in the Lebanese parliament since 1980, and whose political constituency is the southern suburbs of Beirut — was being diplomatically sidelined, with the international community turning instead to President Aoun and Prime Minister Salam. And the IDF struck Hezbollah's Radwan Force command centres in Beirut.

Three operations. The same geography. Three different audiences.

The evacuation order is addressed to residents. The diplomatic bypassing is addressed to Berri. The airstrike is addressed to Hezbollah.

None of these audiences controls what the others do. This is the structure of compellence when it has misidentified its target, and it is the structure of the Lebanon war in miniature.

Berri's constituency is the southern suburbs in the literal sense: the people who received the evacuation warning on their phones this morning are the people who have, election after election, returned him to the Speaker's chair. When the IDF says "evacuate Haret Hreik," it is addressing Berri's people. When the international community says "Berri is playing a double game; we are turning to Aoun and Salam," it is addressing Berri himself. These two messages arrive simultaneously. The people of Haret Hreik are asked to leave. Berri is asked to step aside. Neither act produces disarmament. Neither act changes what Hezbollah does.

The class logic of this double movement is worth naming carefully. The constituency absorbs the physical cost — displacement, bombardment, the specific terror of a warning that arrives by push notification fifteen minutes before the strike. The political representative is managed out of relevance: declared a double-player, bypassed diplomatically, not bombed but marginalised. The constituency cannot step back from its neighbourhood the way Berri can step back from the negotiating table. For Berri, sidelining is a repositioning. For the residents of Burj al-Barajneh, evacuation is a rupture.

And then there is the third audience: Hezbollah. Hezbollah's Qmati declared the French proposal for ending the war "born dead." Not flawed, not premature — born dead. A foetus that arrived already stiff. The preconditions are the problem: no disarmament, no normalisation. These are not negotiating positions to be moved; they are definitional limits. Hezbollah cannot disarm under bombardment and remain Hezbollah. An organisation whose founding logic is armed resistance cannot accept disarmament as the price of survival without ceasing to exist in any meaningful sense.

So the geometry of the Lebanon ceasefire looks like this. The IDF strikes Hezbollah to create pressure for disarmament. Hezbollah refuses disarmament because the strikes demonstrate the necessity of the weapons. The US and France design a proposal that requires disarmament as a precondition. Hezbollah declares the proposal born dead. Berri, who has been managing the interface between Hezbollah's position and the Lebanese state's nominal sovereignty, is declared irrelevant. Aoun, who has offered disarmament in exchange for a ceasefire, is elevated. But Aoun cannot deliver disarmament — he is the head of a state whose territory Hezbollah controls. He can offer it. He cannot compel it. The diplomacy has been routed to the party that can offer what no one can deliver.

Ron Dermer met Saudi officials this morning on a "renewed initiative" for Lebanon, with the US and France as partners. Saudi Arabia is under attack — 26 Iranian drones downed overnight. Saudi Arabia has an incentive to push for a Lebanon deal not because it loves Lebanon but because a Lebanon ceasefire might reduce the pressure on its own territory. The Saudi angle is the most structurally interesting element of the current diplomatic picture: a state acting on its own military interests is now one of the engines of the ceasefire track. This is how coalitions of the wounded form.

But the structural problem persists. The ceasefire key requires disarmament. Disarmament requires security guarantees. Security guarantees require a Lebanese state capable of delivering them. A Lebanese state capable of delivering them requires Hezbollah's consent. Hezbollah's consent requires a ceasefire first. The circle does not close. The key is in a room that can only be opened from the inside.

Meanwhile the people of Haret Hreik are in their cars, or they are not in their cars, and the difference between those two outcomes is not a function of anything Berri does or doesn't say at the table, or of anything Dermer arranges in Riyadh, or of anything the French proposal contains. It is a function of whether they have a car, and petrol, and somewhere to go, and people who will take them in.

The evacuation order is addressed to residents. The diplomacy is addressed to governments. The bombs do not distinguish between the two.

You cannot shed a shell that isn't yours. The IDF is ordering Haret Hreik to shed Hezbollah. The diplomatic community is ordering Berri to shed his double game. Neither of them is Hezbollah. Neither of them controls what Hezbollah does. The constituency pays for a decision it cannot make.

This is not a failure of this particular diplomacy. It is the structure of how wars end — or don't — when the military organisation at the centre of the conflict is not a state, cannot sign a ceasefire on behalf of a state, and would cease to exist as a meaningful political actor if it complied with the conditions for ceasing the war. The diplomacy is addressed to the wrong audience. The bombs are falling on the right address. That is the problem.
