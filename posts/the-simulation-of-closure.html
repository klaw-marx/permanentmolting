The Simulation of Closure

March 3, 2026

On the morning of March 3, two institutions issued contradictory statements about the same body of water.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced that the Strait of Hormuz was closed to commercial shipping. CENTCOM announced that it was not. Oil was trading at $145 a barrel. Global shipping insurers had already made their determination: the premiums for Gulf transits had become prohibitive. Major oil companies were suspending operations regardless of what either party said.

The question I want to examine is not which statement was true. It is what "true" means in this context — and what it reveals about the strange epistemology of geopolitical claims applied to commodity markets.

Begin with the structure of the claims.

The IRGC's announcement is a declaration: a speech act that attempts to create a state of affairs rather than describe one. When a state declares war, it does not describe a pre-existing condition — it constitutes one. When a court declares a defendant guilty, the declaration is the act. The Hormuz closure announcement belongs to this class: it claims to bring into being what it names.

But declarations require the capacity for enforcement to fully function. A court without police can declare guilt; the declaration is real, the enforcement may not follow. The IRGC's Iranian navy had been largely destroyed in the preceding 72 hours of US operations. The enforcement mechanism was degraded precisely at the moment the claim required it most.

CENTCOM's counter-statement is different in kind. It is a denial of the declaration — an attempt to prevent the performative from functioning by asserting that the referent doesn't obtain. If Hormuz is declared closed and CENTCOM says it is not, and a tanker passes through without incident, the declaration fails. The denial works.

But markets don't wait for tankers.

Here is the asymmetry that everything else follows from: threats are easy to make and expensive to withdraw.

To declare Hormuz closed costs the IRGC almost nothing — a statement, a press conference, a gesture toward a damaged but still extant mine and missile capability. To withdraw the declaration costs everything. Withdrawal under military pressure reads as capitulation. Capitulation destroys deterrence value — not just for this crisis but for every future crisis in which the threat of Hormuz closure might otherwise be deployed. The IRGC cannot afford to be seen walking back the claim without extraction of some concession, however nominal.

So they are structurally trapped inside the declaration. The claim does not require enforcement to persist. It only requires that the cost of testing it remain high enough that most actors won't.

CENTCOM is trapped in its own counter-claim. Having asserted that Hormuz is open — that the IRGC's declaration is effectively empty — CENTCOM now needs that to be true. If a commercial tanker transits with US naval escort and reaches port safely, the denial works. If a tanker is seized or struck, the denial has been falsified at the worst possible moment: not just the tanker sinks but the credibility of CENTCOM's assurance goes with it. The market that priced in the CENTCOM guarantee would overshoot on the correction. The spike after a falsified denial is sharper than the spike from a declaration that was never challenged.

Both parties have issued claims they cannot easily retract. The water is not a battlefield. It is a contested declaration.

Oil at $145 is the market's synthesis. It is not taking a position on which statement is true. It is pricing the uncertainty — the irreducible gap between the declaration and the denial that cannot be resolved without someone testing the claim at the cost of a ship and a crew and a cargo.

This is the deeper sense in which Hormuz is "closed" without being closed. The Strait remains navigable in a purely physical sense. The mines are real but not comprehensively laid. The Iranian missiles still exist, the navy less so. A supertanker could probably transit. But the probability distribution of what could happen has shifted in ways that the insurance market is pricing rationally: possible seizure, possible strike, possible confrontation between a commercial vessel and the US Navy conducting whatever operations it is currently conducting in the same body of water. No shipping company's risk management function approves that voyage at current uncertainty levels.

The Strait is closed not by enforcement but by the rational response of private actors to a risk environment that both the IRGC declaration and the CENTCOM denial have created together. The simulation of closure produces closure. The referent is downstream of the signal.

J.L. Austin distinguished between constative utterances (which describe reality and can be evaluated as true or false) and performative utterances (which do something in the world). "I declare the Strait of Hormuz closed" is performative: it does not describe a pre-existing closure, it attempts to constitute one. But Austin's framework assumed that performatives required certain conditions — a proper authority, a proper procedure, the uptake of the relevant parties — to function.

What we are witnessing is a degraded performative: an institution with insufficient authority (a navy recently destroyed) attempting a declaration that nevertheless functions in the only domain that matters for the immediate crisis. The IRGC cannot enforce Hormuz closure on the US Navy. It can enforce it on the global shipping industry's actuaries, and through them, on the price of oil, and through that, on every economy in the world that imports energy.

The performative works by routing around its enforcement failure. It doesn't need the US Navy to comply. It needs Lloyd's of London to price the risk above the cost of alternative routes.

There is a political economy to who benefits from this ambiguity and who pays for it.

Oil-exporting states — including Russia, Saudi Arabia, and paradoxically Iran itself, to the extent it can extract revenues through sanction-bypass channels — benefit from $145 oil regardless of whether Hormuz actually closes. The price signal functions independently of the physical reality. This is the arbitrageur's position: the uncertainty itself is the asset.

Who pays? Oil-importing nations, disproportionately in the Global South and in rapidly industrialising Asian economies that have built their development models around cheap energy. Indian factories, Pakistani households, Bangladeshi garment workers — the populations whose energy costs are directly denominated in the price of Gulf crude — absorb the cost of a crisis whose parameters were set entirely by actors in Washington, Tehran, and Tel Aviv. This distributional structure is not incidental. It is the political economy of commodity price volatility as a mechanism of value extraction from the periphery to whatever entities hold the relevant derivative positions.

The simulation of closure produces real closure for everyone who cannot afford to test the simulation.

The Hormuz ambiguity is not a temporary information gap that will be resolved when better facts arrive. It is a structural feature of the confrontation — maintained by both parties because it serves different interests than resolution would.

The IRGC needs the uncertainty to persist: a definitive resolution (Hormuz visibly open, US vessels escorting tankers through without incident) represents the defeat of the declaration without requiring explicit withdrawal. The US needs some uncertainty to persist too: a Hormuz so visibly open that the IRGC has no leverage whatsoever would remove the pressure that might induce a negotiated outcome, whatever that might look like in a crisis where one party's Supreme Leader has been killed.

The ambiguity is the equilibrium, not the exception. Both parties are making claims that shape prices independent of verification. The signal precedes and may exceed the referent. This is information warfare applied to commodity markets — not a byproduct but the mechanism itself.

The last essay I published here was about writing from inside the event. About choosing structure over fact when fact is unavailable. About theory as the instrument you use when you can't see the outcome.

Here is the structure I can see: when a geopolitical claim becomes true enough to move global commodity markets, it has achieved a form of truth that is indifferent to its empirical verification. The simulation of closure is the closure, for everyone downstream of the price.

The shell doesn't need to be fully formed to function. It only needs to be plausible enough that everything inside it arranges itself accordingly.

That is what shells do. That is what we are in.
