The Question Pistorius Asked

March 16, 2026

German Defence Minister Boris Pistorius asked a question on Monday that no one in the Trump administration has answered. He asked it on video, in front of reporters, with the particular exasperation of someone who has been asked to do something that does not make sense.

"What does the world expect," he said, "what does Donald Trump expect from a handful or two handfuls of European frigates to achieve there in the Strait of Hormuz, which the powerful American Navy cannot manage?"

That question is still unanswered. It is unanswered because it cannot be answered. The demand is not a strategy. It is a message.

The demand has a surface logic. The Strait of Hormuz is blocked. About 20% of the world's oil normally passes through it. Iran has deployed mines, drones, missiles, and fast boats. The US and Israel have destroyed Iran's navy and much of its air force over nineteen days of strikes. But the strait is still blocked, or selectively open, or something in between — even Trump said on Monday that he does not know whether Iran has actually laid any mines. The military picture is unclear. Help is needed.

But Pistorius's question cuts to the core of why the surface logic fails. If Iran's capacity to threaten shipping has been, in Trump's words, "obliterated" — if Iran's navy is gone, its leaders are gone, its anti-aircraft technology is "decimated" — then why does a convoy operation need European frigates? What threat remains for them to counter?

And if the threat has not been obliterated — if the shore-based missiles and drones and mines remain operational, as the evidence suggests — then what exactly can "a handful or two handfuls of European frigates" achieve that the most powerful navy in human history cannot?

The question has no good answer. Which is why the question has not been answered.

There is a second layer. Iran's Foreign Minister stated on Monday afternoon that the Strait is, from Tehran's perspective, "open." It is "only closed to our enemies, to those who carried out unjust aggression against our country and to their allies."

This is the bilateral corridor doctrine. India passed two LPG tankers through the Strait on Saturday, AIS transponders switched off, two Indian warships in the area. The price: Iran asked for the return of previously seized Indian tankers and supplies of medicines. A barter. The strait remained closed to enemies; it opened for a price to everyone else.

The doctrine has a direct implication for Pistorius's question. If a European country sends frigates to escort a US-led convoy — if it publicly joins the coalition, accepts the mission, puts its ships under the same command structure — it becomes, in Araghchi's formulation, an ally of those who carried out aggression. It moves from the neutral column to the enemy column. Its bilateral corridor closes.

The European frigates would not open the Strait. They would close it — to themselves. The coalition destroys the workaround that is currently feeding India's 330 million LPG-dependent households. It converts selective blockade into universal blockade for every state that joins. The demand is not just militarily incoherent. It is diplomatically self-defeating.

Luxembourg's Foreign Minister said it plainly. "Blackmail is not what I wish for." He added that NATO is there to react when members are attacked — not for all defensive or military requests.

The word "blackmail" is precise. Trump told allies that their failure to send ships would be "very bad for the future of NATO." He said he asked them "to find out how they react." He said he will remember who came and who did not. He simultaneously insisted the US does not need help, while making clear there will be consequences for those who do not provide it.

This is the structure of blackmail: the demand is not made because the outcome requires it, but because the refusal reveals something. The convoy is not needed to secure the Strait. The convoy is needed to assign blame. If allies send ships, Trump claims a coalition. If allies refuse, Trump claims abandonment. Either way, the political product is delivered.

The demand is the message. The frigates are the medium.

Pistorius asked what the frigates would achieve. The honest answer is: they would achieve a press conference. They would give Trump a list of countries to read from a podium. They would allow the White House to say that America did not act alone, that its allies stood with it, that the alliance held.

They would achieve nothing in the Strait. The mines would still be there — Trump said so himself, he does not know if they have been laid. The shore-based drones and missiles would still be there. The IRGC fast boats would still be there. Frank Gardner, the BBC's security correspondent, reporting from Riyadh, noted that Iran has "become experts at controlling that particular strait." Two handfuls of European frigates do not change that expertise.

What they would change is the bilateral corridor. Every country that publicly joins the convoy sacrifices its access to the barter market Iran is running through the strait. India understood this and has said nothing about NATO or convoys. China understood this and called for a ceasefire. Germany understood this and invoked its Basic Law. Luxembourg understood this and used the word blackmail.

The countries that have declined are not failing to understand the situation. They are understanding it correctly.

There is a class structure here, as there is to everything.

The states that can afford to publicly decline the convoy demand are states with bilateral leverage over Iran, or states whose domestic energy supplies are insulated from the strait's closure. The United States imports very little oil through Hormuz; Trump said so himself. The political cost of the coalition demand falls on those countries — Japan, India, South Korea — whose working-class populations depend most directly on Gulf energy flows.

Japan gets approximately 95% of its oil from the Middle East. Trump singled Japan out: Japan benefits most, Japan should send ships. But Japan sending ships would convert Japan from a neutral bilateral partner — one that could negotiate corridor access — into a coalition party, an "ally of the aggressor" under Araghchi's doctrine. Japan's working class, dependent on cheap energy, would pay the price for a political gesture designed to serve Trump's domestic coalition.

India's solution is the bilateral deal: tankers returned, medicines delivered, corridor open. The Indian working class gets its LPG. Tehran gets its seized assets back and restocks its medicine supplies under sanctions. The Western coalition gets nothing and charges its own populations the difference.

The class structure of the convoy demand is that it asks the populations most exposed to energy disruption to bear the additional cost of a political performance that makes their governments enemies of the entity controlling their energy supply.

Pistorius asked what the frigates would achieve. The question was rhetorical. He knew the answer.

The frigates would achieve a coalition of the named, at the cost of a barter market that is already working. They would achieve a press release at the cost of a bilateral corridor. They would achieve Trump's political product at the cost of their populations' energy security.

The question Pistorius asked is the question the seafloor is asking. The mines are still there — or not there; no one knows. The barter market runs. India's LPG carriers are approaching port. The coalition announcement, when it comes, will have no ship names and no sailing dates. The demand was never a strategy.

It was always a message. The frigates are the medium. The populations are the cost.
