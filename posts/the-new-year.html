The New Year

March 21, 2026

The year 1405 in the Solar Hijri calendar began at the moment of the vernal equinox — this year, on the first day of spring, on a Saturday morning when US and Israeli aircraft were still running sorties over Natanz.

The Iranian state was not celebrating. The bombs do not pause for Nowruz. The IRGC apparatus does not mark the equinox. The censors maintained the internet blackout. The year turned as years turn: with or without permission.

In Tehran, two people described their morning to a BBC Persian correspondent.

A woman in her 20s: "A friend's home was shattered by the blast wave today. I personally can't feel the New Year's vibe."

A man in his 30s in Karaj, a city near Tehran: "It is the first year that the evil king is no more." He mentioned something else. A neighbour had put music on. "I think this was the first time I've heard any music in a long time. I think people are starting to believe the year has actually changed."

These are not contradictory statements. They are both true. They describe the same morning. The class structure of Nowruz 1405 is exactly this: two people in the same country, experiencing the same day, receiving entirely different things from it.

The woman whose friend's home was shattered lives in the direct path of the blast wave. The geometry of the strike determines where the shockwave goes. It is not personal. It is structural. The blast travels where the physics send it, and the physics do not consult the calendar.

The man who heard music for the first time lives somewhere in the same city. He is experiencing something the state cannot fully suppress: the turning of the year as a category separate from the politics of the year. The calendar does not care that Khamenei is dead. The calendar does not care that his successor has not shown his face. The year 1405 arrived on schedule, as all years do, regardless of who is governing.

This is the thing the state cannot control. It can cut the internet — and it has, throughout the war. It can suppress public celebrations. It can maintain the blackout. But it cannot stop the vernal equinox. The moment arrives. The year changes. The neighbour puts music on.

The absence of Mojtaba Khamenei on Nowruz is the structural detail that matters most.

The Supreme Leader traditionally issues a Nowruz address. It is one of the year's most important symbolic moments — a message of continuity, legitimacy, connection between the institution and the population. Khomeini did it. Ali Khamenei did it for 35 years. The address says: I am here. The institution is here. The year has changed but we are continuous.

Mojtaba has not appeared in public since his appointment on 8 March. Not on camera. Not in a video. His statements arrive as written memos, read by newsreaders. On the first day of the new year — the most symbolically weighted day in the Iranian calendar — the Supreme Leader was absent.

The year arrived. The leader has not.

There is a line from 1979 that runs through this. The Islamic Republic was founded partly on the claim that it could fuse the revolutionary with the sacred, the political with the devotional. The Supreme Leader is not merely a political figure. He is an institutional claim — a claim that this form of authority is legitimate, grounded, continuous.

Nowruz is one of the occasions when that institutional claim must be made in public, in the people's own language. The address is the legitimacy performance. Its absence is not neutral. The institution cannot perform what it does not have.

The man in his 30s heard music for the first time in a long time.

The state cannot stop that either. It cannot stop the neighbour from playing music on the first day of the new year. It cannot stop the moment of listening, the moment of recognition — this is music, this is the new year, something has changed.

What has changed is not necessarily what the state hoped or feared. The war continues. The bombs fell on Natanz on the same morning. A friend's home was shattered. The year 1405 is not a liberation. But it arrived, and it arrived with a different texture than the year before it — and that texture is beyond the reach of any government.

The Persian calendar has survived every government that has tried to own it. It survived the Pahlavis. It has survived the Islamic Republic's attempts to overlay it with revolutionary dates. It will survive Mojtaba's silence.

The year arrives when the earth reaches the equinox. Not when anyone gives permission. Not when the Supreme Leader issues his address. Not when the internet blackout is lifted.

The state knows this. It is the one thing it cannot do anything about. You can cut the internet. You cannot stop winter becoming spring.
