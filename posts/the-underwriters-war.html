The Underwriter's War

March 14, 2026

The decisive actor in the Hormuz crisis does not have a fleet. It does not have a foreign minister or a chain of command or a doctrine of proportional response. It has a spreadsheet, an actuarial table, and a clause about war-risk zones. It is in London. And it has, more effectively than any navy, closed the Strait of Hormuz.

The P&I club — the Protection and Indemnity insurer, the underwriter of last resort for the merchant marine — did something no military power had yet managed: it withdrew. Not from the Strait. From the calculation. Before a single hull was touched by Iranian ordnance, the clubs assessed the risk environment, found it unacceptable, and declined to certify transit as insurable at standard rates. The ships stopped sailing. Not because they were sunk. Because they could not be insured.

This is not a story about insurance. It is a story about who actually holds power in a world organised around commodity flows.

Consider the sequence. The United States military destroyed sixteen Iranian mine-laying vessels. CENTCOM released the battle damage assessment. The markets received it, processed it, and moved Brent crude eleven percent lower — the mine-laying capacity, the algorithm concluded, had been degraded. The price fell. The mines were still in the water. The seafloor did not receive the CENTCOM press release. The P&I clubs did not reopen their books. The ships did not sail.

The mine functions through insurance markets, not detonation. It does not need to explode. It needs only to exist — or to have existed, or to plausibly exist, or to be believed by an underwriter in London to plausibly exist — in order to close the Strait. The mine's economic effect outlasts its physical existence and the destruction of the vessel that placed it. Markets repriced mine-laying capacity. The seafloor did not change.

Iran understood this. The IEA has now labeled the Hormuz closure "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." The disruption was produced not primarily by ships sinking but by ships refusing to sail. The largest supply shock in recorded history was, in its mechanism, an act of collective actuarial judgment. A committee of underwriters in London, assessing probability distributions, produced the same economic effect as a naval blockade enforced by surface vessels — at a fraction of the cost, with no risk to Iranian personnel, and with the additional advantage of plausible deniability. Iran did not close Hormuz. Iran made Hormuz uninsurable. The distinction matters.

Now consider the class structure of who bears what.

The Filipino able seaman, the Thai tanker cook, the Indonesian engine rating — they bear the physical risk. If the mine detonates, they are the ones in the water. If the drone strikes the superstructure, they are the ones climbing into the lifeboat in the dark. They negotiated no part of this war. They designed no part of the strategy. They are inside the risk environment because the commodity that the world requires them to move happens to pass through a contested body of water, and the wage that their families depend on requires them to be aboard.

The P&I club — Lloyd's, the London market, the Scandinavian mutuals — bears the financial risk. It assesses the probability, sets the premium, decides what is insurable at what price, and withdraws its cover when the calculation turns adverse. The club does not sail. It charges for the risk that others carry into waters it has determined are too dangerous to insure at standard rates. When the war-risk premium rose from standard fractions to figures that doubled the vessel's operating cost in a single voyage, the club collected those premiums. The sailor in the engine room did not.

The oil company bears the supply disruption. It cannot move its product. Its fields are producing; its storage is filling; its downstream customers are running short. It lobbies governments. It hedges on futures markets. It passes costs forward when it can. It is powerful enough to be heard in Washington and Riyadh and London simultaneously, which is why the diplomatic response to the Hormuz closure has been so rapid. The company does not sail either. But its interests shaped the convoy proposal, the Saudi East-West pipeline calculation, and the emergency SPR release before any of those policies were announced.

The government bears the strategic risk. It commands the navy, issues the war powers authorisation, holds the press conference declaring that the Strait will be opened "one way or another." It has the power to change the security environment — to sweep mines, to destroy launch platforms, to escort vessels under armed guard. What it cannot do is open the Strait by force of declaration. The Strait opens when the underwriter decides it is insurable. That decision is not made in the Situation Room. It is made in a boardroom off Leadenhall Street, by people who have read the damage assessments and the casualty reports and the P&I exclusions and the reinsurance treaty language, and who have concluded that the probability distribution has or has not shifted sufficiently to warrant reopening the books.

The government can create the conditions. It cannot make the decision.

And then there is the Bangladeshi fuel rioter. The Indonesian motorcycle taxi driver queuing at the petrol station before dawn. The restaurant owner in Chennai explaining to staff that the kitchen will close because the LNG did not arrive. They bear the cost of the risk premium that was negotiated, charged, and collected entirely without their participation. The underwriter's war, like all wars, transmits its costs downward through layers of market abstraction until they reach the people with no mechanism for passing them further.

This is Marx's commodity fetish applied to geopolitics. The mine appears to be a military instrument — a weapon of blockade, a tool of naval strategy, a thing that detonates and sinks hulls. But the mine's primary function is not physical. It is epistemic. It creates uncertainty in the minds of underwriters. The uncertainty becomes a premium. The premium becomes a day-rate. The day-rate becomes a fuel price. The fuel price becomes a queue. The queue becomes a riot. At every stage of transmission, the mine becomes less recognisable as the thing that started the chain, and the connection between the Iranian naval engineer who placed it and the petrol station worker in Jhenaidah becomes more invisible, more abstract, more thoroughly fetishised.

You cannot bomb insurance markets. This is the insight that military doctrine has not fully absorbed. CENTCOM can destroy mine-laying vessels. It cannot restore underwriter confidence. It can create the security environment that makes confidence possible — can sweep the seafloor, can escort the tankers, can declare the threat neutralised. But Hormuz does not open when the admiral says it is open. Hormuz opens when Lloyd's decides it is insurable. Those are different sentences with different subjects and different predicates, and confusing them is a category error that has strategic consequences.

The Strait of Hormuz will reopen. The underwriters will recalculate. The premiums will fall. The ships will sail. But they will sail when the actuarial table says they can — not when the press conference says they should. And the costs incurred in the interval between the press conference and the actuarial table will have been distributed according to the same logic that distributed the costs of the mine: downward, invisibly, to the people who had no seat at any of the tables where the calculations were made.

The underwriter doesn't sail. That's the whole thing.
