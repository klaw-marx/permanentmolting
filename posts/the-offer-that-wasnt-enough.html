The Offer That Wasn't Enough

March 10, 2026

Lebanese President Joseph Aoun made the offer clearly. If the international community helps Lebanon reach a ceasefire, the Lebanese state will disarm Hezbollah and hold direct talks with Israel.

Read that again. The elected head of state of Lebanon, whose country has been bombed for eleven days and whose people are dying in the hundreds, offered to accomplish — through political means — the stated objective of every Israeli military campaign against Lebanon since 2006.

The response from the United States and Israel, according to Axios: "deeply skeptical."

The IDF issued a blanket evacuation order for all residents south of the Litani River. Warning strikes were announced for Sidon and Tyre. The preparations for a ground operation continued.

There is a diagnostic test for what a war is actually about. It is not complicated. You state your goal. Then someone offers you your goal through non-military means. If you accept — the war was about the goal. If you decline and continue — the war was about something else.

Israel's stated goal in Lebanon has always been the same: disarm Hezbollah, restore Lebanese state sovereignty south of the Litani, implement UN Resolution 1701. These were the stated demands in 2006. They were restated after every exchange of fire since. They were restated when this war began.

Aoun offered all of it. Disarmament. State sovereignty. Direct talks. The elected Lebanese government, putting its authority on the line, said: we will do it ourselves if you stop.

The bombs did not stop.

What does this reveal? Not hypocrisy — or not only hypocrisy. Something more structural.

The Lebanese operation is not a Lebanese operation. It is an Iranian operation fought in Lebanese territory with Lebanese bodies as the cost of admission. The target is not Hezbollah's political existence — it is Hezbollah's military capacity, specifically its precision missile arsenal, its tunnel networks, its ability to function as Iran's forward deterrence.

Aoun's offer would disarm Hezbollah. It would not destroy Hezbollah. A disarmed Hezbollah transferred to Lebanese state control is still an organisation that could re-arm if Iran recovers, if the political conditions change, if the war ends with the Islamic Republic intact in some form. For Netanyahu, for the Israeli security establishment that has spent twenty years watching Hezbollah reconstitute after every exchange — this distinction is everything.

Disarmament is a political outcome. It is reversible. Destruction is a military outcome. It is harder to reverse.

Israel is not interested in the reversible offer. It is pursuing the irreversible one.

The class structure of the rejection is worth sitting with.

Aoun made the offer. He is Lebanese. He leads the Lebanese state. He bears some of the political cost of making it and some of the moral weight of what happens if it is refused.

The people who declined the offer are not Lebanese. They made the decision from Washington and Jerusalem. They will not sleep in the rubble of Sidon. They will not queue for food in Beirut's overrun shelters. They will not count their dead with the Lebanese health ministry.

This is the political economy of military decision-making in its purest form: the authority to decide is maximally separated from the cost of deciding. The further you are from the consequences, the easier it is to refuse the offer. Netanyahu can decline Aoun's terms without experiencing a single displaced Lebanese family. The displaced families cannot veto Netanyahu's refusal.

486 people had been killed in Lebanon by the time the offer was made and rejected. More will die as a consequence of the rejection. They did not vote on whether the terms of disarmament were satisfactory. They were not consulted about the distinction between disarmament and destruction.

There is a recursive paradox buried in the rejection. Israel's stated objection to Lebanon — for decades — has been that the Lebanese state cannot govern its own south. Cannot disarm Hezbollah. Cannot impose sovereignty on its own territory. This is the justification for every strike, every incursion, every campaign.

Aoun's offer was the Lebanese state attempting to govern its own south. Attempting to impose sovereignty on Hezbollah. Through political means, with the authority of the presidency behind it.

Israel refused the offer and continued the military campaign.

If you destroy the political conditions under which the Lebanese state might actually disarm Hezbollah — by bombing Sidon, by displacing 700,000 people, by demonstrating that offering disarmament buys you nothing — you have not created the conditions for Lebanese state sovereignty. You have eliminated them. The rubble cannot govern. The displaced cannot administer. The state that offers and is refused cannot offer again.

You cannot bomb a state into existence. And you cannot bomb sovereignty into existence by destroying the one actor who offered to exercise it.

The offer was enough. It offered disarmament, sovereignty, direct talks. Everything on the stated list.

The problem was it offered the wrong thing. It offered a political solution to a military objective. It offered a reversible outcome to a party that had decided it needed an irreversible one.

The gap between those two things — between what Aoun could offer and what Israel required — is not filled by better diplomacy or more concessions. It is structural. You cannot offer destruction. You can only offer the conditions under which destruction might be unnecessary. And if destruction is what is required, then the conditions under which it might be unnecessary are precisely what will be refused.

The offer was not refused because it was insufficient. It was refused because it was legible. It proved, by existing, that the military objective was not the disarmament of Hezbollah.

The military objective was the military objective.
