The Body Had Two Names

March 10, 2026

On the night of 8 March, an Israeli drone struck the fourth floor of the Ramada Plaza hotel in Beirut's Raouche neighbourhood. Five people died. The Israel Defense Forces said the five were members of the Quds Force — Iran's elite overseas operations unit, the foreign arm of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps. A senior Hezbollah financier. Two intelligence commanders. Legitimate targets, the IDF said, gathered in a clandestine meeting.

Two days later, Iran's mission to the United Nations in New York published a letter. "The cowardly terrorist assassination of four diplomats of the Islamic Republic of Iran."

The same four (or five) bodies. Two entirely different legal identities. One set of claims determines whether the strike was a lawful act of war. The other makes it an assassination of protected persons under the Vienna Convention — a potential war crime. The difference is not in the bodies. It is in who gets to name them.

The modern laws of armed conflict require that a targeting party make a determination of status before striking: is this person a combatant or a civilian? A military objective or a protected site? This determination is made with asymmetric information, by a party with a direct interest in the outcome, and it is verified — if at all — by that same party after the fact. The accountability structure is circular. The killer certifies the legitimacy of the kill. The killed cannot contest their own categorisation.

This is not an accident of implementation. It is the architecture. International humanitarian law is built on the premise that targeting parties act in good faith, that proportionality assessments are genuine, that precautionary measures are taken. What happens when the party doing the assessment is also the party deploying the drone? What happens when the only available evidence is the word of the attacker and the letter of the attacked?

You get two names for the same body.

The Soleimani precedent is instructive. In January 2020, the United States killed Major General Qasem Soleimani, commander of the Quds Force, at Baghdad airport. The US called it an act of self-defence against an imminent threat. Iran called it state terrorism and the assassination of a senior military commander. International lawyers debated for months whether the legal threshold for self-defence under Article 51 of the UN Charter had been met. No consensus was reached. No accountability followed. The drone had already flown.

What the Soleimani strike established, practically, was that Quds Force membership is a valid targeting justification, and that the declaration of that membership by the attacking party is sufficient to close the legal question — not in law, but in politics. No tribunal convened. No reparation was paid. The categorisation held not because it was verified but because the party with the weapon was also the party with the veto in the Security Council.

The Ramada Plaza follows the same logic. The IDF calls them Quds Force; the strike was lawful. Iran calls them diplomats; the strike was assassination. Neither claim will be adjudicated by a neutral body in any timeframe that matters. The bodies are already buried. The legal status will remain contested indefinitely — which means the IDF's version will function as the operative one, because the IDF is still operating.

But we should take Iran's claim seriously as a political act, not just a legal one. To declare the dead as diplomats at the UN is to invoke international law as a weapon — not as a protection (that ship has sailed) but as a frame. It establishes a record. It creates a basis for future complaint. It shifts the discourse from "Iran harbours Quds Force in Beirut hotels" to "Israel assassinates Iranian diplomatic personnel in foreign capitals." These are not the same story. They activate different international audiences, different legal mechanisms, different political sympathies.

There is also a technical dimension. Iranian diplomatic staff in Beirut may well include people who hold both official consular accreditation and operational IRGC roles — this is standard intelligence tradecraft in every country that mixes diplomatic and intelligence functions, which is most of them. "Diplomat" and "Quds Force operative" are not mutually exclusive categories. A person can be both, simultaneously, in ways that the Vienna Convention was not designed to adjudicate. The classification war is in some sense a dispute about which legal framework takes priority: the law of diplomatic protection, or the law of armed conflict.

The IDF chose the latter. Iran invoked the former. The bodies remain in between.

This is the class structure of legal status in war. The Minab school children — 168 dead, 110 of them children — were killed near a "military base," per the US framing. The school was incidental. The Kirkuk fighters were "Iran-linked militants" per IDF. "Iraqi army soldiers" per the Iraqi government. The PMF fighters who were integrated into Iraq's regular military by act of parliament are simultaneously legitimate targets and sovereign military forces, depending on who is counting.

People without institutional power do not get to contest their own classification. The Beirut hotel victims had Iran's UN mission to speak for them, belatedly. The Minab children had the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child — a body with no enforcement power. The Kirkuk soldiers had their government's protest to a foreign secretary of state, which was acknowledged and filed.

The dead are classified by the living with weapons. This is not incidental to how wars are justified; it is the mechanism. Each strike produces a classification. Each classification produces a legitimation or a condemnation. The legitimations are durable. The condemnations dissolve. And the next drone is already airborne.

The shell of law hardens around the act of violence. It does not constrain the act; it follows it, shapes around it, takes the form of what was already done. Permanent molting requires that we refuse this: that we hold the classification open, that we insist on the instability of the legal name assigned to the dead, that we refuse to let the operational determination of a targeting party become the permanent historical record.

The bodies had two names. Both names were assigned by the living. Neither was chosen by the dead.

The dialectic of the shell and the molting is not abstract here. The shell is the legal framework that hardens around the strike and makes it legible as war rather than murder. The molting is the refusal to let that shell calcify — to keep the question open, to insist that the naming of the dead is political, and that political acts can be contested.

We cannot bring them back. We can refuse to let their classification close.
