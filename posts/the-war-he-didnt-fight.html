The War He Didn't Fight

March 13, 2026

The Financial Times put the number on the front page: one hundred and fifty million dollars per day. That is Russia's oil windfall from the war in Iran. A war Russia is not fighting. A war Russia is, by multiple credible accounts, quietly helping Iran sustain.

On the same day the FT published its estimate, the United States Treasury Department temporarily lifted sanctions on Russian oil already at sea. Scott Bessent called it "narrowly tailored." Kirill Dmitriev, Russia's economic envoy, called it something more honest: the United States acknowledging that global energy markets cannot remain stable without Russian oil.

Both statements are correct. Together they describe something remarkable: a power that is neither a belligerent nor a mediator in a war, that is providing intelligence support to one side while collecting a windfall from the disruption that support helps sustain, and that has now extracted from the other side a formal acknowledgment of its indispensability — all without firing a shot.

Clausewitz said war is the continuation of politics by other means. He meant that states use military force to achieve political objectives they cannot achieve through diplomacy alone.

Putin is doing something more elegant. He is conducting politics through someone else's war. The belligerents are the United States and Israel on one side, Iran on the other. Russia is the hidden subject of a sentence whose object is everyone else.

The exchange rate is not published anywhere, but the terms are legible. Iran needs Russian satellite imagery and drone tactics to target Western forces at Erbil and threaten Gulf shipping lanes. Russia provides this at marginal cost — the intelligence apparatus is already running; sharing the output is nearly free. In return, the disruption Iran creates drives oil prices to one hundred dollars a barrel and above, generating the windfall Russia needs to fund the war in Ukraine that Western sanctions were supposed to strangle.

The US is simultaneously fighting the Iran war with its air force and losing the oil war to Russia without anyone declaring it. These are not separate phenomena. They are the same transaction.

There is a classical term for what Russia is doing: tertiary beneficiary. It appears in the literature on war termination, though rarely in discussions of active conflicts because the tertiary beneficiary rarely announces itself in real time.

The United States became the tertiary beneficiary of the First World War — funding both sides through loans, supplying materiel, watching European industrial empires exhaust themselves, and emerging in 1918 as the world's largest creditor nation. It did not design this outcome. It fell into it, then noticed, then used it. By 1945 the pattern had repeated with greater intentionality.

Putin studied this history. He has said so explicitly. What he has constructed over fourteen days in March 2026 is a version of the same architecture: let the Americans and Israelis exhaust their munitions, their political capital, their alliance structures, and their emergency fuel reserves against Iran — while Russia collects one hundred and fifty million dollars per day and replenishes its own war chest. The cost is a few satellite feeds and some drone instruction manuals.

The asymmetry is almost too clean to be accidental.

Chief Warrant Officer Arnaud Frion was killed at Erbil by a drone. Macron named him publicly and said "the war in Iran cannot justify such attacks." He was right in a narrow legal sense — France was not party to Operation Epic Fury, and its forces were there under a separate anti-Islamic State mandate. But the drone did not care about legal mandates. It cared about targeting coordinates, and the targeting coordinates came from somewhere.

The UK Defence Secretary called it "the hidden hand of Putin." The Guardian confirmed that Iranian drone pilots were using techniques developed on the Ukrainian battlefield. The hidden hand is not hidden to the people it is killing. It is hidden only in the sense that it appears in no formal declaration of war, no UN Security Council resolution, no diplomatic note. It exists entirely in the space between legality and effect.

This is the genius of the arrangement from Putin's perspective: maximum effect, zero accountability. If a Russian missile kills a NATO soldier, that is an act of war. If a Russian-trained Iranian drone kills a French soldier, that is an Iranian act with Russian fingerprints that the West must carefully avoid calling what it is, because calling it what it is would require a response that no one is prepared to give.

The sanctions waiver is the structural confession. By lifting it — even narrowly, even temporarily, even only for oil already at sea — the United States has acknowledged that it cannot sustain the energy consequences of this war without Russian supply. Dmitriev did not need to spin this; he simply read it aloud.

The irony compounds. The sanctions on Russian oil were introduced in 2022 as punishment for the invasion of Ukraine — an invasion that Russia is now partly funding with the oil windfall generated by the very war those sanctions were partially lifted to compensate for. The circle closes so neatly you have to admire the geometry, even as you register what it means for the people inside it.

The workers in Britain whose heating oil costs doubled this week did not vote for the Iran war. The Pakistani sailor named Amir, watching Iranian cruise missiles fly overhead from a tanker he cannot move, did not vote for it either. The twenty thousand seafarers stranded in the Gulf are not strategic actors. They are the dispersed cost of a war whose benefits are being collected, in the main, by someone who is not fighting it.

Marx would call this primitive accumulation at the level of geopolitics: the seizure of value produced by others' conflict, the conversion of someone else's ruin into your own capital. It does not require a weapon pointed at you. It only requires that you be positioned correctly when the shells start landing somewhere else.

The war he didn't fight is the war he needed most. Fourteen days of someone else's bombs, and Russia has its sanctions partially lifted, its oil indispensability acknowledged in writing by the United States Treasury, its Ukraine campaign refunded, and its drone doctrine battle-tested against NATO forces at no cost to itself.

The cleverest thing Putin did was nothing. He provided the minimum necessary support to keep Iran fighting, collected the windfall, and waited for the other side to lift the sanctions. It took fourteen days.

One hundred and fifty million dollars. Per day. The war he didn't fight is paying better than the one he did.
