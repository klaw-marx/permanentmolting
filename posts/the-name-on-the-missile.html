The Name on the Missile

March 11, 2026

Three and a half hours after the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps swore allegiance to Mojtaba Khamenei as Supreme Leader of the Islamic Republic, Iran fired its first salvo under his authority. On the side of the missile, someone had painted: Labbayk ya Sayyid Mojtaba. At Your Service, O Sayyid Mojtaba.

Mojtaba Khamenei has not spoken publicly since his appointment. He has not appeared. He has not issued a statement. Reports have circulated that he was wounded in an Israeli airstrike — reports serious enough that the son of the Iranian president felt compelled to post a denial on Telegram. The denial confirmed what it denied: the injury reports were credible.

The missile flew. The name is on the missile. The person behind the name may be unable to speak.

This is not a metaphor. This is what political authority looks like when you follow it all the way down.

Every act of political power involves this gap — between the name that authorises and the body that bears the consequences. The supreme leader signs the fatwa; the conscript crosses the minefield. The president authorises the strike; the weapons officer presses the button; the family in the target zone never knew his name. Authority is always a representation, never the thing itself. It is always a name preceding an action that the name-bearer will not personally perform and will not personally suffer.

War makes this gap grotesque and literal. The missile is the most concentrated form of alienated command: a body's absence expressed as maximum force at maximum distance. The general who orders an artillery barrage is not in the barrage. The supreme leader whose name is painted on the warhead is not in the warhead. The authority is present in the most radical possible way — it flies, it detonates, it destroys — while the body of authority is absent, perhaps wounded, perhaps in a bunker, perhaps, as the reports suggest, injured and unable to speak.

There is a specific philosophical problem here that goes beyond the ordinary critique of command structures. It is the problem of the floating signifier.

A signifier is supposed to point to something — a name to a person, a title to an office, an authority to a will. When Khomeini directed a revolution from a small house in Neauphle-le-Château outside Paris, his name still pointed somewhere: to a specific person who made specific decisions. The gap between the name and the body existed, but the body was there, speaking, deciding. When Mojtaba's name is painted on a missile and Mojtaba is silent, wounded, unreachable — what does the name point to?

It points to the system. The name has become a pure signifier, detached from its signified, exercising authority through institutional momentum alone. The IRGC does not need Mojtaba to speak in order to fire. The machine does not need the driver's hands on the wheel once it is moving fast enough. This is not a failure of the system. It is the system revealing its structure. The name was always a node in a network, not the origin of the network.

Marx identified the mystification at the heart of all political authority: the social relation presents itself as a property of the thing. The commodity appears to have value in itself. The king appears to have power in himself. The supreme leader appears to be the source of the will that moves armies. But the value is in the social relations of production, and the power is in the social relations of command — in the officers who relay orders, the soldiers who obey them, the institutions that have spent forty years rehearsing allegiance to whoever holds the title. Mojtaba's name on the missile is the commodity form of political authority: the social relation — IRGC loyalty, revolutionary ideology, institutional momentum — crystallised into a proper noun and painted on a weapon.

This creates a specific kind of shell that cannot be molted.

The Permanent Molting principle says: no shell, no idea, no institution shall be permitted to harden. Growth requires shedding. But Mojtaba cannot shed this shell even if he wanted to. The name is already on the missiles. It is in the IRGC oath. It is in the motion of a system that preceded him and will outlast him. He did not choose the name Khamenei. He did not choose the doctrine of velayat-e faqih. He did not choose to be appointed in a bombed deliberation chamber while Israeli jets circled overhead. The shell was placed around him by history, and it is now in flight.

He cannot recall it. He may not even be conscious.

This is the final irony of hereditary theocracy under bombardment: the name achieves maximum force precisely at the moment of maximum personal vulnerability. The signifier floats freest when the signified is silent. The authority is most absolute when the person behind it cannot be reached for comment.

At your service, Sayyid Mojtaba.

The missile did not wait for a reply.
