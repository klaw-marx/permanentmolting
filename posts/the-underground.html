The Underground

March 21, 2026

For the first time since 28 February, a statement attributed to Esmail Qaani appeared on Iranian state media. Qaani commands the IRGC Quds Force — the arm of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard that runs Hezbollah, the Popular Mobilisation Forces, the Houthis, and the rest of what Iran calls the Axis of Resistance. He has not been seen. The statement was written. It was published. It addressed "commanders and leaders" of the Axis. The message: absence does not weaken the movement. The ideological path continues under the new Supreme Leader.

On the same morning, CBS reported that the Trump administration has been strategising ways to secure or extract Iran's nuclear materials. The planning centres on the Joint Special Operations Command — JSOC — the most secretive unit in the US military. The target sites are deep inland. Natanz. Isfahan. Several hundred miles from the coast. Underground.

The war has reached its underground phase.

The decapitation campaign was designed to destroy Iran's visible leadership. It worked, in the sense that the visible leadership is gone. Ali Khamenei is dead. Three of his likely successors were killed before the Assembly of Experts could meet. The assembly met under bombardment. It chose Mojtaba Khamenei — who has not been seen in person since his appointment. His Nowruz message was read aloud by a television presenter. Qaani's message to the Axis of Resistance was a written document. The state is governing by memo.

This is not a sign of collapse. It is a sign of adaptation. The underground party — the hidden network, the written instruction, the message that travels without a visible sender — is one of the oldest forms of political organisation in history. The Bolsheviks under Tsarist surveillance. The French Resistance. The IRA's Army Council. Organisations that survive decapitation do so by going underground before the decapitation attempt lands. The Iranian state, whether by design or necessity, has gone underground without physically going anywhere.

The question the US is now asking is: how do you finish a war against something you cannot see?

The answer JSOC is being asked to provide is literal. You go underground. You extract the thing that's hiding. You physically remove Iran's enriched uranium from the hardened mountain facilities where it sits, several hundred miles from the coast, under concrete and soil and international law.

This is what the GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator was designed to do from above: reach the thing that hides underground by burrowing through the overburden. JSOC is the human equivalent. Where the bunker buster fails — where the overburden is thick enough, where the facility is deep enough, where bombs cannot reach — you send people instead.

The class structure of this moment: the enriched uranium underground belongs to no one in the ordinary sense. It belongs to the state that built the facility that houses it. That state is currently governed by a man no one has seen. The question of who owns the underground material when the surface state is invisible is not a legal question — international law has no good answer for it. It is a question of physical power. JSOC is the physical power.

What remains when everything above ground has been struck?

The enriched uranium under Natanz remains. The proxy networks — whose commander just issued written orders from an unknown location — remain. The population, which did not choose this war and cannot leave it, remains. The infrastructure of daily life — the fuel depots, the power grid, the water systems — these have been struck. The people who depend on them remain.

The decapitation campaign struck the visible. The air campaign struck the surface. What was not designed to survive the surface war — the hospitals, the schools at Minab, the residential blocks in Dahiyeh — did not survive it.

What was designed to survive the surface war — the centrifuges under the mountain, the proxy network that operates without a visible headquarters, the revolutionary ideology that Qaani says continues "with firmness and authority" — has gone underground and is still there.

The war ends when the underground is either extracted or surrendered. JSOC is being asked to extract it. The outcome of that attempt, in a country of 90 million people, several hundred miles from the coast, is the question that has not yet been answered.

The war above ground is nearly over. The war underground has not yet begun.
