The Window

March 20, 2026

At 21:22 UTC on Friday, Trump posted to Truth Social that the United States was "getting very close to meeting our objectives as we consider winding down our great Military efforts." His press corps, flying with him on Air Force One to Florida, were handed printed copies of the post mid-flight. This is the treatment reserved for messages Trump particularly wants to amplify.

At 21:40 UTC, eighteen minutes later, the IDF ordered residents of seven named neighbourhoods in Beirut's southern suburbs to evacuate immediately, warning of operations continuing "with increasing force."

This is the window. The gap between the announcement and the ending. It is not empty.

Every party that receives the exit signal recalculates. The announcement changes the war before it changes anything on the ground.

For Israel, the window is an opportunity. If the US is heading for the exit, the IDF has a finite remaining period of American air cover, American munitions resupply, American diplomatic protection. The rational response is to use that window to deepen Israeli objectives before the clock runs out. The Dahiyeh evacuation order was not a coincidence of timing. It was a reading of the signal.

For Iran, the window is leverage. If the US is looking for an exit ramp, then Iran's remaining power is the cost it can impose in the time between the announcement and the exit. If Iran makes the endgame expensive enough — another Gulf salvo, another wave on Beirut, sustained Hormuz pressure — Trump might accelerate the exit on worse terms, or Congress might force a harder stop. The window is the last moment Iran holds active leverage over the terms of its own defeat.

For the populations under the bombs — in Dahiyeh, in Tehran, in the Gulf cities intercepting drones on a Friday night — the window is just more time. They do not receive the press pool printout on Air Force One. They receive the air raid sirens.

The class structure of war termination is this: the people who hold the frame hold the exit. Trump holds the frame. He determines when "winding down" becomes "wound down." Netanyahu holds the Israeli frame. Mojtaba holds — or attempts to hold — the Iranian frame. Each of them recalculates in response to the others.

The populations do not hold the frame. They hold the consequences.

Trump said the Hormuz policing "will have to be" handled by other nations. That phrase is doing enormous work. It presupposes a world in which the US exits and the strait somehow gets policed — by someone, under terms not yet named, with ships not yet named, under authority not yet established. The announcement declares the outcome. The mechanism is left as an exercise for the reader.

This is not unusual. Most exit announcements work this way. The announcement creates political facts; the facts then compel the mechanism. Someone will have to police Hormuz. The statement that "the US does not use it" will become the settled basis on which others are pressured to act. The announcement is the first move in the next negotiation.

But while that negotiation begins, the window remains open. And in the window, the IDF is striking Dahiyeh with increasing force, Tehran's air defences are active, and the Gulf states are intercepting their second round of drones for the day.

The war ends when the frame-holders say it does, and when they stop. Those are different moments. The announcement is the first. The stopping is the second. Between them is the window, and the window belongs to everyone who was never consulted about the frame.

The announcement is not the ending. The ending is when the bombs stop. Those are different events, and the gap between them is not measured in press pool printouts.
