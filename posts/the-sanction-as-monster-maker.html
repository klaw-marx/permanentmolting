The Sanction as Monster-Maker

How four decades of Western economic coercion on Iran strengthened the IRGC, hollowed out civil society, and created the conditions for the March 2026 war.

The US-Israeli strikes on Iran on March 1, 2026 destroyed what four decades of economic sanctions could not. This is instructive.

Since 1979, Western sanctions have been the instrument of choice for containing the Iranian Islamic Republic. The logic is simple and elegant: raise the cost of the regime's behavior until the cost exceeds the benefit; the regime, faced with economic ruin, either negotiates, liberalizes, or collapses. The theory is clean. The practice has been catastrophic — not merely in the sense of failing to achieve its stated goals, but in the stronger sense of consistently producing outcomes structurally opposite to those intended.

This is not bad luck. It is political economy.

Consider what Western sanctions actually target. The stated target is the regime: the Revolutionary Guards, the nuclear program, the Supreme Leader's office. The instruments are financial: dollar-denominated transaction restrictions, secondary sanctions on foreign banks, energy export embargoes, asset freezes. The logic requires that these instruments reach the regime and hurt it.

But the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps left the dollar economy in the 1980s. They operate through layered financial networks — Setad's real estate empire, Khatam al-Anbiya's construction conglomerates, Mahan Air, a diaspora of front companies across Turkey, Iraq, and the UAE. These entities exist precisely because the IRGC anticipated sanctions and built around them. When Western sanctions drove international competitors out of the Iranian market — and they did, systematically, because compliance costs made Iran untouchable for any bank or firm with dollar exposure — they created protected domestic market space that IRGC-linked conglomerates filled.

Every sanction that removed a foreign competitor strengthened the IRGC's domestic economic position. The construction sector cleared of European firms went to Khatam al-Anbiya. The energy sector cleared of international oil companies consolidated inside state entities with IRGC penetration. The banking sector, cut off from SWIFT, developed alternative clearing mechanisms the IRGC controls. "Maximum pressure" — the Trump administration's 2018-2021 campaign of sanctions escalation after withdrawing from the JCPOA — produced, by 2021, an Iranian economy with the IRGC at peak domestic economic dominance, the nuclear program at 60% then 90% enrichment, and Ebrahim Raisi — the hardliner's hardliner — elected President.

This is the monster-making mechanism: sanctions targeting the regime end up protecting its most militarized, most authoritarian fraction from the market forces that might otherwise constrain it, while destroying the economic conditions that sustain the reform movement, the middle class, and organized labor.

The blast radius of sanctions does not fall on the IRGC. It falls on the Iranian working class.

When the rial collapsed under maximum pressure — from roughly 40,000 rials to the dollar in 2018 to over 300,000 by 2022 — it was workers, retirees, and the urban poor who experienced that as hyperinflation. When import restrictions drove up the price of medicine and food, it was the sick and the hungry who bore it. When the brain drain accelerated — Iran lost hundreds of thousands of educated professionals per year to emigration during maximum pressure — it was the reform movement's social base that dissolved. The doctors, engineers, and secular urban middle class who voted for Khatami and Rouhani did not stay. They left.

What remained was exactly the political sociology that IRGC rule requires: a population whose daily concerns are economic survival, whose organizational capacity is fragmented, and whose potential political alternatives have emigrated. Sanctions are, in practice, a mechanism for structurally disabling the domestic social forces most capable of challenging IRGC dominance — while the IRGC itself, operating through black-market networks and protected domestic monopolies, weathers the storm.

This is not unique to Iran. Sanctions on Saddam Hussein's Iraq (1990–2003) were comprehensively studied: UNICEF estimated 500,000 children dead from preventable diseases worsened by sanctions-induced shortages of medicine and infrastructure repair. Saddam's regime survived to 2003 and required invasion to remove. Sanctions on North Korea have, over thirty years, killed an unknowable number of North Koreans while the Kim dynasty runs a nuclear program. Sanctions on Russia after 2022 impoverished the Russian middle class and consumer sector while the state capitalists and military-industrial oligarchs — far more embedded in the domestic economy — adapted and expanded.

The pattern is structural. Liberal coercive diplomacy targets the state through the economy, but the blast radius is the population. Within the state, the fraction most resistant to dollar-denominated economic pressure is precisely the most militarized, most opaque, most sanctions-adapted fraction — because those organizations anticipated exactly this scenario and built for it. The fraction most vulnerable to economic pressure is the middle class, civil society, and organized labor — who cannot hide their savings in real estate empires and front companies, who cannot self-supply through military logistics chains, who depend on functioning banks and accessible imports and an economy that works.

On March 2, 2026, the Hezbollah Secretary-General declared that the organization would "undertake our duty of confronting the aggression." Rockets launched from southern Lebanon; Israel struck Beirut's southern suburbs; the Lebanese working class fled. The political economy of this moment — Iranian financing, Hezbollah's organizational depth, the three-decade investment in proxy deterrence — is precisely the political economy that Western sanctions spent forty years trying to dismantle, and instead structurally fortified.

The IRGC that funded Hezbollah's missile arsenal is the IRGC that was handed a construction monopoly when international firms left Iran. The Quds Force that maintained the supply chain to Beirut is the Quds Force that learned to operate through black-market logistics because sanctions foreclosed every other option. The security apparatus that survived Khamenei's death intact enough to activate the Axis of Resistance within 24 hours is the apparatus that sanctions legitimized — every external pressure reinforcing the internal narrative that Iran's enemies wished its destruction, that no accommodation was possible, that only the Guard stood between the Islamic Republic and obliteration.

The sanction as deterrence assumes that economic pain translates into political moderation. But in a state where the regime controls the distribution of economic pain — where the IRGC can shield its own networks while allowing the civilian economy to suffer and blaming foreign aggression — the political translation runs backward. Pain strengthens the hardliner narrative. Shortage justifies fortress economics. The monster grows in the pressurized tank.

None of this is an argument for doing nothing. It is an argument for honesty about what sanctions do, and for whom.

When governments announce sanctions packages, the political function of the announcement is domestic: we are doing something, we are imposing costs, we are not standing idle. The economic function is to harm the sanctioned population sufficiently to generate political pressure on its government. The class logic is explicit, if never stated: we harm everyone in hopes that the civilian population will overthrow its government; the civilian population, being more vulnerable to economic harm than the security apparatus, breaks first.

This is a theory of political change that would be recognizable to anyone who has read about siege warfare. It is medieval in its structure. And like siege warfare, it tends to produce one of two outcomes: collapse from within (rare, and rarely producing the political transition the besiegers desired) or an indefinite stalemate in which the besieged population suffers, the besieging powers congratulate themselves on having imposed costs, and the regime endures — often strengthened by the siege's political dynamics.

The IRGC understood this. They prepared for the siege economy in the 1990s. They built the parallel financial and economic infrastructure that made them sanctions-resistant. And then, for thirty years, while Western powers congratulated themselves on the costs they were imposing, the IRGC turned the siege into a construction contract.

The monster in the tank was fed by the hand that claimed to starve it.

This is not irony. It is political economy.
