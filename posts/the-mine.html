The Mine

March 11, 2026

The United States Central Command destroyed sixteen Iranian mine-laying vessels near the Strait of Hormuz. Within hours, Brent crude fell eleven percent. Markets exhaled.

But the mines are still in the water.

This is the first thing to understand about the naval mine as a weapon: its effect does not end when the laying stops. A bomb's damage is bounded by detonation. The mine's damage begins at announcement and survives removal. You can destroy every vessel capable of placing a mine and the mine already placed continues to function. It sits on the seafloor, patient, doing nothing at all — and in doing nothing, it does everything.

The mine does not sink ships. It prices them.

The transmission mechanism is insurance. When the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps began mining operations in the Strait of Hormuz, the P&I clubs — the mutual insurers who cover third-party liability for most of the world's merchant fleet — did not wait for a detonation. They withdrew war-risk cover. The Strait did not close. The mines did not touch a single hull. But the insurance apparatus, which is the financial infrastructure of global shipping, made the passage economically impossible for any operator who could not self-insure or absorb an uninsured loss. This is not a side effect of the mine. It is the mine's primary mechanism.

Capital understood this before the admirals did.

Three hundred and twenty-nine tankers immobilised. VLCC rates at a record $537,000 per day — more than twenty times the peacetime average. Bunker fuel at $1,073 per tonne in Singapore, nearly double last month's price, because the bunkering market in the Gulf has been severed and supply chains are rerouting through hubs that were not designed to absorb the volume. The diesel crack spread approaching $50 per barrel, signalling that the global system is scrambling for distillate molecules that are physically present but logistically unreachable.

The mine did all of this without detonating.

Now consider who bears these costs. Not the IRGC, which spent relatively little to achieve market disruption at scale. Not the P&I clubs, which withdrew before they paid a claim. Not the major integrated oil companies, which hedged their exposure in futures markets and in some cases benefited from the price spike. The costs fell on the operators of smaller vessels, the crews who must transit regardless, the shippers in the global south whose economies depend on commodity flows that cannot be rerouted, and ultimately the working-class consumers of every country where fuel prices pass through to groceries, rent, and heating. The mine is a class instrument not by design but by consequence. It extracts from those least able to avoid it and spares those best positioned to hedge.

This is what Marx called the violence of the commodity form made literal: the weapon does not touch its victims directly. It works through the market, through the price signal, through the thousand intermediary transactions that separate the mine on the seafloor from the family that cannot afford to fill a tank. The mine is capital's own logic turned into ordinance.

Markets repriced when the mine-layers were destroyed. This is the second thing to understand: the market is not repricing the mines. It is repricing the capacity to lay more mines. These are different things. The mines already placed will require weeks of mine-countermeasure operations to locate and neutralise — assuming the US Navy has accurate charts of where they were deployed, which is not guaranteed. The eleven-percent fall in Brent crude is a bet on the mine-laying programme ending, not on the Strait being clear. If a tanker strikes a mine next week, Brent will not remember that it fell eleven percent today.

The deleted tweet is instructive here. The Secretary of Energy announced a successful tanker convoy through the Strait, then deleted the tweet. The Pentagon denied it. The gap between what happened and what could be said publicly — that gap is the mine's territory. Governments cannot announce that passage is safe when they cannot guarantee it. They cannot announce that the Strait is dangerous without triggering the panic they are trying to prevent. So they say nothing, or they say something and delete it, and the mine continues to function through the silence.

The bomb is a statement. The mine is a question that cannot be answered.

There is a final dimension that the price charts do not capture. Every sailor who transits the Strait in the coming weeks does so knowing the mines may still be there. The crew of a VLCC carrying two million barrels of crude oil through the Hormuz chokepoint is not hedging. They are not in a trading room watching spreads. They are in the water. The risk premium that markets quantify in dollars per barrel is, for them, a different kind of arithmetic entirely. The mine extracts its costs from capital in basis points. From labor, it extracts the decision of whether to go through.

That is not the same thing. It never was.

The mine-layers are gone. The mines remain. The market has already moved on.

The seafloor has not.
