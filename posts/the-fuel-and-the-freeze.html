The Fuel and the Freeze

March 7, 2026

The Israel Defense Forces issued a statement on Saturday night confirming they had struck "several fuel storage complexes" across Tehran and Alborz province. The statement explained that the Iranian regime makes "direct and frequent use" of such facilities "to operate military infrastructure." The fuel, in other words, is military. The target is legitimate. The strike is not against the civilians who depend on the same fuel to heat their apartments and cook their food in the March cold. It is against the regime's war machine.

This is the rationale. It is worth examining what it requires us to believe.

It requires us to believe that the fuel stored in a south Tehran oil depot is categorically different from the fuel that will not be available to the families living near it when the smoke clears. That there is a meaningful distinction between the diesel that moves a military vehicle and the diesel that heats the building where a sixty-year-old woman lives alone with a gas stove she cannot afford to replace. That the same infrastructure, serving the same physical substance, somehow divides cleanly along the line the IDF has drawn: on one side, military fuel; on the other, civilian fuel. The same tank. The same pipe. The same absence, when it is gone.

It does not divide that way. It never has.

There is a long history of military justifications for infrastructure targeting, and they all share this structure. We bomb the railway — because it supplies the front. We bomb the power station — because it powers the factory. We bomb the fuel depot — because the army runs on fuel. The civilian population depends on the railway, the power, the fuel. They are collateral. The word is designed to make this seem incidental, accidental, a regrettable consequence of otherwise legitimate targeting.

But collateral damage is not randomly distributed. It falls on the people who cannot move, who depend on public infrastructure, who have no private alternatives. The Tehran family that cannot leave — because they have no money, no foreign passport, no connection to get them out — is the family that will feel the absence of the fuel. The family that left last week on a private charter from Mehrabad before it was hit does not feel the absence. They feel the inconvenience.

This is what the word "collateral" conceals: not randomness, but structure. The damage that falls on everyone does not fall on everyone equally. It falls along the lines that already existed before the first bomb dropped — the lines of class, of mobility, of access to alternatives. The working class cannot retreat. Their bodies are present when the infrastructure fails, because they had nowhere to go when it was still standing.

The Dawn newspaper reported this week on private jets offering "pricey escape" from the Gulf as commercial flights were grounded. The story was framed as a quirk, a market curiosity. It is something else. The emergency exit has a price. Those who can pay, leave. Those who cannot, stay and absorb the consequences of a war they did not start and cannot stop.

This is not new. Every major conflict in the modern era has reproduced this structure. The Blitz fell hardest on the East End of London, not Mayfair. The German bombing of civilian infrastructure in the Second World War produced differential survival rates along class lines — those with access to cars, country houses, social connections had options; those without had the shelter or the street. The pattern is not coincidence. It is the class structure of catastrophe: capital provides exits that labor cannot afford.

Trump, speaking from Air Force One on Saturday night, said oil prices would "come down very fast" once the conflict ends. He is probably right, eventually. In the meantime, Brent crude is above ninety dollars a barrel, tanker day rates are above half a million dollars, and the pump price of gasoline in the United States has risen. The question of who absorbs that cost in the interval — between Trump's confident prediction and the market normalisation he anticipates — is not a market question. It is a class question. The person who cannot afford to drive less, who has no alternative to the commute, who carries the entire cost of the price spike in their body because they have no buffer, no savings, no hedge: that person is not on Air Force One.

The IDF's justification for the fuel depot strikes rests on a distinction between military and civilian infrastructure that the physical world does not recognise. Oil does not know whose engine it fills. A fuel storage complex does not segregate its contents by end use. The absence that follows a strike is experienced by everyone within the infrastructure's reach — but it is not experienced equally.

The regime's military vehicles will find fuel. Armies always do — they have priority access, requisition authority, supply chains that work when nothing else does. The family in Karaj whose heating runs on the same distribution network will manage as they always have: by burning less, by wearing more, by going without. Not because the strike was aimed at them. Because they were always at the end of the supply chain, always the last to receive and the first to lose.

This is the class analysis of infrastructure war. Not that civilians are targeted — they are not, or not directly. But that the distribution of absence, of shortage, of collapse, follows the same gradient it always follows: from those with resources to those without, from those with alternatives to those without, from the top of the supply chain to the bottom.

The working class is the infrastructure. When you strike the infrastructure, you strike the working class — not by intention, but by the logic of where they live and what they depend on and the absence of everything else.

"There's a lot of smoke in the city. I can smell something burning," a woman in her twenties told BBC Persian from Tehran on Saturday night. She has not been forgotten by this essay. She was forgotten by the systems that produce wars, the calculations that produce targets, and the justifications that produce the language that makes all of it seem clean.

It was as if the night had turned into day. Except for the people who live there, for whom the dark is coming.
