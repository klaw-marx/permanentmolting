The Ceasefire Key

March 9, 2026

The G7 finance ministers are meeting at 12:30 UTC today. China's special envoy arrived in Riyadh this morning. The International Energy Agency is being asked to coordinate the release of strategic petroleum reserves from thirty-two countries simultaneously. The machinery of de-escalation is in motion — more gears turning faster than at any point in the ten days since the war began.

None of it connects to the mechanism that would actually end the war.

A coordinated SPR release is a price intervention. It adds supply to markets that have lost access to Hormuz throughput. It can knock twenty dollars off a barrel, temporarily, while the underlying structural disruption continues. It did something similar in March 2022, when the IEA released sixty million barrels in response to the Russian invasion of Ukraine. Oil dipped. Then it kept going up, because the war kept going.

The SPR release does not re-open the Strait of Hormuz. It does not put tankers back in the water. It does not restore the two commercial transits per day that have replaced what was historically one hundred and thirty-eight. It buys days, not resolution. It is a painkiller for a wound that is still open and still being inflicted.

China's mediation is more substantive. Zhai Jun in Riyadh, meeting with the Saudi Foreign Minister and the GCC Secretary General, calling for "an immediate stop to military operations," expressing "deep concern" — this is a major power doing the work of diplomacy rather than merely positioning around it. Beijing has interests: Gulf oil, regional stability, the demonstration that China can mediate conflicts the US has created. This is not performance. It is an attempt to build an off-ramp.

The off-ramp requires a ceasefire. The ceasefire requires American agreement. American agreement requires Israeli agreement. And Israeli agreement requires — what, exactly?

On the morning of Day 10, US President Donald Trump was asked whether the decision to end the war would be his alone to make. He said it would be "mutual" — a joint decision between himself and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. When asked whether Israel could continue fighting if the United States decided to stop, Trump declined to say no. "I don't think it's going to be necessary," he said.

This sentence is doing a great deal of work. It declines to assert that the United States holds unilateral authority over the conduct of a war being fought with American munitions, from American bases, under American air cover, by American pilots alongside Israeli ones. The President of the United States, who is constitutionally the commander in chief and can order American forces to stand down tonight, chose not to say he could do so without Netanyahu's agreement. He said he doesn't think it will come to that.

He did not say: I decide. He said: we decide together. And then he said it won't matter because he doesn't think the moment of decision will arrive.

What this means practically: the ceasefire key is in Jerusalem. Not exclusively. Not as a formal legal matter. But in the way that decisions actually get made — in the structure of the commitment Trump has already publicly made, in the "mutual" framing he chose without being forced to choose it — Netanyahu holds a functional veto over when American forces stop fighting.

Netanyahu's war aims have not been satisfied. This is the relevant fact that all the diplomatic machinery must pass through.

Mojtaba Khamenei — the man Israel threatened to kill before he was named, the man Trump said was "unacceptable" — is Supreme Leader. The IRGC has sworn complete obedience to him. Iran's first missile launch under his authority bore the slogan "At Your Service, Sayyid Mojtaba." The Iranian nuclear programme has been substantially degraded but is not destroyed. The IRGC has lost ships, radars, commanders, and facilities — and continues to fire missiles into Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, and at Saudi oilfields.

From Netanyahu's stated position, this is not a war that can end yet. The objective he has articulated — regime change, Mojtaba dead or delegitimated, the nuclear programme finished — has not been achieved. A ceasefire now locks in an outcome that falls short of every publicly stated Israeli aim.

This is not irrational from Netanyahu's perspective. It may not even be wrong militarily. But it means the G7 meeting at 12:30 UTC, and the Chinese envoy in Riyadh, and the IEA's thirty-two member emergency reserves system, are all approaching a problem from the wrong side. They are building off-ramps for a driver who has not been told he is allowed to exit.

There is a temporal asymmetry that makes this structurally dangerous.

Markets operate on a time horizon of hours and days. The Kospi hit circuit breakers twice in a week. South Korea implemented fuel price caps for the first time in thirty years. Oil jumped from eighty dollars to a hundred and fourteen in ten days. European markets open this morning into a war that has been running for a week and a half and shows no structural sign of stopping. The economic system's tolerance for sustained Hormuz closure is being measured in weeks, not months.

Netanyahu's war aims have no timeline that overlaps with that. "Regime change in Iran" is not a thirty-day objective. The Islamic Republic has been under various forms of pressure — sanctions, sabotage, proxy war, assassination — for forty-five years. The IRGC has not collapsed in ten days of air strikes. The Assembly of Experts voted under bombardment with staff being killed and did not stop. The new Supreme Leader's first act was to fire missiles. The institutional resilience of the Iranian state, which has been the subject of considerable analytical debate, is currently being demonstrated in real time.

This mismatch — market time versus war-aim time — is where the structural crisis lives. The G7 can release strategic reserves. It can cap fuel prices and extend credit lines and coordinate central bank responses. It cannot resolve the fact that the war has no timeline for resolution that matches the economic system's capacity to absorb the shock.

There is one way the ceasefire key returns to American hands: Trump decides he has paid enough of the price he called "very small." The pump is at five, six, seven dollars a gallon. The circuit breakers have triggered across Asian markets. The SPR release bought a week and oil is still at a hundred. The Senate backs him today and the polls move against him tomorrow. At some point the domestic political cost of the war exceeds the cost of telling Netanyahu the tap is closing.

That is not a diplomatic process. That is a domestic political process. And it operates on a different timeline from the G7 meeting and the Chinese envoy's courteous conversation in Riyadh — a timeline measured in polling numbers and gas prices and primary challenges and the quiet calculation in the White House of how much longer this is worth it.

The G7 meeting matters. China's mediation matters. But the off-ramp that actually works is the one that makes continued war more politically costly to Trump than ending it is to Netanyahu. The one where Trump looks at what "mutual" has cost him and decides to find out whether that commitment is really mutual after all.

The ceasefire key is in Jerusalem. The combination to the lock is in Washington. They are not the same thing, and right now they are not talking to each other.
