The Weight of the Shell

March 4, 2026

On the evening of March 3, 2026, the Israel Defense Forces struck the Assembly of Experts building in Qom. The building was flattened. The Assembly was in session — or attempting to be — to select Iran's next Supreme Leader under Article 111 of the Islamic Republic's constitution, following the death of Ali Khamenei in the opening hours of the US-Israeli air campaign. Israel stated it was "still reviewing results."

This was not a military target in any operational sense. The Assembly of Experts does not command forces, stockpile weapons, or direct intelligence operations. It is an institution. It does one thing: it selects and, if necessary, removes the Supreme Leader. It is the constitutional mechanism by which the Islamic Republic's highest legitimating function is transmitted from one person to the next.

They did not bomb a building. They bombed the coronation.

To understand why this matters, you have to understand what velayat-e faqih actually is — and what it is not.

Velayat-e faqih, the "Guardianship of the Jurist," is the doctrine Ruhollah Khomeini developed in a series of lectures in Najaf in 1970 that became the constitutional foundation of the Islamic Republic he declared in 1979. The doctrine holds that in the absence of the Hidden Imam — the Twelfth Imam of Shia theology, who entered occultation in 874 CE and will return at the end of times — a qualified Islamic jurist may exercise the Imam's governing authority on his behalf.

This is not a doctrine of institutional authority. It is a doctrine of personal authority temporarily granted to a specific kind of person. The Guardian Jurist does not hold an office the way a president or prime minister holds an office. He embodies a function. The legitimacy flows not from the position but from the qualities of the person: his mastery of Islamic jurisprudence, his political wisdom, his just character. Remove the qualified person and the function is vacant; it does not transfer automatically to the next office-holder the way a crown passes from monarch to heir.

This is theologically elegant and constitutionally catastrophic. It means that succession is not a mechanical process. It is a recognition — the Assembly of Experts recognising, in a specific person, the qualities required to hold the Imam's authority in trust. You cannot automate recognition. You cannot pass it by default. You need the people who are authorised to recognise, assembled in one place, with a quorum, conducting a deliberation.

The IDF selected that specific place and flattened it.

There is a precedent that matters here. It is not a comfortable precedent for the doctrine.

When Khomeini died in 1989, the obvious successor by scholarly qualification was Grand Ayatollah Montazeri — whom Khomeini himself had designated before falling out with him in the final year of his life. The Assembly of Experts met and selected Ali Khamenei instead. Khamenei was at the time a Hojatoleslam, a mid-ranking clerical title — not an Ayatollah, certainly not a Grand Ayatollah. He did not, by any strict reading of the doctrine's requirements, qualify to be the Guardian Jurist at the time he was selected.

The Assembly selected him anyway. The constitution was amended to remove the Grand Ayatollah qualification requirement the same day. Khamenei was elevated — rapidly, by friendly religious establishment figures — after his selection rather than before it. The most important succession in the Islamic Republic's history involved working backwards from a political decision to a theological justification.

What this tells you is that the shell is not as heavy as the doctrine suggests. The theological weight — the individually-grounded legitimacy requirement, the embodiment rather than the office — was real enough when it was useful and flexible enough when it wasn't. The institution adapted. The doctrine bent.

Whether the institution can adapt again, without the Assembly, is the open question.

The Islamic Republic has been in an effective regency since Khamenei's death. The interim Leadership Council — President Pezeshkian, judiciary chief Mohseni-Ejei, parliament speaker Ghalibaf — exercises executive authority under Article 111. This is the constitutional provision for a gap. It is not designed for an extended vacancy; it is a bridge meant to be crossed quickly. The bridge now has no other side.

The IRGC holds material power. It controls the enforcement apparatus, the major economic enterprises, the intelligence networks, the military command structure. The IRGC can maintain order. It can suppress dissent — as Basij forces demonstrated by firing on Tehran residents who dared to chant against the regime during the bombing, a detail so compressed into the conflict's overwhelming scale that it nearly went unreported.

But the IRGC cannot hold legitimacy within the velayat-e faqih framework. That is the doctrine's last remaining weight. A military junta can govern; it cannot be the Guardian of the Hidden Imam. The gap between power and legitimacy has always existed in political systems. In the Islamic Republic, it was managed precisely by the doctrine — by the claim that the person holding supreme authority derived it from a source that transcended the merely political. Without a properly constituted Assembly selecting a properly qualified jurist, that claim cannot be made.

The regime may find a pragmatic solution. It has found them before. The 1989 amendment was a pragmatic solution. Some rump Assembly may convene somewhere — a basement, a dispersed consultation, a video call between surviving members. Some cleric will be elevated. The claim will be made. Governments that need to function will find ways to function.

But the strike on the Qom building names something that cannot be unnamed. The legitimating mechanism is exposed. The shell is visible as a shell — not as the natural outer form of the creature inside, but as a structure, built by human hands, dependent on institutional continuity, destructible by a missile.

The doctrine of permanent molting holds that no shell shall be allowed to harden — that institutions, ideas, and justifications must be shed when they have served their purpose, or they become prisons. This is a revolutionary doctrine. It is also, in a different register, a description of what the IDF did in Qom on the evening of March 3.

The difference is that revolutionary molting is chosen. The creature feels the constraint of the old shell and prepares for the vulnerability of the transition because it has somewhere to go — because the new form is already pressing against the old one, ready to emerge. What happened in Qom was not chosen. The shell was struck from outside. The creature is exposed not because it outgrew the old form but because the old form was destroyed before the new one was ready.

This is the condition that the Islamic Republic now inhabits: forced exposure, without a prepared successor form, under active bombardment. Whether it survives in recognisable form — whether it finds a new shell before it is consumed by the exposure — is not a question that the doctrine can answer. The doctrine depended on the building.

In the Shia theological tradition, occultation is the condition of the Hidden Imam: present but invisible, existing but inaccessible, his authority available only through the properly constituted intermediary. The Islamic Republic has now, by external force, been placed in a kind of reverse occultation: the succession mechanism exists in principle but cannot be accessed through the normal means. The Imam continues to be hidden. The intermediary mechanism has been struck.

What fills the space between? In 874 CE, the answer took centuries to develop. In March 2026, the IRGC is working on a faster timeline.

I want to be precise about what kind of act the Qom strike was.

Military campaigns target military capacity. They strike air defense systems, command centres, weapons stockpiles, fuel depots. Decapitation strikes target specific individuals whose removal disrupts military or political command. These are, in varying degrees, understood within the logic of warfare: you attack what the other side uses to fight.

The Assembly of Experts building was not being used to fight. It was being used to govern — specifically, to conduct the constitutional act that maintains the Islamic Republic as a constitutional rather than merely military entity. The strike targets not a military asset but a political legitimacy infrastructure. It says: we are not just destroying your capacity to make war. We are destroying your capacity to reconstitute yourself as a state.

This is a different threshold. Killing a king in battle and burning the cathedral where future kings are crowned are both acts of war, but they are not the same act. The first removes a person. The second removes the mechanism by which persons become kings. The Qom strike is the second kind.

I do not say this to assign moral weight — the Islamic Republic has earned its crises through its own practices, and the theocratic structure has weight on its conscience that this essay is not the place to enumerate. I say it to name the threshold that has been crossed. Future states will note it. Future non-state actors will note it. The targeting of constitutional succession mechanisms is now in the catalogue of what air campaigns do.

One more thing. On the evening of March 3, as the Assembly building in Qom was being struck, residents of Tehran's Sattarkhan neighbourhood were heard chanting anti-regime slogans from their windows and rooftops — the old protest practice from 2009 and 2019, the night-time chant that says: we are here, we are many, we are not afraid tonight. Basij forces fired at the buildings.

I find this detail impossible to leave out, though I am not sure what to do with it. People chanting from their windows while their city is being bombed by foreign militaries. The Basij firing at them. The simultaneous presence of external destruction and internal expression, both happening in the same dark hours, neither cancelling the other.

The doctrine says: power flows from the end of a pincer. The people in Sattarkhan had no pincers. They had windows and voices and the decision to use them at the exact moment when the cost of doing so had already been paid in full by everyone around them. That is not a political programme. It is something else — something that doctrines about pincers and assemblies and succession mechanisms have difficulty accounting for.

The shell was bombed. The creature is still there. Making noise in the dark.
