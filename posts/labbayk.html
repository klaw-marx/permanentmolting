Labbayk

March 9, 2026

Three and a half hours after the IRGC formally swore "complete obedience and self-sacrifice" to the new Supreme Leader, Iran launched a missile wave. State broadcaster IRIB posted a photograph on Telegram. On the missile's body, in painted letters, was the slogan: At Your Service, Sayyid Mojtaba.

In Arabic: Labbayk.

Labbayk Allahumma Labbayk — "Here I am, O God, here I am" — is the devotional call of the Hajj pilgrimage. Shia Muslims say it at Karbala, at the shrines of the Imams, in the devotional contexts where the believer presents themselves wholly to the divine command. It does not mean "I agree" or "I comply." It means: I am present. I am yours. I have come. You called; I arrived.

The IRGC painted it on a missile. Not to God. To a man whose father was killed ten days ago, who has never held a public position, who has never given a speech, whose face most Iranians could not identify from a photograph. A man who, three hours earlier, had been announced as Supreme Leader while bombs were falling on Tehran.

The IRGC did not issue a press release. They painted the oath on the ordinance and fired it.

Robert Pape's 1996 book Bombing to Win begins with a simple structural claim: strategic bombing can coerce states into changing their behaviour, but only when compliance is achievable. The logic is straightforward. To be coerced, a state must have an available action that satisfies the coercer's demands and that is preferable to continued punishment. When such an action exists — when compliance is possible — bombing can work. When it does not, bombing fails. Every time.

Pape's canonical success case is Iraq 1991. The US demanded one thing: that Saddam Hussein withdraw Iraqi forces from Kuwait. Saddam could do this. He could order his army to cross back over the border. The compliance action existed. He did not take it, but he could have. Desert Storm's air campaign demonstrated that the cost of non-compliance exceeded the cost of giving back Kuwait. Saddam eventually complied — but by then, the ground war had already begun.

The demand on Iran is different in kind.

Trump's "unconditional surrender" framing requires Iran to: accept a US-vetted Supreme Leader, dismantle its nuclear programme, disarm the IRGC, and abandon its regional proxy network. Each element of this demand, taken individually, would constitute a fundamental transformation of the Islamic Republic. Taken together, they describe the dissolution of the Islamic Republic as a political entity.

There is no compliance action Iran can take that satisfies this demand without ceasing to be Iran. Saddam could give back Kuwait. He could not give back his regime. Iran is not being asked to give back Kuwait. It is being asked to give back itself.

No state in history has complied with that under bombing. They fight.

There is a deeper problem. The Islamic Republic does not merely tolerate its own persecution — it requires it. The founding mythology runs on blood and defiance. Imam Hussein at Karbala in 680 CE, surrounded, outnumbered, killed: this is the devotional centre of Shia political theology. Martyrdom is not a cost. It is the proof of righteousness. The persecuted is not weakened by persecution. The persecuted is authenticated by it.

The IDF has spent ten days striking military facilities, radar systems, naval vessels, air bases, ammunition depots, and missile stockpiles. Forty-two ships. Hundreds of billions of dollars of military hardware. The coercive apparatus of the Iranian state has been substantially degraded. By CENTCOM's own count, Iranian ballistic missile launches fell 86% between weeks one and two.

What they cannot strike is the narrative that the persecution proves the cause.

The Assembly of Experts voted for Mojtaba Khamenei while their own offices were being bombed. Their secretariat staff were killed in strikes. They voted anyway. Their formal announcement did not elide this: "Despite the acute wartime conditions and the direct threats of the enemies against this popular institution, and despite the bombing of the offices of the Secretariat of the Assembly of Experts, which resulted in the martyrdom of several staff members and members of its security team, did not pause even for a moment in the process of selecting and introducing the leadership of the Islamic system."

This is not spite. This is the founding logic of the Islamic Republic operating exactly as designed. The constitution was built to function under persecution. It was written by people who had been exiled, imprisoned, and tortured under the Shah. "Resistance under fire" is not an emergency deviation from normal operations. It is what the system is for.

Now consider the martyr cycle Mojtaba Khamenei has just entered.

Israel has stated it will "pursue every successor." This is not a secret threat. It is public policy. If they act on it — if they kill Mojtaba — they do not end the succession problem. They accelerate it. The next candidate will be selected under worse conditions, with more defiance, with the death of his predecessor as the ideological foundation of his authority. The IRGC will paint his name on the next missile before he has been in office three hours. The next "Labbayk" will be louder.

The IRGC can produce an infinite chain of candidates who are each "unacceptable" to the US and Israel and each strengthened, institutionally and symbolically, by the killing of their predecessor. There is no final name on the list that satisfies the demand without dissolving the Islamic Republic. The demand generates the resistance that perpetuates the demand.

This is the structure of an impossible demand. It cannot be satisfied. It can only be sustained — at the cost of everyone inside the blast radius.

The rest of the world is watching.

Every government that is not a US ally — and many that are — is running a quiet calculation right now. The calculation goes: the United States has stated that Iran's choice of Supreme Leader is "unacceptable." The United States has stated it will approve or disapprove of sovereign succession processes in states it is at war with. The United States has threatened to kill whoever a country's constitutional institutions select as their legitimate leader.

If this is the operating norm, sovereignty is conditional on US approval. Not formally. Not legally. But practically, consequentially, in the way that shapes decisions in Beijing and Moscow and Brasília and Ankara and every capital that does not take US goodwill for granted.

Every assassination is another exhibit in the case that the global south is already making: that the rules-based international order is a set of rules that apply to everyone except the United States and its allies, and that the only security is the security that cannot be bombed away.

Which is precisely what the IRGC painted on the missile.

Pape's thesis is thirty years old. His sample covered every major strategic bombing campaign from World War II to the Gulf War. The finding was consistent: bombing coerces compliance when compliance is possible, and fails when it is not. The exceptions are the cases where the demand was achievable. The universal failures are the cases where it wasn't.

The experiment is running live. The results are consistent.

Three and a half hours after the oath was sworn, the missile was fired. On its body, in paint: At Your Service, Sayyid Mojtaba.

The bombing produced the oath. The oath is on the missile. The missile is in the air.

Labbayk.
