The Maduro Mirage

March 10, 2026

After his press conference on Day 10, Donald Trump made an unscheduled stop at El Arepazo, a Venezuelan restaurant in Doral, Florida. He bought arepas. He greeted Venezuelan exiles who told him he was the greatest president of their lifetimes. He didn't mention Iran.

He didn't need to. He had already named his template.

Asked at the press conference who he wanted to lead Iran, Trump said he preferred someone "internal" — and cited Venezuela's Delcy Rodríguez as a model. A cooperative insider with existing institutional legitimacy who could work with the United States. Not an externally imposed figure, not a full regime change. A pliable indigenous outcome.

The Venezuelan restaurant made the symbolism complete. Trump is not pursuing an abstract strategic goal in Iran. He is pursuing a specific historical precedent: the fall of Maduro, the return of the exiles, the handshake with whoever inherits the rubble. He wants to replicate the feeling of watching Venezuelans celebrate in Doral. He wants his Iranian equivalent.

The problem is that the Venezuela template is broken at precisely the point that matters.

Let us be precise about what happened in Venezuela.

Maduro fell not because the United States bombed him. The United States sanctioned him — oil revenue, financial access, technology. For years. The Venezuelan economy contracted by 75% over the course of a decade. Hyperinflation destroyed savings. Food shortages drove seven million Venezuelans out of the country. The military, which had been Maduro's instrument, watched its own purchasing power evaporate and its institutional standing collapse. When the internal fracture finally came, it came from within: defections, deals, factionalism.

The exile community in Miami and Doral was the ideological infrastructure for the successor government. They had maintained networks, credentials, and international legitimacy across years of diaspora. They could walk back in and be recognised as "Venezuela" by the institutions that matter.

This is the model Trump wants to replicate. The problem is that he has changed the mechanism — from sanctions to bombs — and changed the institutional target — from a personalised kleptocracy to a revolutionary theocracy — without updating his expectations for what those changes produce.

Maduro's Venezuela was a regime. The Islamic Republic of Iran is a state.

The distinction matters. A regime is a set of people who have captured state institutions for their own benefit. When the regime fractures — when the people who benefited from it stop believing their interests are served by it — the state persists and can be redirected. The PSUV held power through patronage. Remove the patronage, and the network disaggregates. The military that served Maduro served him because of what he gave them. When he could no longer give it, their loyalty became negotiable.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps is not a patronage network. It is a revolutionary institution with its own theology, its own reason for existing, its own founding mythology. It was created to embody the revolution — not to serve it instrumentally, but to be it. The IRGC's legitimacy does not flow from the Supreme Leader's patronage. The Supreme Leader's legitimacy flows, in part, from the IRGC's recognition. When Ali Khamenei was killed on Day 1 of Operation Epic Fury, the IRGC swore allegiance to Mojtaba within hours. There was no hesitation. No negotiation. No defection calculation. The institution recognised its new head and continued its mission.

The bombs that were supposed to fracture the system authenticated it instead.

The exile community problem compounds this.

The Venezuelan opposition in Doral were useful as a successor government because they were recognisable as Venezuela to the outside world, and because their exile was understood domestically as the result of political persecution rather than ideological contamination. Maduro's critics were suppressed, not delegitimised by association with foreign enemies.

The Iranian exile community — particularly the monarchist strand around Reza Pahlavi — supported the US-Israeli strikes. Pahlavi made public statements backing the campaign. Within Iran, those statements were used against him immediately. The Islamic Republic's founding mythology was explicitly constructed as a rejection of the Shah and everything he represented, including his relationship with the United States. An Iranian exile who cheers American bombs does not play the same role that a Venezuelan exile who cheers American sanctions plays. The former is, in the Islamic Republic's political vocabulary, a traitor and a collaborator. The latter was a refugee.

Trump's Iranian equivalent of Doral does not exist. The exile community that could credibly walk back in after the bombs stop does not have the domestic legitimacy the Venezuelan diaspora had. The Islamic Republic taught two generations of Iranians that this is exactly what a foreign-backed restoration looks like. The lesson has been learned.

There is a final structural problem, which is the one Trump named without realising its significance.

He said he wanted someone "internal." He is right that any sustainable outcome requires an internal figure with genuine legitimacy. But the internal figures with genuine legitimacy who might have been amenable to a cooperative arrangement with the United States have, by Trump's own admission, been killed in the strikes. "Many moderate options have reportedly been killed in US strikes," BBC's Debusmann noted after the presser. The bombardment has not created the conditions for a Delcy Rodríguez. It has reduced the pool of people who could be one.

The Iran that will survive these strikes will be more IRGC-dominated, not less. The Assembly of Experts voted for Mojtaba under bombardment. The IRGC declared obedience. The Iranian state's civil institutional architecture has demonstrated that it can function without its leadership cadre — it simply replaces them and continues. The moderate, internationally-credible, internally-legitimate figure that the Venezuela model requires does not survive in an environment where being associated with moderation is now associated with the faction that was destroyed by enemy bombs.

Bombing does not create Delcy Rodríguez. It creates Mojtaba Khamenei.

Trump left the Venezuelan restaurant with arepas for Air Force One. He did not mention Iran. The symbolism was doing the work he wanted it to do: the exiles who were right would go home, the country that chose the wrong side would find its way back, and the president who had the courage to act would be remembered alongside the presidents who were too cautious to do what needed to be done.

The mirage is not that the goal is unworthy. Cooperative governments are better than hostile ones. Exiles going home is better than exiles staying exiled. The mirage is the mechanism — the belief that bombing a theocratic revolutionary state produces the same internal dynamics as sanctioning a personalised kleptocracy.

Venezuela's revolution had already eaten itself before the United States got involved. The IRGC's revolution is eating everyone else. The institutions that would need to fracture for the Venezuela model to work are the institutions that have just been hardened by the attempt to break them.

The arepas will run out. The template will not deliver the meal it promised. What remains on the table when it doesn't is the question that no one in Doral is asking yet.
