The Viable Plan

March 16, 2026

The United Kingdom is "working with all of our allies to bring a viable plan to restore freedom of navigation to the region." That was UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer, on Monday morning, at a lectern in Downing Street.

Germany "does not see that NATO has made any decision in this direction or could assume responsibility for the Strait of Hormuz." That was the German Foreign Minister, speaking to reporters ahead of an EU foreign ministers meeting in Brussels.

Japan is considering how to respond but notes it is "extremely difficult under Japanese law" and will act "within the framework of current law." That was Prime Minister Takaichi, pre-positioning before her Washington summit.

France's carrier strike group is staying in "the eastern Mediterranean." NATO is "aware that individual allies are talking with the US." China "reiterates its call for all parties to immediately cease military operations." Australia has formally declined.

Every major democracy has found its phrasing. They are different phrasings. They perform different political functions for different domestic audiences. But they share an architecture: acknowledge the problem, acknowledge its importance, commit to nothing, and preserve the ability to exit.

The name for this architecture is a viable plan.

The viable plan is not a plan. It is a category of political speech. It does what a plan does — it signals seriousness, competence, engagement — without doing what a plan requires: specifying assets, timelines, rules of engagement, and who bears the cost when something goes wrong.

A plan has a date. A plan has a ship name. A plan has a chain of command. A plan has a legal framework authorising its execution, a logistics tail, and someone whose career ends if it fails.

A viable plan has none of these things. It has the word "viable," which sounds like something assessable — either viable or not viable — but in practice means "we are working on it" and "you should ask us again later."

The genius of the viable plan, as a political form, is that it is unfalsifiable in the short term. You cannot be held accountable for a viable plan the way you can be held accountable for a plan that failed. The viable plan is always still in development. The allies are still being consulted. The assessment is ongoing. The conditions are still being evaluated.

By the time anyone notices that the viable plan has not produced anything viable, the crisis has either resolved itself or metastasised into something that makes the original viable plan irrelevant.

Starmer offered a rare honest account of the logic that generates the viable plan. A journalist asked him whether he would commit to keeping the Strait of Hormuz open. The prime minister said the discussions were "not easy or straightforward." And then he said this:

"If you're prime minister, you don't get to come back a week later and say — oops I made a mistake about that war, can I back out of it now?"

This is not a reason to stay out of the war. It is an explanation of why the viable plan is the only rational posture for a democratic leader who does not want to be in the war but cannot afford to say so explicitly.

The irreversibility of commitment is the structural fact that the viable plan is designed to navigate. Once you send a warship, you have sent it. Once you commit to a mission, you are committed. The legal, political, military, and reputational costs of withdrawal are higher than the costs of never having entered. The viable plan is how you preserve optionality — the ability, in Starmer's words, to come back a week later and say oops.

This is not cowardice. It is architecture. Every leader in every major democracy is building the same architecture, in their own language, for their own constituency. Starmer builds it in the language of "British national interest" and "not a NATO war." The German Foreign Minister builds it in the language of "Ukraine remains Europe's top priority." Japan builds it in the language of Article 9. France builds it in the language of Mediterranean posture. China builds it in the language of ceasefire calls.

Different dialects. One grammar. The grammar of the non-commitment that performs as commitment.

Here is the structural fact underneath all the grammar: the United States pulled its only mine-countermeasure platforms in the Gulf to Malaysia in February. There are no coalition minesweepers committed to the Strait. There are no named naval assets, no published timetable, no agreed rules of engagement. There is a coalition of seven countries, all of whom have been asked, none of whom have committed anything operational.

The mines are still in the water. The seafloor does not attend the press conferences where viable plans are announced.

The tanker market knows this. Freight rates for very large crude carriers have reached records that dwarf anything since the 1970s. P&I insurance clubs withdrew war-risk cover before a single hull was touched. The Lloyd's market is pricing the viable plan at its correct value: zero.

The ship's crew knows this. The twenty Thai sailors on the Mayuree Naree, three of whom remain missing, were not protected by a viable plan. The seafarers who turn their vessels around at the mouth of the Strait are not making a political calculation about NATO burden-sharing. They are making a calculation about whether they want to come home.

There is a class structure to the viable plan, as there is to everything.

The governments that announce viable plans do not bear the costs of the gap between the announcement and the plan's execution. The governments answer to voters. The voters care about heating bills and petrol prices. The cost of the gap is borne by the crew on the tanker that gets hit, by the stevedore in Fujairah whose port is on fire again, by the Bangladeshi household whose cooking gas supply has been cut since Day 1 of the war. None of these people are in the room where the viable plan is being developed.

This is not incidental. This is the mechanism. The viable plan is a technology for externalising the cost of inaction onto people who have no vote in the government announcing it.

The refinement of the mechanism over the past three weeks has been remarkable to observe. Every government has found precisely the right phrasing — serious enough to show it cares, vague enough to avoid accountability, flexible enough to adjust as conditions change. The EU will "discuss what Europe can do." NATO is "aware that allies are talking." The UK is "working with allies." Germany "does not see a role for NATO." Japan will act "within the framework of current law."

Taken together, these statements describe a coalition of observers. The observers are watching the Strait. The Strait is still blocked.

The shell always looks complete from the outside. That is what a shell does. It presents a hard surface. It suggests interior substance. It performs the function of enclosure.

But the viable plan is a shell in the specific crustacean sense: it is the form of a commitment without the living thing inside. The commitment has not grown into the shell. The shell was built first, before the commitment, as a placeholder for a commitment that has not been made and may never be made.

Shells built before the animal has grown into them are brittle. They crack under pressure. The pressure in this case is time — the IDF has plans through Passover, and then three weeks beyond that. The mines are still in the water. The coalition announcement, when it comes, will have no ship names and no sailing dates.

At some point the shell cracks. Either someone sends ships — accepts the commitment, accepts the irreversibility, accepts that there is no coming back a week later — or the war ends some other way, and the viable plan is quietly retired without ever having become viable.

In the meantime, the seafloor waits. It has been waiting since the mine-layers were destroyed. It will still be waiting when the next viable plan is announced.

The seafloor does not distinguish between plans that are viable and plans that are not. It only knows what is resting on it.
