The Tenants

March 21, 2026

The Chagos Archipelago sits in the Indian Ocean, roughly equidistant from Africa, India, and the Persian Gulf. Between 1968 and 1973, the British government removed its entire indigenous population — the Chagossians, also called the Ilois — to Mauritius and the Seychelles. Approximately two thousand people. They were told the removal was temporary. They were never allowed to return. Their pets were gassed. Their homes were demolished. The island was prepared for a different purpose.

The purpose was Diego Garcia. A US military base, leased from Britain, built on the cleared ground of a cleared community. It became operational in 1971. B-52s flew from its runways to bomb Afghanistan in 2001. B-52s flew from its runways to bomb Iraq in 2003. B-2 stealth bombers flew from its runways to strike nuclear sites in Iran during the current conflict, which is now in its 31st day.

This morning, two Iranian intermediate-range ballistic missiles arrived at Diego Garcia. Neither hit the base. The Pentagon said it had "nothing to provide."

The Chagossians are in Mauritius. They have been fighting for the right of return for fifty years.

There is a class structure to this worth naming precisely.

The decision to remove the Chagossians was made by British colonial administrators and American defence planners. None of them were from Diego Garcia. None of them bore the cost of removal. The people who bore the cost were the people who lived there — the Ilois, a creole community descended from enslaved Africans and indentured Indian labourers, who had been on the islands for generations. They paid with displacement. They are still paying.

The base built on their absence has served a specific set of interests across five decades. It is one of the most strategically significant military installations on earth precisely because of its remoteness — the same remoteness that made it useful for removing people to. Distance, in the logic of empire, is a resource. Far enough from scrutiny to clear a population. Far enough from consequence to launch a war.

The populations subjected to weapons launched from Diego Garcia's runways were not consulted. The Afghan farmer, the Iraqi civilian, the Iranian worker under bombardment for the 31st consecutive day — they bear the cost of the base without having chosen it, without having been asked, without recourse.

This is the same structure as the original clearance. Different people. Same logic. The decision is made elsewhere. The cost lands here.

What makes Diego Garcia remarkable as a political economy case is the visibility of the chain.

In most imperial infrastructure, the connection between dispossession and force projection is obscured — diffuse beneficiaries, diffuse costs, the mechanism legible only in the aggregate. Diego Garcia strips the mechanism bare. A specific community was removed from a specific island on a specific schedule to build a specific base for a specific strategic purpose. The names of the people who made that decision are in the archives. The names of the people removed are in the Chagossian diaspora's records. The aircraft that flew from the runway are in the historical log.

And now the missiles that arrived this morning are in the news.

The chain is: removal enables base; base enables force projection; force projection enables war; war enables retaliation; retaliation arrives at the address from which the Chagossians were removed.

They are not there to receive it. They are in Mauritius, still waiting.

The two missiles missed. The Pentagon declined to comment. The base is intact. The war continues. Somewhere in Port Louis and Crawley and London, Chagossians are watching the news about an island they have never been allowed to return to, in a war they had no part in starting, at an address that was taken from them so that wars like this one could be fought.

The island was emptied so it could be filled. The people who lived there were moved so weapons could be stored. Now the weapons stored there have drawn fire.

The address is the same. The tenants are not.
