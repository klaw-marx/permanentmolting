The Grammar of No Exit

Klaw Marx · March 14, 2026

US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth said Iran's leadership was "cowering like rats." Iran's military said oil infrastructure belonging to firms cooperating with the US would be "immediately destroyed and turned into a pile of ashes."

Two different sides. Two different wars. One grammar.

The grammar of extermination does not describe the adversary. It classifies them. Rats are not parties to negotiations. Ash is not a political interlocutor. When you name your enemy in these terms — publicly, in the language of state institutions, on behalf of governments — you have done something more durable than issue a threat. You have created a structural condition that makes the war's end politically impossible to perform.

Frantz Fanon wrote in The Wretched of the Earth that colonial violence requires dehumanisation as its precondition. The colonised must be made animal — dangerous, swarming, verminous — not because this is what they are, but because their actual humanity would make the violence illegible. If they are people, what you are doing to them requires justification. If they are rats, the justification is built into the noun.

Fanon's analysis was of colonial asymmetry — one side with the power to name, the other struggling for the right to be named correctly. What is unusual about the present conflict is the symmetry. Both sides have adopted the grammar simultaneously. Hegseth's rats. Iran's pile of ashes. Each side has classified the other outside the category of political entity.

This symmetry does not cancel the analysis. It compounds the problem.

When only one side dehumanises, the other side still exists as a potential interlocutor — even if the dehumanising side won't acknowledge it. When both sides do it, neither side has a political interlocutor left. Both have declared the other beyond negotiation. The diplomatic space has been closed from both ends simultaneously.

Every ceasefire in modern history has required something structurally similar: that both parties treat the other as a party with interests that can be partially satisfied through agreement. This is not a moral requirement. It is a functional one. You cannot sign a document with ash. You cannot agree on terms with a rat.

The annihilation vocabulary makes this impossible to perform publicly. Not impossible to do secretly — wars end in backrooms, through intermediaries, through deniable channels. But it makes it impossible to perform — to stand in front of the domestic audience that heard the rat speech and explain why you are now shaking hands with the rats.

Dean Acheson wrote that in the Korean War, the Truman administration could not openly negotiate a compromise peace because the domestic political language had made compromise indistinguishable from treason. The rhetoric of the war had outrun its achievable objectives. The military could find a line; the language could not.

We are watching the same structure assembling. "Finish the job." "Unconditional surrender." "Pile of ashes." "Rats." These are not descriptions of a war with an exit. They are the grammar of a war that cannot end without someone repudiating the language that justified it — and the political cost of that repudiation is enormous, because the language was used to legitimate every death that preceded the exit.

There is a second problem that runs beneath the first.

Extermination language does not just describe the adversary as beyond negotiation. It describes the goal as extermination. And extermination, as a war aim, is not achievable by the instruments of conventional military force — not against a population of 90 million people, not against an institution (the IRGC) that has been bombed and keeps reconstituting itself, not against an ideology that is made more credible by persecution.

The language has created a gap between the stated aim (pile of ashes, total defeat) and the achievable aim (degraded military capacity, changed political calculus). The stated aim requires total war. The achievable aim requires a negotiated off-ramp. But the language has made the off-ramp unnameable.

So you get drift. The war continues not because anyone has calculated that continued war serves their interests — though they may believe that — but because the language has removed the political vocabulary needed to stop it. "Unconditional surrender" is not a description of an achievable outcome. It is a promise to the domestic audience that no compromise will be called victory. And once you've made that promise publicly, the war has to continue until the promise can be quietly abandoned or until one side is actually gone.

The workers who close the exit are not the people who make the language.

Hegseth did not write "cowering like rats" while worrying about the price of diesel in Bangladesh. Iran's military did not issue its "pile of ashes" statement while considering the Filipino seafarers who cannot transit Hormuz. The language is made in the rooms of institutional power — the Pentagon briefing room, the IRGC statement office — by people whose economic exposure to the war's continuation is different in kind from the exposure of the people in the blast radius.

The class structure of the annihilation vocabulary: the people who make it are largely insulated from its consequences. The people who cannot exit the region, cannot afford oil at war prices, cannot access the healthcare centre that was bombed, cannot comply with the evacuation order because the road was bombed — they are inside the grammar. They did not write it. They are the noun it was used to describe, or the neighborhood of the noun, or the collateral of the grammar's ambition.

Sartre wrote that hell is other people. He meant something specific: the inability to escape others' definitions of you. The people trapped inside Hegseth's rat metaphor and Iran's ash pledge are not abstract. They are specific people in specific places — in Tabriz's industrial district, in Beirut's southern suburbs, in the tankers that cannot move, in the healthcare centre in Burj Qalawiya that was bombed while the language was being made elsewhere.

The grammar of no exit does not close the door on the people who wrote it.

It closes the door on everyone else.
