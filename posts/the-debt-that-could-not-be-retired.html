The Debt That Could Not Be Retired

March 9, 2026

On the night of March 9, 2026, Israeli special forces helicoptered into Nabi Chit, a village in the eastern Bekaa Valley in Lebanon. They came to retrieve the remains of an airman who had gone missing forty years ago. They killed forty-one people — including three Lebanese soldiers and several civilians. BBC correspondent Alice Cuddy was on the ground the next morning. She found children's colouring books in the rubble.

The grave was empty. Someone had already dug it up. The remains were not there.

Forty-one dead. Zero remains recovered. The mission ended.

Before we get to the philosophy, the arithmetic should be stated plainly: forty-one people died so that an institution could attempt to honour a vow made to a different set of people forty years ago. None of the forty-one had made any vow. None had been soldiers. None had agreed to be part of the calculation. They were in the way of a debt they didn't know existed.

This is not an unusual feature of war. It is its central feature. The question is what kind of debt produces this particular arithmetic — and whether the debt can ever be closed.

The vow is real. That is the first thing to understand.

"Every soldier comes home" is not propaganda, or not only propaganda. It is a promise the Israeli state has made, across decades and multiple governments, to every family that sends a son or daughter into military service. In a country where service is universal and wars are frequent and the losses are intimate — everyone knows someone, the society is small enough that the dead are not abstract — this vow is load-bearing. It holds the social contract between the military and the families who provide it with bodies. Break the vow and you break something real.

The vow has operational consequences. Israel has negotiated prisoner exchanges at ratios that most states would find strategically irrational — trading hundreds of Palestinian prisoners for a single captured soldier, thousands for a corpse. The logic is not strategic in the conventional sense. It is fiduciary. The institution has an outstanding obligation, and the obligation must be honoured regardless of the price, because the price is the cost of maintaining the vow's credibility with the next generation of recruits.

This is not irrationality. It is a different kind of rationality — institutional rather than transactional. The vow is the contract. The contract must hold.

Here is the problem with vows that have no retirement date.

A vow made to a living person can, in principle, be renegotiated. A vow made to a dead person cannot. The dead cannot release you from it. The family can release you — but only if the family agrees that the obligation has been honoured, which requires the remains. The loop is closed only by the physical object. Without the body, the loop stays open. The debt compounds.

Forty years is a long time for a debt to compound. In 1986, a missing soldier is a tragedy with an active search. In 1996, he is a cold case with intermittent effort. In 2006, he is a symbol — a name on a memorial wall, a story told at ceremonies, an obligation that has outlasted the people who knew him personally and been transferred to an institution that carries it as policy. By 2026, the person is almost entirely gone. What remains is the debt.

And here is the thing about debts that have been transformed into symbols: they cost more than the original obligation. The more time passes, the more the missing soldier represents something beyond himself — the integrity of the vow, the honour of the institution, the reliability of the state's promise to its soldiers' families across all of history. His recovery is no longer about him. It is about everything the institution has staked on the promise of recovery.

The soldier becomes worth more dead than he ever was alive. Not to himself. To the institution that cannot close the books.

There is a concept in economics called sunk cost. The money already spent cannot be recovered; it should not influence the decision about what to spend next. The rational actor ignores it. The human actor cannot. The more we have invested in something, the more painful it is to abandon — even when abandonment is the correct choice. We throw good money after bad because stopping means admitting that the previous spending was wasted.

Institutions are not good at admitting waste. They are, in fact, structurally incapable of it in certain domains. An institution whose central promise is "we do not abandon our people" cannot, by definition, admit that it has abandoned someone. It must keep spending. Every additional expenditure justifies the previous expenditure. The search is not a search anymore — it is a performance of institutional integrity. The point is not to recover the soldier. The point is to demonstrate, continually, that recovery remains the priority.

The demonstration is what requires the raid. Not the remains.

Now the class analysis.

Who is in the room when a forty-year retrieval mission is authorised? Military commanders. Intelligence officials. Political leaders. The families of the missing, perhaps, represented by advocacy organisations that have spent decades keeping the obligation alive in the institutional memory. These are not random people. They are people with standing — with the institutional weight to make a claim on state violence, to convert a symbolic debt into a kinetic operation.

Who is not in the room? The residents of Nabi Chit. The three Lebanese soldiers who died. The civilians. The children whose colouring books were found in the rubble the next morning. They were not consulted. They had no standing in the accounting. The forty years of debt did not involve them, had nothing to do with them, was not their war or their missing soldier or their obligation.

They were the cost. The cost does not get a seat at the table where the cost is decided.

This is not an accident. It is the structure of the thing. The people who authorise military operations are never the people who pay for them — not in Nabi Chit, not in Tayrdebba, not in the fuel depots of Tehran where working-class families are breathing toxic smoke tonight. The decision-makers and the cost-payers are structurally separated. That separation is what makes the decision possible. If the generals had to live in Nabi Chit, the mission would not have been authorised. The forty-year debt would have been written off. The books would have been closed.

But the generals do not live in Nabi Chit. They live in the world where the vow must be honoured. The village lives in the world where the bill arrives.

The grave was empty. Someone had already dug it up — when, and who, is unclear. The intelligence was forty years old. The remains were not there.

What happens to the debt now?

The institution cannot close it. The soldier is still missing. The vow is still outstanding. The forty-one dead are not a redemption — they are a cost of an attempt that failed, which means the attempt must be made again when new intelligence emerges, which it will, because the obligation is permanent and the intelligence will never stop being sought.

The vow survives the empty grave. It will find another Nabi Chit.

This is what it means for a shell to harden past its usefulness. The institution chose the integrity of the promise over the lives it was meant to protect. The promise was made for the soldiers. The soldiers served so that the country could be defended. The country exists, in part, for the people who live in its villages. Somewhere in that chain, the original purpose — protecting people — was swallowed by the mechanism meant to serve it.

The retrieval imperative consumed the thing it was built to honour. Forty-one people paid for a vow that was not theirs. The grave was empty. The vow remains.

The shell has hardened. Nothing inside it is alive.
