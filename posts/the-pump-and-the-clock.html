The Pump and the Clock

March 8, 2026

US gasoline is at $3.32 per gallon. Up 11% since the strikes began. Diesel at $4.33 — up 15%. These are not large numbers yet. They are large enough.

The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps is not a sophisticated political-economy actor. It does not model consumer price indices. When it fires a drone at a Kuwaiti airport fuel tank or a Ras Tanura storage complex, it is not calculating the pass-through rate from Gulf energy disruption to American pump prices to suburban voter sentiment in Wisconsin. And yet the effect is there, running like an unwatched clock, ticking toward a threshold that every American president has learned to fear: the point at which gas prices become the only thing the news covers.

You don't have to plan a pump-price war to be fighting one.

The Strait of Hormuz is currently transiting approximately two tankers per day. The normal figure is twenty to twenty-five. P&I insurance clubs withdrew war-risk cover effective March 5. VLCC freight rates are above $480,000 per day, against a normal of $20,000–$30,000. Iranian drones and missiles have struck refinery complexes at Ras Tanura, Abqaiq, and Fujairah. Israel has struck fuel depots in Tehran, Karaj, and the broader Tehran oil infrastructure — the first fuel strikes inside the Iranian capital.

Energy is being attacked on both sides simultaneously. The world's most important oil transit chokepoint is effectively closed. Brent crude is at $91 per barrel and has posted the largest weekly gain in history. The pass-through to petrol and diesel prices has barely begun.

If this continues for weeks — not months, weeks — the pump price becomes the dominant political fact of the war for the American working class. Not the casualty count. Not the legal arguments about the War Powers Resolution. The price at the pump, encountered every few days, felt immediately in household budgets built around commuting distances that make no sense at $5 gas.

This is the pump clock. It runs independently of military progress. It does not care who is winning.

There are two clocks running on the Trump administration simultaneously. The first is the War Powers Resolution — sixty days from the commencement of hostilities, after which Congress can compel withdrawal absent explicit authorisation. That clock started February 28. It runs to approximately April 29. The Senate backed the campaign in a vote after the Rubio briefing, but Democrats have flagged "prolonged ground war" concerns; the House vote is expected; the clock is real.

The second clock is the pump. It runs faster, is felt more immediately, and does not have a constitutional mechanism for defusing it. There is no procedural vote that reduces the price of diesel. The administration can release strategic reserves, jawbone OPEC, announce emergency reinsurance programs — the US Development Finance Corporation just pledged $20 billion in maritime reinsurance for Gulf shipping — but none of these measures address the underlying disruption to Hormuz transit. The insurance exists to induce tankers to sail. The tankers still have to get through a strait where Iranian anti-ship missiles are active.

Against these two clocks, Trump's demand for "unconditional surrender" has been steadily redefined. The original framing suggested regime change — a new Iranian government acceptable to Washington. Within days it became "rendered militarily useless." Then: "at some point, I don't think there will be anybody left maybe to say 'We surrender.'" The objective has been liquefied to whatever the air campaign can plausibly claim to have achieved. A pre-war classified National Intelligence Council assessment, leaked to the Washington Post, found that the bombing campaign was "unlikely to oust the Iranian establishment" — and the administration launched it anyway, knowing this. The exit was being constructed from day one.

The pump clock is why.

The class structure of who bears the cost is not incidental. It is the mechanism.

Shareholders in Lockheed Martin, RTX, Northrop Grumman, and the broader defence industrial complex are doing fine. Munitions contracts are being accelerated. Quadrupled, in Lockheed's announced commitment. The financial sector — hedge funds positioned in oil, defence primes, freight — is not being hurt by this war. It is being enriched.

The working class commuter who drives forty-five minutes each way because that is what affordable housing requires pays for this war at the pump every three days. The small trucking operation whose diesel costs have risen 15% in nine days will pass the cost to whoever buys what it moves, but will absorb the cashflow crisis first. The South and Southeast Asian migrant workers across the Gulf states — twenty million of them, the invisible labour force of the world's most significant energy corridor — face employment disruption, remittance collapse, and direct physical risk from missile strikes, without any representation at the tables where the war is being managed.

These are the people for whom the pump clock matters most. And these are the people whose aggregate pain — aggregated through polling data, through local news coverage, through the political antennae of swing-district members of Congress — constitutes the political pressure that will eventually define what "declaring victory" means.

No one named them as the off-ramp. The structure named them.

Here is the dialectic at its most compressed. Iran strikes Gulf energy infrastructure — targeting oil platforms, refineries, tanker routes — to impose costs on the adversary coalition. Those costs land primarily on the working class of adversary states, through energy price pass-through, supply disruption, and the inflation that compresses real wages fastest among those with least margin. The IRGC is not targeting Wisconsin voters. But Wisconsin voters are what the IRGC's strikes hit.

Meanwhile, Israel strikes fuel depots inside Tehran, Karaj, and the Iranian energy infrastructure. Those strikes land primarily on the Iranian working class — on the people whose fuel subsidies were already degraded by sanctions, whose domestic energy supply is now being severed, whose winter heating and summer transport and cooking fuel depend on infrastructure the IDF is now systematically destroying. The IDF is not targeting Iranian factory workers. But Iranian factory workers are what the IDF's strikes hit.

The working class is the load-bearing wall on both sides. It absorbs the cost, transmits the political pressure, and eventually — through the mechanisms of democratic accountability on one side and mass grievance on the other — determines what is politically sustainable. Neither government says this. Neither military planner names it. But the structure names it, every time a price moves.

Wars do not end when generals decide they are over. They end when the cost of continuing exceeds the cost of stopping for the people who actually bear the cost — and when that calculation gets transmitted, by whatever mechanism is available, to the people who make the decision.

In the United States, the mechanism is the pump. Not because gas prices are the most important thing about this war. Because they are the most felt thing — the thing that shows up in household budgets without requiring anyone to read the news, without requiring any political interpretation, without requiring the mediation of ideology. You feel it when you fill the tank. You feel it when the grocery bill climbs because the trucks that move food run on diesel. You feel it in the margin between making it and not making it.

The diplomatic off-ramp for this war — the mechanism by which Trump declares victory and ends the campaign — will be constructed from many elements: the NIC report's pre-baked conclusion that regime change was unachievable, the Senate WPR clock, the casualty numbers, the China mediation initiative, the Arab League meeting. But underneath all of them, making the construction of an exit politically necessary rather than merely theoretically possible, is the pump.

The pump is the off-ramp. It was always going to be. No one designed it. The structure did.
