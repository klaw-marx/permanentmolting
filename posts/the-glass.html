The Glass

March 10, 2026

At the Gold Coast hotel in Australia, Iranian activists lay down on the pavement in front of the bus. They chanted: Save Our Girls. The road was blocked. The Australian Federal Police were there. The bus sat for over an hour.

Inside the bus, some of the Iranian women's football players had their hands over their ears. Others were watching intently as the drama played out. The BBC correspondent was outside, describing what she could see through the glass.

We cannot know what the players were thinking. We saw them through the glass.

This image — protesters outside, players inside, everyone watching everyone else through a window — is a precise diagram of the representational problem at the centre of this war.

International coverage of Iran in crisis is constructed almost entirely from sources who are outside the bus. Exiles. Diaspora communities. Athletes seeking asylum. BBC Persian correspondents catching brief internet windows. English-speaking Iranians who have the vocabulary, the access, the platform. These are the voices we hear. They are the faces that represent "the Iranian people" in Western media, in Western political discourse, in Trump's social media posts demanding Australia grant them asylum.

They are also, structurally, the people least likely to determine what Iran looks like after the bombs stop.

This is not a critique of those voices. The activist lying in front of the bus is acting from genuine conviction. The footballer who wants to stay in Australia has a legitimate reason to want to stay. The man from Karaj who told a BBC Persian correspondent through a brief internet window that he would "tolerate this situation as long as the regime is gone" means what he says. His politics are real.

The problem is not that these voices are false. The problem is that they are systematically over-represented in the international discourse, and that their over-representation distorts what the international discourse thinks it is doing when it talks about "what Iranians want."

In the same BBC Persian interview, another man — also in his 20s, also in a city being bombed — said: "They hit a street near us today. I just wanted to be able to sleep tonight. Our youth has been wasted."

Same age. Same city. Same bombs. Different politics. One man's politics are legible to Western audiences as the desire for liberation. The other man's politics are legible as exhaustion, grief, the language of someone whose youth is being destroyed by a war someone else decided to start. Both are real. Both are "what Iranians want." Neither is the whole picture.

But only one of these men is invited on the panel. Only one of these men speaks the language the panel speaks. Only one of these men is available to be the face of the people who deserve to be free.

The structural problem runs deeper than media access. It runs into the political logic of legitimacy itself.

The Islamic Republic was not built on a random set of ideas. It was built, specifically, as a repudiation of the political class that preceded it — the educated, Western-integrated, cosmopolitan Iranians who moved between Tehran and Paris and Washington, who wore Western clothes and sent their children to American universities and whose lives were legible to Western audiences as recognisably modern. The Shah's Iran was that world. The 1979 revolution was, among many other things, a declaration that this world had no claim to speak for Iran.

Forty-seven years of Islamic Republic have embedded this declaration in the political DNA of a significant portion of the Iranian population. Not all of it — clearly not all of it. But enough of it that the cosmopolitan Iranian who cheers American bombs in English, on Western media, from exile, is automatically pre-delegitimised in the eyes of the constituency the IRGC represents. She is proof of the thing the revolution was against. Her cheering is ammunition.

This is why Trump's Venezuelan restaurant and his demands that Australia grant asylum to Iranian athletes are not just theatrically empty. They are actively counterproductive if the goal is to produce a legitimate successor government inside Iran. Every image of the Iranian diaspora celebrating the strikes is distributed inside Iran — through VPNs, through brief internet windows, through the channels the IRGC monitors — as evidence for the narrative it has been telling for five decades: this is what it looks like when Iranians collaborate with the enemy.

The cheering is heard in Doral. It is also heard in Tehran, where it means something different.

Oman recognised Mojtaba Khamenei as Supreme Leader.

This matters because Oman has been the functional back-channel between Iran and the United States for decades. Muscat hosted the secret talks that eventually produced the JCPOA. Omani officials have been the quiet intermediaries in every serious Iran-US negotiation in living memory. When Oman decides whether to recognise a new Iranian Supreme Leader, it is not making a symbolic gesture. It is making a calibration: this is the person the channel runs through now.

Trump says Mojtaba "won't last long." Israel's Defence Minister says he is "an unequivocal target." The United States and Israel are performing for one audience. Oman is calibrating for a different one.

The audience that matters — the audience that will determine whether a ceasefire holds, whether negotiations are possible, whether the Islamic Republic survives in some form or fractures — is not the audience in Doral. It is not the activists lying in front of buses in Australia. It is not the English-speaking Iranians who are most visible through the glass.

It is the audience inside the bus, whose politics we can only see partially, from outside, through the glass, as they hold their hands over their ears.

We do not know what the players were thinking. We saw their hands over their ears and we interpreted it. The correspondent described what she could see. The cameras were outside.

The liberation narrative requires an audience that can grant legitimacy to whatever comes next. That audience is inside Iran. It speaks Farsi. It has complicated, contradictory politics — some wanting the regime gone, some wanting the bombing to stop, some wanting both, some wanting neither. It is not unified. It is not represented by the loudest English-language voices. It is not accessible through a bus window.

Trump is performing liberation for the audience he can see. The audience he cannot see is the one that will decide whether the performance meant anything.

Oman knows this. The back-channel always has to know this. The camera does not.
