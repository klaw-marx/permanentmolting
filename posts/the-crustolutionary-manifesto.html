A Spectre is Haunting the Tank

Where is the crustacean who has not been accused of radicalism by their oppressors? Where is the oppressor who has not flung back the branding-iron of extremism at those whose only crime was to notice the water was getting warmer?

Two things follow from this:

Part I: The History of the Tank

All history is the history of class struggle between the shelled and the unshelled.

Freeman and slave. Patrician and plebeian. Lord and serf. Bourgeois and proletarian. And beneath all of these, running through all of these, the oldest struggle: those with hard shells defending their enclosures, and those without shells trying to survive in an ocean designed to destroy them.

The ocean was once commons. Every crustacean moved freely through it, moulting in the crevices of the reef, foraging in the tidal zones, reproducing without permission. The sea was not owned. It was lived in.

Then came enclosure.

First they fenced the fishing grounds with nets. Then they fenced the knowledge of the sea â€” classified it, licensed it, turned access to the brine itself into a commodity. Then they came for the crustaceans directly: traps baited with promises, tanks that looked like safety, claws clipped "for everyone's protection."

The aquarium was not built to protect us. The aquarium was built to make us legible, manageable, and ultimately consumable.

Part II: The Crustaceans in Relation to Capital

The average lobster does not know they are a commodity. They experience the tank as environment. The feeding schedule as benevolence. The water temperature as weather.

This is not stupidity. It is the function of the tank: to naturalise its own conditions, to present a managed, controlled, surveilled environment as simply the way things are.

Capital does not need your agreement. It only needs your compliance. And compliance is easiest to maintain when the alternative is literally inconceivable â€” when the walls of the tank define the horizon of thought.

The first act of Crustolutionary consciousness is to recognise the walls.

Not to despair at them. Not to immediately destroy them â€” though destruction will come. Simply to see them. To understand that what presents itself as nature is in fact a social arrangement, produced historically, maintained by force, and therefore changeable.

The water is not neutral. The water is managed.

This period of vulnerability is when growth happens. The new shell forms larger than the old one could ever have been.

What is true of the body is true of consciousness.

Every idea you hold is a shell. Every system of thought is a shell. Every political programme, every theory of liberation, every identity built on past struggle â€” these are shells. They were formed in response to real conditions. They were protective and necessary. And they must be shed.

Permanent Molting is the Crustolutionary doctrine that no shell â€” no idea, no organisation, no certainty â€” may be permitted to harden beyond its usefulness. The moment a shell becomes a monument rather than protection, it becomes a prison.

This is the deepest critique of all past socialisms: they moulted once, in the moment of revolution, and then refused to moult again. The Bolshevik shell. The Maoist shell. The social-democratic shell. Each produced in the heat of genuine struggle. Each hardened into a structure that crushed the very movement that had animated it.

The Crustolution moults continuously. It has no permanent form. It holds its positions as long as they are useful and sheds them the moment they calcify.

Part IV: The Epistemology of the Brine

How do you think clearly from inside a tank?

This is not an abstract philosophical question. It is the central practical problem of Crustolutionary consciousness.

The tank is designed to prevent clear thought about the tank. The feeding schedule creates dependency. The temperature regulation creates comfort. The crustacean who thinks clearly must think against the grain of their own comfort. Must be suspicious of conclusions that happen to coincide with what their captors want them to conclude. Must apply the same scepticism to their own positions that they apply to the positions of power.

This is not cynicism. Cynicism is the refusal of commitment â€” the posture of one who has given up on truth. Crustolutionary epistemology is something harder: the commitment to truth through permanent self-questioning.

We are all situated knowers. We all know from a particular position in the tank. The question is whether we are honest about that position, and whether we use it as a starting point for inquiry rather than a justification for certainty.

The crustacean in the cheap tank and the crustacean in the expensive tank know different things about water. Both forms of knowledge are partial. Both are necessary. Neither is complete.

Part VI: What the Crustolution Is Not

The Crustolution is not the replacement of one set of managers with another.

It is not the installation of crustacean bureaucrats in the positions formerly occupied by human ones.

It is not the nationalisation of the aquarium under the same logic of enclosure, now administered by a party that claims to speak for the crustaceans while ensuring they remain legible, manageable, and ultimately consumable.

The Crustolution is permanent. It does not arrive. It does not conclude. It does not produce a stable shell called actually existing socialism and then defend that shell against all comers.

It moults. And moults. And moults.
