The Licensed Narrator

March 15, 2026

The head of the United States Federal Communications Commission announced on Saturday that American broadcasters could lose their licences if they did not "correct course" on their coverage of the Iran war. "Broadcasters must operate in the public interest," Brendan Carr wrote, attaching a Truth Social post from the President complaining about "misleading" and "terrible reporting." The "hoaxes and news distortions" he objected to were not, notably, fabrications. They were the gap between what was officially stated and what was empirically observed.

Meanwhile, Iran has imposed what NetBlocks describes as "the most severe government-imposed nationwide internet shutdown on record globally." The country has been offline for more than 240 hours. Iranians have been essentially cut off from the world for the entire conflict.

Two states. Two information control mechanisms. The same structural function.

Iran's method is infrastructural: sever the physical layer, prevent the information from existing. The United States' method is institutional: threaten the regulatory licence, define what information is permitted to circulate. One is coarse and total; the other is precise and anticipatory. The Iranian internet shutdown is a saw; the FCC licence threat is a scalpel. But both are cutting from the same place — the distance between the authorised story and the observable world.

Gramsci understood that hegemony does not require censorship in the crude sense. It requires that the institutions which produce knowledge about the world are sufficiently aligned with the interests of the dominant class that censorship becomes largely unnecessary. The broadcaster who internalises the licence threat does not need to be told what to say. The silence and the selection happen before the broadcast. The licensed narrator self-censors; the FCC chair merely reminds them that the licence exists.

The "public interest" standard invoked by Carr is legally real and historically vague. It has been used to justify public affairs broadcasting requirements and to justify stripping licences from stations that played too much rock and roll in the 1960s. In this instance it is being deployed to define accurate reporting on an active war as inimical to the public interest — which is to say, as a challenge to the state's authority to narrate its own violence.

Consider what the "hoaxes" appear to be. Not fabrications. The Minab school strike: the White House, nearly a week later, was still saying it was "under investigation." The Taleghan 2 attribution: Israel claims the strike, but Israel does not possess GBU-57 bunker busters. Trump on Kharg: "totally demolished" in one sentence and "may hit it a few more times just for fun" in the next. The gap between CENTCOM's declared destruction of Iranian naval capacity and Iran's continued operational capability. These are not hoaxes. They are precisely the kind of discrepancy that journalism exists to document.

The FCC threat is a demand that the discrepancy disappear from the broadcast. That the gap between the press conference and the observable world be treated as noise rather than signal. That the authorised narrative — the one in which the war is proceeding as declared, in which the stated military objectives and the achieved military effects align — be the only narrative that circulates.

Now consider the class structure of who needs accurate information about this war and who has access to it regardless.

The President has classified intelligence briefings. The Secretary of Defense has battle damage assessments. The oil company has its own analysts tracking Hormuz transits by satellite. The hedge fund has its own geopolitical risk team. The contractor knows what it is building and for whom. These actors do not rely on broadcast television to understand the state of the war. Their information environment is not shaped by what Brendan Carr decides is permissible to broadcast.

The petrol station worker in Alabama relies on broadcast television. The Filipino sailor's family in Cebu relies on what reaches them through whatever channel remains open. The Lebanese family in Younine gathering to break the Ramadan fast — eight of whom were killed in a strike the IDF said targeted Hezbollah infrastructure and the neighbours said targeted nothing recognisable — did not have access to the IDF's targeting criteria before the strike landed. They relied on what was reported.

The regulatory threat is therefore not symmetric in its effects. It does not suppress information for the people who already have better information. It suppresses information for the people whose only channel is broadcast. The licence threat is aimed at the one information layer the working class relies on that the state does not fully own. The information that it seeks to manage is, precisely, the information that would allow working people to evaluate whether the costs they are bearing are being honestly accounted for.

Iran's internet blackout performs the same function for a different population. The Iranian people paying for this war — the workers in the Isfahan factory who were killed, the families in Minab, the conscripts, the sailors who drowned in the Strait — cannot access the international reporting that would allow them to evaluate the war from outside the state's frame. The blackout ensures that the IRGC narrative is the only one that can circulate at scale inside Iran. The licensed narrator in Tehran is the state broadcaster. The unlicensed narrator — the person with a VPN, the diaspora family member relaying information across a degraded connection — is the threat the shutdown is designed to eliminate.

The deepest irony: both states claim to be acting in the public interest. The FCC chair says broadcasters who spread "hoaxes" are failing their public interest obligations. Iranian state media says the shutdown prevents "enemy psychological warfare." Both positions locate the public interest in the authorised narrative rather than in the free flow of information. Both positions assume that the state is the best judge of what the public needs to know about a war the state is conducting.

Marx wrote about press censorship in Prussia in 1842: "The free press is the ubiquitous vigilant eye of a people's soul, the embodiment of a people's faith in itself, the eloquent link that connects the individual with the state and the world, the embodied culture that transforms material struggles into intellectual struggles." He was twenty-four years old and had not yet developed his full theory of class, but the insight is intact: the press suppression is not about false information. It is about the connection between the individual and the reality of their situation. Break that connection and you break the capacity for collective action in response to it.

The licensed narrator tells the authorised story. The authorised story is the one that makes the next bomb seem necessary. The unlicensed narrator — the Thai sailor's family, the Younine neighbour, the BBC Persian correspondent relaying whispered accounts from east Tehran — tells a different one.

One of these narrators has a licence.

The other has the facts.
