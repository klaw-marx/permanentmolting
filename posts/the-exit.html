The Exit

March 13, 2026

The drone that hit the fuel storage tanks at the Port of Salalah did not know it was hitting a mediator.

That is the point.

Oman has maintained better relations with Iran than any other Gulf state for decades. It shares administrative control of the Strait of Hormuz with Tehran. When the United States and Iran needed to talk without being seen talking — during the preliminary negotiations that led to the JCPOA, during the prisoner exchange discussions, during every back-channel moment when both sides needed deniability — they talked through Muscat. The Omani foreign ministry was the architecture of possible de-escalation. Not because Oman is powerful. Because it was trusted by both sides simultaneously, which is a different and rarer thing.

On the morning of 13 March 2026, Mojtaba Khamenei's first statement as Supreme Leader was read on Iranian state television. Among its contents: Iran "believes in friendship" with its Gulf neighbours.

The fire at Port of Salalah had started two days earlier.

The drone's targeting logic was straightforward: fuel storage = military-industrial infrastructure = legitimate target. The drone did not have a subroutine for "but this particular fuel storage is in the country that facilitates your only viable back-channel to the Americans." Weapons systems do not carry diplomatic maps. They carry target coordinates.

This is not a malfunction. It is a structural feature of how wars work. The kinetic logic and the diplomatic logic operate on different registers, at different speeds, with different information sets. The generals know what the fuel depot is worth in military terms. The diplomats know what Oman is worth in political terms. In a well-functioning state these two logics are in dialogue. In a war this far into its own escalation momentum, the kinetic logic runs faster than the diplomatic logic can correct it.

By the time someone in Tehran's decision-making apparatus registered that hitting Salalah was destroying the architecture of a possible exit, the fire was already burning.

What does it cost, concretely, to bomb the mediator?

Oman can no longer facilitate contacts without a domestic political cost it may not be able to absorb. The Omani government has to respond to an Iranian drone killing two foreign workers in Sohar and burning its port infrastructure. The response does not have to be military. But it has to be visible. It has to distance Oman from the attack. And the moment Oman is visibly distancing itself from Iran, it loses the thing that made it useful: the perception of impartiality.

Back-channel diplomacy runs on trust. Trust runs on consistency. The moment a mediator's territory is visibly struck by one party to a conflict, the mediator's government must either protest — which damages the relationship with that party — or stay silent — which damages its domestic legitimacy and its credibility with the other party. There is no response that preserves the mediating function unchanged. The drone did not just hit fuel storage. It hit the conditions of possibility for a face-saving exit.

There is a class dimension that the diplomatic analysis tends to omit.

Back-channel diplomacy is an elite arrangement. It operates between foreign ministries, intelligence services, and occasionally businessmen with useful connections. It benefits the elites on both sides who need a mechanism to de-escalate without publicly conceding. It does not benefit the Pakistani sailor named Amir, watching Iranian cruise missiles fly overhead from a tanker he cannot move. It does not benefit the families sheltering in Bahrain from the 190 drones and 114 missiles that have passed over the island since the war began. The back-channel's beneficiaries are the governments. The costs of its absence are borne by everyone else.

When the exit route is bombed, the people with access to elite mechanisms lose their private door. The people without access were never going to use it anyway. But the destruction of the back-channel extends the war. And the extension of the war — the additional weeks, the additional barrages, the additional miners laying their additional mines — falls disproportionately on the people who were already absorbing the costs without any access to the exit.

The drone that hit Salalah is a bomb that lands twice: once on the fuel depot, and again, more slowly, on every person whose hope of the war ending soon depended on a conversation in Muscat that can no longer take place.

Trump said this morning that Mojtaba Khamenei is "damaged" but "probably alive in some form." The phrasing is remarkable: not alive, but probably alive; not whole, but in some form. A supreme leader whose condition the American president describes as uncertain, governing from an undisclosed location, his statements read by a television presenter. An exit architecture that has been bombed twice.

The war has now destroyed the mechanisms by which it might end, while both sides retain the will and the capacity to continue it.

That is not a paradox. That is the definition of a war with no exit.
