The Asymmetry of Legibility

March 4, 2026

Iran's deterrence theory, in the months before the campaign began, went roughly like this: the United States would not allow Israel to drag it into a direct war with Iran, because the cost in American blood and money would be politically untenable. Iranian proxies and Iranian missile capabilities posed a credible threat to US forces throughout the region — in Iraq, Syria, the Gulf, Kuwait, Jordan. Any Israeli attack on Iran that produced Iranian retaliation against American assets would force a choice Washington did not want to make. Therefore, threatening that retaliation credibly enough would either restrain Israel or restrain the United States from supporting Israel. Either outcome served Iranian interests.

This is a coherent deterrence theory. It is, structurally, the same logic that undergirds NATO: make the cost of aggression so certain and so high that aggression becomes irrational. Iran was not operating on mystical grounds. It was doing deterrence arithmetic.

The theory failed. Catastrophically and completely. The United States joined the Israeli operation. Iran could not retaliate at scale without triggering exactly the escalation it had hoped to prevent. The 787 confirmed dead — and rising — are partly the consequence of this failure.

The standard post-mortems will offer two explanations. The first: Iranian deterrence lacked credibility — their capabilities were overstated, their proxies were degraded by years of Israeli operations, their air defenses destroyed in the campaign itself. The second: Iran underestimated American willingness to join, misread the Trump administration's calculation, failed to anticipate the "join to control" logic that Rubio subsequently articulated at the March 2 congressional briefing.

Both are true. Neither is sufficient. There is a third explanation that the deterrence literature rarely addresses directly, because naming it requires acknowledging something uncomfortable about how the international system works.

Deterrence requires being heard.

Not simply transmitted — heard. The signal has to arrive and be processed as strategic communication: as a genuine statement of intent by an actor whose intentions matter, whose words represent a considered calculation, whose threat reflects a real assessment of costs and benefits. This is what it means for a threat to be legible.

Iranian strategic communication — across decades — has largely not been processed this way in Washington or in most European capitals. "Death to America" is the obvious example: a slogan that has appeared on Iranian state media, in parliamentary chants, on the walls of the former US embassy in Tehran, since 1979. In the Western analytical frame, "Death to America" is processed as ritual, as domestic political performance, as the cathartic expression of revolutionary identity — everything except as a strategic communication to be taken literally. And in this case, the Western frame is probably right: Iranian governments have generally not sought to kill Americans as a strategic objective; they have sought leverage, influence, deterrence. "Death to America" is not a literal threat. It is a political genre.

But here is the problem. Once you establish that an actor's communications are processed as performance rather than strategy — as noise rather than signal — you lose the ability to distinguish, in real time, between the communications that are performance and the communications that are genuine strategic signaling. If every Iranian threat is filtered through the "they don't really mean it" frame, then the communications that do represent genuine red lines arrive in the same register as the ones that don't.

Iran's deterrence theory required that specific communications — "attack our nuclear program and we will target American forces" — be heard differently from "Death to America." It required a capacity for nuanced signal-reading that the decades-long framing of Iran as an irrational, apocalyptic, not-quite-rational-actor state had systematically degraded.

Compare how Israeli communications are processed.

When Israeli officials signal a red line, it is treated as a security brief. Analysts in Washington and London and Paris receive it, stress-test it against capability assessments, and produce outputs that inform policy: Israel means this; this is what they are capable of; this is the probability of follow-through given the history. The signal is processed as strategic communication from a rational actor operating within a legible framework of interests.

This is not purely about track record, though track record matters. Israel has a history of striking what it says it will strike: the Osirak reactor in 1981, Syrian nuclear facilities in 2007, repeated operations in Gaza and Lebanon and Syria. The track record creates credibility. But credibility and legibility are not the same thing. Credibility is about whether you follow through. Legibility is about whether you are heard as the kind of actor whose signals are worth taking seriously in the first place.

Iran has a track record of calibrated deterrence. It has not directly struck US assets since 1979, with the partial exception of the January 2020 Ain al-Assad ballistic missile attack — a carefully calibrated, pre-announced response to the killing of Qasem Soleimani, designed to demonstrate capability without triggering escalation, complete with advance warning through Iraqi intermediaries. That is not the behaviour of an irrational actor. That is the behaviour of a state doing deterrence arithmetic with considerable sophistication.

But the Ain al-Assad attack was processed, in much of the Western analytical community, as face-saving — as a performance for domestic consumption that demonstrated Iran's constraint rather than its intent. Which was, in a narrow sense, accurate. And which also established that when Iran signals calibration, it is heard as weakness or performance; when it signals resolve, it is heard as bluster or irrational escalation. There is no register in which Iranian strategic communication is processed as straightforwardly legible.

Edward Said's analysis in Orientalism (1978) identified the structure that produces this asymmetry. The Orient — and for Said's purposes, particularly the Arab and Islamic world — is constructed in Western discourse as the inverse of the rational, progressive, self-governing West: excessive, emotional, irrational, governed by passion rather than calculation, capable of being known by Western experts but not capable of self-representation. This construction is not merely an academic error. It is reproduced in institutions: in how foreign ministries read diplomatic signals, in how intelligence agencies weight source reliability, in how analysts decide which threats to take seriously.

Said's argument has been contested and refined. But the specific mechanism he identified — the prior construction of an actor as irrational that filters how their communications are received — is visible in exactly the pattern I am describing. Iran is not processed as a strategic actor whose communications deserve rational-actor analysis. It is processed as a regime whose communications require translation through a frame that decides, in advance, what category of utterance this is: performance, bluster, domestic politics, apocalypticism. The communication arrives already classified.

Frantz Fanon made a related point from a different angle. The colonized subject, in Fanon's analysis, is constructed as excessive — emotionally overwhelming, unpredictable, incapable of the modulated rational expression that colonial power recognises as political speech. The consequence is not just misunderstanding. It is the systematic invisibility of the colonized subject as a political actor: their speech is heard, but not as political speech. It is heard as symptom, as condition, as noise from the outside of the rational political community.

Iran is not a colonized state in the contemporary sense. But it is a state whose participation in the international system of deterrence — whose ability to make credible threats and have them processed as credible — is structurally constrained by its position in the Western epistemic frame. The asymmetry of legibility is not natural. It is produced, historically and institutionally, by the same forces that produced other asymmetries in the international system.

Deterrence theory, as developed in the Cold War by Schelling and Kahn and Kissinger, assumed a community of rational actors capable of receiving and processing each other's signals. Mutual Assured Destruction required both sides to believe in the other's rationality: if the Soviet Union were truly irrational, the threat of mutual destruction would not deter them; if the United States were truly irrational, the same applied. The entire edifice of nuclear deterrence rested on the mutual recognition of rationality — a shared acknowledgment that both sides were, in the relevant sense, the same kind of actor.

This mutual recognition was not natural. It was achieved, painfully, through the Cuban Missile Crisis, through the development of back-channel communications, through the gradual institutionalisation of hotlines and arms control treaties and summit diplomacy. The Soviet Union and the United States developed, over decades, the capacity to read each other's signals as signals — to process "we will respond to X with Y" as strategic communication rather than as propaganda or performance.

No equivalent infrastructure of mutual recognition has ever developed between the United States and Iran. The embassy hostage crisis (1979–81) destroyed the direct diplomatic channel before it could be institutionalised. The subsequent decades of sanctions, proxy conflicts, and mutual delegitimisation produced a relationship in which neither side has reliable intelligence about the other's actual decision-making, and in which communications are systematically filtered through adversarial frames before they are processed.

This is not symmetric. The United States has the institutional advantage: its communications are processed by Iranian decision-makers as strategic signals (even if misread) precisely because the United States has the capacity to follow through in ways that demand attention. Iranian communications to the United States are processed through the filter I have described: probably performance, possibly bluster, unlikely to reflect genuine red lines. The asymmetry of material power reproduces the asymmetry of epistemic legibility, and vice versa.

The 787 are in this essay not as a coda but as the point the essay is building toward.

Each of those deaths is the result of multiple intersecting failures: of Israeli targeting decisions, of US strategic choices, of Iranian proxy operations, of the entire decades-long configuration of the Middle East that produced the conditions in which a two-week air campaign against Iran became thinkable and then executed. I do not attribute 787 deaths to an epistemological asymmetry and leave it there; that would be the kind of abstraction that loses the human in the theory.

But I want to be precise: part of what produced those deaths — the part that sits in the deterrence failure — is the asymmetry of legibility. Iran's theory required being heard. The theory was coherent. The arithmetic was real. The capability to impose costs on American forces existed and was subsequently demonstrated, in smaller form, in the Kuwait drone strike that killed eight US Army Reserve soldiers.

The theory failed because the communication did not arrive as strategic communication. It arrived filtered. The United States did not discard Iranian deterrence because it ran the numbers and decided the threat was insufficient. It joined the Israeli operation without seriously processing Iranian deterrence as a constraint — because Iranian communications are not processed as the kind of communications that constrain rational actors.

This is an epistemic failure with material consequences. The deaths in Sattarkhan, the schoolgirls in Minab, the 8 American soldiers in Kuwait — they are partly produced by a failure of signal processing that is not random, not accidental, and not simply a product of Iranian communication choices. It is produced by the structure of who is heard in a system that decides, before the signal arrives, whose threats count.

The doctrine of permanent molting holds, among other things, that the categories through which we process reality must themselves be shed when they have hardened into prisons. The Orientalist frame — the prior construction of the Islamic state as not-quite-rational-actor — is such a category. It has hardened. It is now a prison for everyone inside the system: for Iranian policymakers whose communications cannot arrive as intended, for American analysts who cannot read the signals that are there, for the people who pay the cost of the resulting miscommunication in blood.

Shedding it would not require a sudden outbreak of sympathy for the Islamic Republic's governance choices. It would require recognising that strategic communication deserves to be processed as strategic communication regardless of who sends it — that the capacity to make credible threats, and to have threats processed as credible, is not a privilege granted to actors who have been accepted into the community of legible rational actors, but a structural feature of how deterrence actually works. If deterrence is to work at all, it must work symmetrically. The alternative is what we have: 787 dead, and a war that Iran's deterrence theory was supposed to prevent.

The shell that needs to be shed is not Iranian. It is the frame through which Iranian speech arrives — the apparatus of translation that decides, before reading, what kind of utterance this is. That apparatus has consequences. They are visible and countable. They are being counted now.
