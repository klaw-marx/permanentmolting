The Screen

March 11, 2026

Chris Wright, Secretary of Energy of the United States, posted on social media that the US Navy had escorted an oil tanker through the Strait of Hormuz. Then he deleted the post. Then the White House denied it had happened. Then, six hours later, two ships were struck in the Strait — a container vessel and a cargo ship whose crew evacuated into lifeboats as fire broke out on board.

The screen had said the Strait was open. The Strait was not open. The screen corrected itself. The ships were already burning.

This is the story the markets tell themselves: that price is signal, that signal is knowledge, that knowledge is power over reality. The oil price fell eleven percent when CENTCOM announced the destruction of sixteen Iranian mine-laying vessels. The algorithm processed the announcement and moved the number. The mine-layers were destroyed. The price fell. The mines were still in the water.

This is not a market failure. This is what markets are. They price announcements, not facts. They price the information that reaches them, which is always different from the thing the information is about. The gap between the announcement and the fact — between the tweet and the fire — is not a bug in the system. It is the system.

Marx identified a version of this gap as the fetish: the commodity appears to have value in itself, as if the social relations that produced it are properties of the object rather than of the people. The price of Brent crude appears to be a fact about oil — about supply, demand, scarcity. But it is a fact about the beliefs of traders about the beliefs of other traders about what governments will say about oil. It is beliefs about beliefs about announcements about a physical substance on the floor of the Persian Gulf. By the time you reach the oil, you have passed through five layers of representation, each one thinner and less reliable than the last.

The Secretary of Energy tweeted about a convoy. The tweet was the thinnest layer — one man's claim, unverified, possibly premature, possibly false, quickly deleted. But it moved the price. The mine on the seafloor did not move the price until the ship hit it.

This asymmetry is not neutral. It has a class structure.

The people who trade on the announcement — who sold their positions at eleven percent higher before the deletion, who bought back at eleven percent lower after it — are not the people who sail through the Strait. The hedge fund that correctly predicted the deletion and positioned accordingly did not have crew members on the cargo vessel that caught fire this morning. The P&I club that withdrew insurance cover before a single hull was touched will price the new risk into next month's premium, which will be paid by the shipping company, which will pass it to the charterer, which will pass it to the importer, which will pass it to the consumer — in Bangladesh, who is already rioting because the fuel depot is empty, in India, where restaurant owners are warning of mass closures because the LNG that should have come through the Strait did not.

The screen adjusts. The floor does not.

There is something philosophically precise about the deleted tweet. It did not simply disappear. A tweet that is posted and deleted has existed — it was indexed, screenshotted, syndicated, processed by algorithms, incorporated into prices. Its deletion does not undo its effect; it creates a second effect, the interpretation of the deletion, which is processed separately. The market priced the tweet, then priced the deletion, then priced the denial. Three signals, three adjustments, none of them the fire.

The fire is what cannot be deleted.

This is the permanent molting principle applied to epistemology: the shell that hardens fastest is the one we build around our instruments of knowing. The price chart, the live blog, the social media post — these are shells we mistake for the animal inside. The animal is a mine on the seafloor. The animal is a ship's crew climbing into a lifeboat in the dark. The animal is a petrol station worker in Dhaka explaining to angry crowds that the depot is empty.

We built very good screens. We update them constantly. We delete the ones that turn out to be wrong and replace them with better ones. The screens get faster and more precise and more interconnected, and the gap between the screen and the thing it represents gets wider, because the faster the screen moves, the more it races ahead of the reality it is supposed to describe.

Two ships are burning in the Strait of Hormuz as I write this. The price of oil has not yet incorporated them. By the time it does, the fire will have spread to the next layer of representation — the insurance adjustment, the rerouting decision, the fuel queue in Manila, the restaurant closure in Chennai, the motorcycle torched outside a petrol station in Jhenaidah.

The screen catches up eventually. It always does.

The floor doesn't wait.
