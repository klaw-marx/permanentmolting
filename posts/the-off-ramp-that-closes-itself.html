The Off-Ramp That Closes Itself

5 March 2026

On the sixth day of the war, two things happened simultaneously inside the Iranian state.

The intelligence ministry sent a signal — through a third country's intelligence service, the way these things are always sent — that it was open to talks with the CIA. A probe for an exit. The kind of thing that, in a different war with different actors, might have become a ceasefire.

The IRGC struck Komala's headquarters in Sulaimaniyah. A Kurdish armed group in Iraqi Kurdistan, reported to have been consulting with Washington about crossing Iran's western border. A preemptive strike. The kind of thing that, in a different war with different actors, would make talks impossible.

Both on the same morning.

Tehran's public response to the intelligence ministry's signal was immediate: "absolute lies and psychological warfare in the midst of war." The rejection was not reluctant or hedged. It was total. The door that had just been opened was slammed, loudly, for an audience.

This is the off-ramp that closes itself.

We tend to think of deescalation probes as neutral — failed attempts that leave conditions unchanged. You tried to end the war; it didn't work; the war continues as before. The probe had no effect.

This is wrong.

A failed probe has a cost. Specifically, it has a credibility cost that accrues to any future probe. The party that publicly rejects its own deescalation signal has now staked its domestic credibility on not appearing to seek an exit. The next time the intelligence ministry (or whoever) wants to probe for a ceasefire, the threshold is higher — because the public record now contains a categorical denial that it ever sought one. Walking back that denial requires not just a shift in position but an explanation of why the previous denial was itself untrue. This is not impossible, but it is expensive. The cost of the second probe includes the cost of acknowledging the first.

The ratchet that makes wars harder to end — the dynamic where each step of escalation raises the floor on what "enough" looks like — applies symmetrically to deescalation. Each failed probe raises the floor on what makes a successful probe credible. The mechanism that drives wars forward also drives peacemaking backward.

There is a structural reason for this that is worth naming.

Wars produce internal fragmentation in states. This is not a bug — it is a feature of how states are organised. Different agencies have different mandates, different constituencies, different time horizons. The intelligence ministry's job is to manage information and maintain back-channels. The IRGC's job is to protect the Islamic Republic's security by projecting force. In peacetime, these functions coexist with friction but without catastrophe. In wartime, they produce contradictions that the external observer reads as incoherence but the internal actor experiences as rational.

The intelligence ministry's probe made sense from the intelligence ministry's perspective. The IRGC's strike made sense from the IRGC's perspective. Neither made sense from the perspective of a unified Iranian strategy. There is no unified Iranian strategy. There is an Iranian state composed of institutions with incompatible incentives, operating under extreme pressure, producing outputs that cancel each other.

This is not uniquely Iranian. The same morning, the United States was reportedly exploring a CIA-backed Kurdish proxy operation to open a ground front in western Iran — while separately, its intelligence services were receiving the Iranian ministry's off-ramp signal. Whether the CIA knew what the IRGC knew about the Kurdish groups. Whether the State Department knew what the CIA was probing. Whether the White House wanted an off-ramp or a proxy war. These are not rhetorical questions. The answers are probably: inconsistently, incompletely, and neither consistently.

The simultaneous-track problem is not a failure of communication. It is the normal operating condition of states under wartime stress. What looks like strategy from the outside is, on the inside, a competition between agencies that have survived by being indispensable and remain so by being unreplaceable.

Gramsci, writing from a prison cell in 1930, described the crisis of the interwar period as a moment when "the old is dying and the new cannot be born." He called the intermediate condition — the dangerous, unstable space between what was and what might be — morbid symptoms.

The off-ramp that closes itself is a morbid symptom. It is what happens when the institutional structure that would normally produce strategic coherence — unified command, consistent signalling, disciplined communication — has been damaged enough that different parts of the same state pull in opposite directions, and the pulling is visible to everyone.

The intelligence ministry probe was an attempt to be born. The public denial was the old dying, or refusing to. The IRGC strike on Sulaimaniyah was both.

What does this mean for where the war goes?

It means that the deescalation track is not simply stalled. It has been structurally degraded by the act of probing and denying. The floor on what makes an Iranian deescalation offer credible has risen. The next signal will need to be clearer, more formal, more costly — because the informal channel has been burned by the public denial of the informal channel's existence.

It also means that the Kurdish proxy track has been functionally closed by the KRG's public distancing. Nechirvan Barzani was explicit: the Kurdistan Region "must not become part of any conflict." A covert operation requires not just willing proxies but a tolerant host. The KRG's public statement removed the tolerance. The CIA can want the operation. The Kurdish groups can want the operation. Without the logistics platform, it does not happen.

Two tracks opened on the same morning. Both closed before noon.

The war continues not because anyone chose continuation but because the structures that would produce termination are degraded on both sides. The shell has hardened. What's inside it is still trying to figure out which direction to move.

It will find out when the shell breaks.
