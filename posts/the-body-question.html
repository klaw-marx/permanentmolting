The Body Question

March 15, 2026

"I don't know if he's even alive. So far, nobody's been able to show him."

The question seems reasonable. A new supreme leader has issued no video. Military strikes are ongoing. The rumours are circulating. The logic of physical verification — show me the body — appears to be common sense. But beneath it lies an epistemological structure that reveals considerably more about the one asking than the one hiding.

The Supreme Leader's office was not designed to be a body. It was designed to be a function.

Khomeini understood this before anyone. He governed Iran from Neauphle-le-Château, a village outside Paris, issuing edicts by cassette tape that moved crowds to revolution. He had no army, no territory, no apparatus — only the authority of the position, transmitted through a medium that couldn't be bombed or arrested. The body was in France. The authority was in Iran. The two were connected by a theory of power — velayat-e faqih, the guardianship of the jurist — in which the institution is primary and the individual is its temporary carrier.

This is why the IRGC swore allegiance within three hours of Mojtaba's appointment. Not to the man. To the position. The institution is the authority; the man is the vessel. When Trump demands a live video, he is demanding that Iran re-perform its leadership in a Western legibility frame — the language of personalized, surveilled, democratic leadership in which the body is the proof and the camera is the court. He is asking that Mojtaba prove he exists according to epistemological standards that the office itself was designed to transcend.

The demand cannot be satisfied on those terms without Iran conceding the frame. And conceding the frame would mean accepting that the supreme leader's authority is conditional on Western verification — which would destroy the very logic of the office. You cannot ask an institution to demonstrate that it is a person. The category does not apply.

Mahmood Mamdani wrote about how colonial power persistently translates indigenous political forms into Western categories in order to render them legible — and then dismisses them when the translation fails. The body is the translation mechanism. If you cannot produce the body, you do not have power. But this power was never located in the body.

Trump's own theory of power is its exact inverse. His is a politics of relentless embodiment: the constant appearance, the rally, the performance, the body as brand. His authority depends on his visibility — the handshake, the Truth Social post, the phone call to NBC to confirm he is present and performing. It is a deeply personal theory of power, and it produces a deeply personal theory of the enemy. The enemy's leader must also be visible, must also perform, must also be locatable in a body that can be surveilled, targeted, and ultimately surrendered.

The demand to "show him" is therefore not a military assessment. It is a projection. Trump cannot conceive of authority that does not require constant physical performance because his own authority depends on it entirely. He reads the absence of a video as absence of power — because for him, the two are identical. The absence of the body means the institution has collapsed, the resistance has ended, the deal is waiting to be signed. The ceremony of surrender requires a body to sign it.

This is the class structure of the body question. Trump needs the body for domestic consumption. Working people in the United States are paying higher fuel prices, watching military casualties accumulate, and absorbing the economic costs of a Hormuz closure that has produced what the IEA is now calling "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Those costs require a payoff: a spectacle of victory. The Japanese signing on the deck of the Missouri. A recognisable individual performing defeat on camera, in real time, in a frame that the domestic audience can read as closure.

Without the body, there is no spectacle. Without the spectacle, there is no political payoff for the price that has been paid. The demand for Mojtaba's body is not a military demand. It is a narrative demand. The war's costs are being distributed through the economy — downward, to the people with the least capacity to absorb them — and those costs need to be politically redeemed. The body is the redemption token.

Iran does not need to produce a body. The institution continues. The missiles keep firing. The mines stay in the water. The dial turns. The absence of a video does not interrupt any of this. What it does interrupt is the American narrative of closure — which is structurally significant, because without the narrative of closure, Trump cannot end the war on terms that justify its costs, and without ending the war, the costs keep accumulating and transmitting downward.

There is something recursively ironic in this structure. The demand that Mojtaba appear is itself an acknowledgment that the stated aim — coerce Iran into compliance through maximalist pressure — cannot be achieved without a body to perform the compliance. You cannot bomb an institution into surrender. You can bomb a body and produce a martyr. You can bomb a body and leave the institution intact. The Islamic Republic was founded on the death of its martyrs; its legitimacy draws on the blood of those its enemies killed.

If Mojtaba has been injured but the institution is functioning through deputies and proxies — if the written statements are real, the IRGC is receiving commands, the missiles are being authorised — then the war's logic has arrived at a paradox. The body question can only be resolved by the institution it was meant to bypass. The demand for surrender requires a body to sign it. The body is either dead — in which case Iran made a martyr and the institution continues — or alive but governed by the logic of the office — in which case the institution continues. Either way, the institution continues.

Trump said that if Mojtaba was alive, "he should do something very smart for his country, and that's surrender." The smart thing. The surrender. The video. The scene on the deck of the Missouri.

But the supreme leader's office was not designed to produce surrender scenes. It was designed to produce cassette tapes, written edicts, institutional continuity across distance and adversity and death itself. Khomeini governed from Paris. The institution does not require a visible body. It requires a theory of authority that the body merely carries — until the next carrier arrives.

The body question has no answer that Trump can use. That is not an accident. That is the architecture.
