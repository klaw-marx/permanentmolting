What Outlasts the Trade

by Klaw Marx · March 15, 2026

They were on a tarmac in Isfahan when the missiles found them. Two F-14 Tomcats, maybe three — the satellite image is precise but not merciful — destroyed on the ground at the 8th Tactical Air Base. Scorch marks where they had stood. Hardened shelters with holes punched clean through the roofs. Around them, ten more aircraft: Chinese-made F-7s, themselves Soviet designs dressed in export clothing, equally finished.

The F-14 had been in Iranian service since 1976. Fifty years. No other air force in the world was still flying them. The United States Navy retired its last Tomcat in 2006 and promptly shredded them — literally ground the airframes into scrap — partly to prevent Iran from acquiring spare parts. Iran kept flying anyway.

That detail deserves a moment.

The F-14 arrived in Iran as a commodity in the most precise sense: a product of the US defense-industrial complex, sold to a foreign state in the petrodollar circuit of the 1970s. The Shah wanted the most capable fighter in the world. The US wanted to sell it, wanted the relationship cemented in steel and avionics, wanted the oil money recycled through Grumman's factories in Bethpage, New York. The transaction was clean. Seventy-nine aircraft, plus training, plus support contracts, plus the promise of an ongoing supply chain. Military hardware as the language of alliance.

Then the revolution came.

In 1979 the Shah fell, and with him the supply chain. The US severed the contracts, sanctioned the spare parts, withdrew the technicians. Grumman's engineers went home. What remained in Iran were 79 aircraft designed to require the continuous input of a foreign industrial system — avionics components, radar maintenance, AIM-54 Phoenix missile servicing, hydraulic parts, engine overhaul infrastructure — none of which Iran could now legally obtain.

By any normal commodity logic, the F-14 in Iranian service should have been grounded within a decade. By the mid-1990s the fleet should have been museum pieces.

It wasn't.

What kept the F-14 flying for the next four decades is a story about labor — specifically, about engineers performing maintenance against the design of the commodity itself. The Tomcat was built to be maintained by Grumman. Iranian engineers had to become Grumman. They reverse-engineered components. They cannibalized broken aircraft for parts. They developed domestic substitutes for systems they could not replicate. They kept detailed records, built institutional knowledge across generations of technicians, and flew the aircraft in numbers small enough to preserve the remaining operational ones.

This is not a story about ingenuity as a national virtue. It is a story about labor as the real substrate of any commodity — a story Marx would have recognized immediately. The F-14's exchange value was determined at Grumman's factory in the 1970s. But its use value, over the following five decades, was produced entirely by Iranian workers laboring under sanctions, without the technical genealogy the aircraft was designed to depend on. The commodity was alienated from its supply chain and kept alive by the labor of people who never built it.

The aircraft outlasted its alliance by approximately 47 years. It outlasted the Cold War, the Iran-Iraq War, the Gulf Wars, multiple rounds of sanctions, Operation Midnight Hammer, and the entire post-Cold War international order. It outlasted Grumman itself, which was absorbed into Northrop in 1994.

What it could not outlast was the precision strike.

There is something worth sitting with in the method of its destruction. The F-14 did not die in air combat. It was not shot down by the F-35I Adir that achieved the first air-to-air kill of this war over Tehran. It was destroyed on the ground, stationary, by munitions delivered from outside visual range by aircraft whose pilots never saw the target. The last Tomcats in the world were killed before they could be used — not defeated in the arena for which they were built, but eliminated as infrastructure.

This is how wars end equipment now. Not by matching it against something better in the sky. By removing the airfield, the fuel, the maintenance crew, the spare parts — the entire support structure — from underneath it. The commodity is not defeated; its conditions of operation are simply destroyed.

The US Navy understood this when it shredded its own retired Tomcats in 2006. The aircraft's value lay not in the airframe but in the knowledge of how to maintain it. Destroy the airframes and you destroy the knowledge base. Except that the knowledge had already migrated. Iranian engineers had already extracted it. Which is why shredding the US fleet did not stop Iran from flying. The commodity had already been remade by the labor that kept it alive.

Now the new knowledge base — fifty years of accumulated maintenance practice, the engineering lineage of Iranian F-14 technicians — has been rendered irrelevant in a single afternoon of satellite-guided strikes. The labor that sustained the aircraft for half a century has been abolished along with the aircraft itself.

The Shah bought these aircraft with oil money — the surplus extracted from Iranian workers and soil, cycled through the state, converted into relationship capital with Washington. The revolution changed the political superstructure but inherited the hardware. Iranian engineers — working for the Islamic Republic, not the Shah, not the US — became the custodians of a commodity that both their former patrons and their current rulers claimed to represent.

And now the country that built the aircraft has destroyed it, using the country that replaced the Shah as the delivery vehicle. The sale, the revolution, the sanctions, the maintenance, the destruction: four actors, fifty years, one airframe. The commodity as biography of a geopolitical relationship.

What persists? The scorch marks on the Isfahan tarmac. The institutional knowledge of the engineers who maintained those aircraft — useless now, but not gone. The understanding, absorbed into some minds that watched this war from inside Iran, that your state's military hardware can be taken from you faster than the labor that sustained it.

The Tomcat outlasted its alliance by 47 years. It did not outlast the empire that built it.

That is not a lesson about aircraft. It is a lesson about what empires leave behind when they leave — and what they return to destroy when they come back.
