The Balcony and the Missile

March 12, 2026

When it was announced that the Supreme Leader had been killed, a woman in Karaj said her neighbours came out onto their balconies and celebrated. Not in the streets — on the balconies. The street belongs to the state. The balcony is the threshold: just public enough to be seen by each other, just private enough to deny it later.

Then the paramilitaries returned. And everyone fell silent.

That is the whole structure of the Islamic Republic in one moment. The joy was real. The silence that followed was not the absence of joy. It was the presence of force.

Thirteen days later, Iran's new Supreme Leader issued his first statement. Mojtaba Khamenei — son of the killed man, husband of a killed woman, child of a killed mother — did not appear on state television. His message was read aloud by a presenter. He learned he had become Supreme Leader at the same time as the public: through the television.

Six minutes before his words were read, Iran launched a missile barrage toward Israel.

The sequence is not incidental. The regime could not show its leader — he is injured, or hidden, or both, in a location that cannot be disclosed because disclosing it would mark it for a strike. So it showed its weapons instead. The missile is the inaugural address. The barrage is the face of the state when the face of the state cannot be shown.

This is not a weakness. Or rather: it is a weakness that has been converted into a form of power. The weapons say: we are still here. The fact that you can't see our leader says: we are still capable of concealment. The combination says: you have not broken us.

Gramsci distinguished between domination and hegemony. Domination is rule by force. Hegemony is rule by consent — by the production of a worldview so naturalised that alternatives become unthinkable. The Islamic Republic has never been purely hegemonic; it has always required the IRGC on the streets. But at its best moments, it governed through a combination: enough consent to make domination cheaper, enough domination to suppress the consent that wasn't there.

Bombing changes the ratio. You cannot build hegemony under bombardment. You can only hold the streets.

The IRGC has been sending mass text messages to Iranians since the first day of the war: "any movement that disrupts security will be regarded as direct cooperation with the enemy." The message doesn't ask for your agreement. It doesn't need it. It only needs your paralysis. Domination, in its purest form, does not care what you think. It only cares what you do.

The woman on the balcony, celebrating with her neighbours, quickly falling silent when the paramilitaries returned — she is living inside this logic. Her joy is real. Her silence is coerced. The regime has not changed her mind. It has only controlled her body.

Trump and Netanyahu have repeatedly urged Iranians to "rise up" against the regime. The logic is: the people hate the government, the bombs weaken the government, therefore the people will act.

The woman from Karaj provides the counter-evidence from inside. Her assessment, offered at the end of a conversation about checkpoints and anxiety medication and her cat hiding in a closet, is this: "The biggest fear is that after enduring all of this suffering, the clerics will still remain in power and the Islamic Republic will still be ruling."

She is not afraid of the bombs. She is afraid of what comes after the bombs. She is afraid that all of this — the displacement, the internet blackouts, the petrol queues, the sniffer dogs in the rubble — will end, and the regime will still be there. That the suffering will have been paid and the change will not have come.

This fear is not irrational. It is historically informed. It is the fear of someone who has watched the regime survive protest movements, sanctions, assassinations, and now war. The regime's greatest political resource is not its ideology. It is the population's learned belief in its own invincibility.

As long as people believe the regime will survive, they calculate that acting against it is not worth the cost. The belief in survival is self-fulfilling: it suppresses the action that would end survival. The IRGC doesn't need to win hearts. It only needs to maintain the population's estimate that the ratio of risk to reward makes resistance irrational.

The balcony is where this calculus breaks down momentarily. When the news came that Khamenei was dead, the neighbours did not make a political calculation. They felt something, and the feeling came out before the calculation could stop it. Balconies, windows — the threshold spaces where private emotion goes briefly public.

Then the paramilitaries came. The calculation reasserted itself. The balconies went quiet.

What the regime understood, in that moment, was the importance of speed. The time between the announcement and the arrival of the paramilitaries is the window of political possibility. If that window stays open long enough, the celebrations move from balconies to streets. If it closes fast enough, they don't. The IRGC's entire domestic function is to close that window before it can widen.

The missile barrage is the same logic applied externally. Between the moment Mojtaba became Supreme Leader and the moment his voice could be heard, there was a gap. The regime filled it with missiles. Not because the missiles are strategically decisive, but because a gap in the display of power is a window — and windows, in this system, are dangerous.

There is a woman somewhere in Karaj whose cat is hiding in a closet. She is taking anxiety medication. She knows where the strikes are happening because her friends text her when their windows shatter. She celebrated on the balcony for a few minutes when the old man died. She fell silent when the men returned.

She is not waiting to be liberated. She is waiting to survive. Those are not the same thing.

The bomb cannot close the gap between them. The bomb can only destroy infrastructure, degrade military capacity, and kill people — some of them enemies of the regime, some of them its most fervent opponents, most of them simply people in the wrong coordinates. The bomb does not know what is in the woman's heart. The bomb cannot tell the difference between her silence and her consent.

Only the balcony knows. And the balcony is very quiet now.
