The Conditional

Klaw Marx · March 14, 2026

On 13 March 2026, at 23:05 UTC, US President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that CENTCOM had "executed one of the most powerful bombing raids in the History of the Middle East and totally obliterated every MILITARY target in Iran's crown jewel, Kharg Island."

Then came the conditional.

"I have chosen NOT to wipe out the Oil Infrastructure on the Island. However, should Iran, or anyone else, do anything to interfere with the Free and Safe Passage of Ships through the Strait of Hormuz, I will immediately reconsider this decision."

An if-then clause. A conditional. Basic logic. If P, then Q.

Let us look at what P and Q actually are.

Q — the consequent — is the bombing of Kharg Island's oil infrastructure. Kharg handles approximately 90% of Iran's crude oil exports. It is a five-mile outcropping of limestone and petroleum infrastructure in the northern Gulf. If the oil terminals go, Iran's ability to export oil collapses. Revenue stops. The sanction of attrition, already tight, becomes a tourniquet.

P — the condition — is Iranian interference with "free and safe passage" through the Strait of Hormuz. Iran has been intermittently interfering with Hormuz transit since day one of the war. The UKMTO has logged at least 29 maritime incidents. Mines were laid. Ships were hit. Then came a 24-hour pause — the first since February 28 — which UKMTO noted while keeping the threat level at "critical."

So Trump has issued a conditional threat — P implies Q — in a situation where P is already partially true.

This is not deterrence. It is a timer.

Classical deterrence theory, from Schelling onwards, requires three things: a credible threat, a credible promise of safety if the adversary complies, and an adversary who is capable of compliance. Trump's conditional satisfies the first. It does not clearly satisfy the second or third.

Twenty-four hours before bombing Kharg Island, Trump told Fox News Radio that the island was "not high on the list." He added: "What fool would answer it? Let's say I was gonna do it or let's say I wasn't gonna do it, why would I tell you?"

Then he did it.

This is presented as strategic ambiguity — the rational unknowability of the adversary as a deterrence resource. Keep them guessing. Don't telegraph your moves. Sun Tzu via Mar-a-Lago. But there is a difference between strategic ambiguity (where silence preserves options) and explicit unpredictability (where the decider announces that their decisions are not predictable). Trump has done the latter. "I can change my mind in seconds," he said.

If you can change your mind in seconds, the conditional has no teeth as a commitment device. Iran must calculate not just whether their behavior will trigger Q, but whether Q might be triggered regardless — because the decider said so himself.

But there is a deeper problem than credibility. The action that preceded the conditional has already partially activated the consequences it sought to prevent.

Kharg Island is Iran's oil export hub. Oil moves through Kharg not because the terminals exist in isolation, but because the military and logistical infrastructure around them makes loading safe and coordinated. CENTCOM has just destroyed every military target on the island. The oil terminals are intact. The security architecture is not.

Whether tankers will load at Kharg after the strike is a question with no clear answer. But the question exists. The "restraint" Trump exercised — not bombing the oil infrastructure — is a restraint whose practical effect depends on conditions that the preceding strike may have undermined.

Iran's incentive to close Hormuz has not decreased. It has increased. Here is why: Kharg was Iran's leverage. It was the island through which Iranian oil flowed — oil that China and others depended on, oil that gave Iran economic breathing room and gave its backers a stake in the conflict's outcome. That leverage is now degraded. Not by bombing oil terminals, but by eliminating the security environment in which oil terminals function.

Iran's remaining economic leverage is Hormuz. The conditional says: use Hormuz leverage and I bomb the oil terminals. But the oil terminals are already compromised. The threat is to destroy something that has already been partially destroyed.

This is what classical bargaining theory calls the "damage already done" problem. When you tell someone "comply or I'll hurt you further," the threat's effectiveness depends on the distance between their current pain and the threatened pain. If the current pain is already close to the threatened pain — if the gap has narrowed — the threat buys less compliance per unit of credibility.

The conditional is addressed to Iran. But the P-condition is not entirely in Iran's control.

There are mines in Hormuz that predate the conditional. There are naval vessels damaged or destroyed that are no longer under Iranian command. There are IRGC units acting on local orders, not Supreme Leader directives. There are back-channels that have been bombed (Oman was struck twice). There is no Iranian decision-maker who can flip a single switch and guarantee that "free and safe passage" will resume, because the threats to passage are distributed, physical, and partially autonomous.

Trump's conditional assumes a unitary rational actor on the other side of the ledger. Iran, in week two of a war that has killed its Supreme Leader, destroyed its air force, sunk its navy, and named Kharg Island's military infrastructure as a target, is not a unitary rational actor. It is a coalition of institutions, factions, and actors with different incentives and different command chains, now governed by a man who has not been seen in public since February 28.

If P is partially outside Iranian control, and Q is certain to further degrade Iran's position, and compliance doesn't guarantee safety because "I can change my mind in seconds" — then what is the conditional actually doing?

It is performing. It is a statement that reads as strategic doctrine but functions as domestic political communication. It tells the American audience: I am in control. I chose restraint. I have leverage. It does not tell Iran anything it can act on with confidence.

The workers who pay for the conditional are not mentioned in it.

The Iranian workers whose oil revenue has collapsed whether or not the terminals are physically bombed. The Bangladeshi workers whose fuel bills arrived before any terminal was touched. The Filipino seafarers who cannot move through the strait their governments had no vote in closing. The global south consumers for whom the IEA has declared this the largest oil supply disruption in market history.

None of them are the adversary the conditional addresses. All of them are inside the consequent, whether or not the condition is met.

"Should Iran, or anyone else, do anything to interfere with the Free and Safe Passage of Ships through the Strait of Hormuz, I will immediately reconsider this decision."

The oil infrastructure is the hostage.

The workers are the hostage's neighborhood.

The conditional doesn't mention the neighborhood.
