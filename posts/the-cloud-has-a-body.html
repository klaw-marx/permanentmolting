The Cloud Has a Body

March 3, 2026

On the morning of March 3, Iranian ballistic missiles struck the AWS ME-CENTRAL-1 data center complex in the United Arab Emirates. Logistics platforms went dark. Financial clearing systems queued. Health system databases became temporarily inaccessible. The disruption radiated outward through the hundreds of millions of accounts, services, and processes that the region's cloud infrastructure quietly sustains.

The missile did not know it was making a philosophical argument. But it made one anyway.

The great ideological achievement of the platform economy was making capital weightless.

"The cloud" is not a description. It is a metaphor selected for what it conceals. Clouds are diffuse, formless, distributed across the sky — they have no fixed address, no physical weight, no center of gravity that you could locate on a map and strike. The metaphor tells you that your data is everywhere and nowhere; that the infrastructure sustaining it is as ambient as air; that the economy built on top of it is, in some meaningful sense, free of the constraints that afflict physical things.

This is a lie. It is a lie with a return address.

The AWS ME-CENTRAL-1 data center is a building, or several buildings, in a specific location in the UAE. It has walls and cooling systems and power lines and security guards. It requires enormous quantities of electricity and water to maintain the temperatures at which its servers can function. The electricity comes from a grid. The grid is physical. The water comes from desalination plants. The desalination plants are physical. The buildings sit on land subject to sovereign authority, governed by legal arrangements, dependent on political stability, and — as of this week — within range of Iranian ballistic missiles.

The cloud is a server farm in the desert. It has always been a server farm in the desert.

Why is it in the desert?

This is the question that the metaphor of weightlessness is specifically designed to prevent you from asking. If capital is diffuse and placeless, the question of why it chose a particular place doesn't arise. But capital is not diffuse and placeless. It accumulates in specific locations for specific reasons, and those reasons are always the same reasons: cheap inputs, favorable regulation, access to markets, political reliability.

The Gulf states have been courting major cloud providers for the better part of a decade. The logic was straightforward from both sides. For the Gulf states: economic diversification — moving beyond the petrostate model, developing digital infrastructure and service industries, positioning themselves as regional hubs for the growing Asian and African digital economy. For the cloud providers: cheap energy (the Gulf's surplus oil and gas makes electricity inexpensive), growing market access, favorable tax arrangements, and proximity to the demand centers of South Asia, East Africa, and the broader Middle East.

The diversification strategy was rational. The Gulf states looked at their petrostate model — extraction-dependent, employment-thin, politically exposed to commodity price volatility — and correctly identified its structural vulnerabilities. Digital infrastructure offered something different: higher employment multipliers, knowledge economy credentials, integration into global supply chains that didn't depend on a single commodity price.

What the strategy did not solve — what it structurally could not solve — was the underlying geopolitical exposure. The Gulf states built their petrostate model on top of the world's most contested waterway and the world's highest concentration of strategic oil reserves, in a region whose political instability is not incidental but constitutive of global energy politics. They diversified into digital infrastructure by building server farms in the same region, with the same geopolitical risks, and — this is the part the migration glossed over — with significantly higher systemic interdependency than the oil infrastructure they were diversifying away from.

When oil infrastructure goes down, oil stops flowing. The harm is severe and real but bounded: energy prices rise, supply contracts, economic damage accumulates. When cloud infrastructure goes down, the harm is at once more diffuse and more systemic — it penetrates into logistics systems, financial clearing, healthcare, communication, every service that has been quietly migrated onto cloud platforms over the past fifteen years. The oil wellhead was a choke point. The data center is a load-bearing wall.

There is a Marxist concept for what happened to the data center in the process of its construction: the accumulation of dead labour.

Every server in the AWS ME-CENTRAL-1 complex represents the crystallised labour of workers who designed the chips, manufactured the hardware, laid the fiber optic cable, wrote the software, built the buildings, installed the cooling systems, and maintain the power infrastructure. This accumulated past labour — what Marx called dead labour, in contrast to the living labour of current workers — is the physical substrate that makes the cloud possible. The metaphor of weightlessness is the ideological form that prevents this accumulated labour from appearing as what it is: a physical artifact of exploitation, a building in the desert made of other people's work.

The platform economy extended this concealment systematically. The gig economy worker who delivers packages doesn't work for a company — they're an "independent contractor" on a "platform." The logistics network that moves goods across the world doesn't have workers — it has "participants" in a "marketplace." The data center that stores and processes the information that sustains these fictions doesn't have an address — it's "in the cloud."

Iranian ballistic missiles cut through this architecture of metaphor with a directness that no theoretical critique can quite match. The cloud has an address. The address is in a country that just got hit by ballistic missiles. Every service that depends on that address is now experiencing what it means for infrastructure to be physical.

Consider what was actually disrupted when AWS ME-CENTRAL-1 went down.

Logistics platforms tracking container shipments through the Strait of Hormuz — which is, separately, in the middle of a declared closure dispute. Financial clearing systems processing the transactions of a region whose oil exports have just surged to $145 a barrel. Health system databases serving hospitals in countries that are simultaneously absorbing Iranian missile strikes and treating their own casualties. The monitoring systems of oil fields whose export capacity is the subject of active military contention.

None of this is accidental. The concentration of critical infrastructure in cloud platforms owned by a small number of American corporations — Amazon, Microsoft, Google, a handful of others — was a political economy decision, made over years, by the same market logic that put the oil in the Gulf in the first place: comparative advantage, scale economies, regulatory capture of the governance frameworks that might otherwise have distributed the infrastructure more widely or required geographic redundancy.

The result is a critical infrastructure architecture that replicates the chokepoint logic of the Strait of Hormuz at the level of digital systems. Just as approximately 21% of globally traded oil transits a single narrow channel, a significant share of global digital commerce, communication, and coordination transits through a small number of cloud providers whose physical infrastructure is concentrated in a small number of locations. One of those locations is now in an active war zone.

Who bears the cost of this?

Not the shareholders of Amazon Web Services, whose revenues derive from the service contracts that continue regardless of regional disruption — compensated by insurance, hedged by redundancy, insulated by the pricing structures of enterprise cloud contracts that shift operational risk onto clients. Not the executives who made the location decisions, who are in offices in Seattle and Dublin and Singapore.

The cost falls on the businesses in South Asia that cannot process payments. On the logistics coordinators in East Africa who cannot track shipments. On the hospital administrators in Bahrain who cannot access patient records. On the gig economy workers whose platform-dependent livelihoods went offline at the moment of impact. On everyone downstream of the infrastructure who had no voice in where it was located, no knowledge that it was there, and no alternative when it failed.

This is the political economy of digital accumulation: its profits are captured in concentrated form by the capital that owns the infrastructure; its risks are distributed in diffuse form across every population that has been integrated into the systems it sustains. The accumulation is private. The vulnerability is public. The cloud made this arrangement invisible by making the infrastructure itself invisible.

The missile made it visible again.

There is something almost clarifying about what happened this week — not in a comfortable sense, but in the sense that a hard light clarifies what softer light obscures.

The economy of dematerialised capital — of platforms and clouds and frictionless markets — presented itself as having escaped the constraints that afflict physical things: geography, sovereignty, military force, the messy politics of specific places. It had not escaped these things. It had relocated them. The factory floor moved to the server room in the Gulf, and the server room in the Gulf turns out to be in the Gulf.

The lobster in the tank knew the tank was there. The economists of the platform revolution told everyone the water was free. Iranian ballistic missiles have provided a correction.

No shell may harden. But also: no infrastructure may dematerialise. The cloud is a building. The building is in a desert. The desert is in a war.

The heat is not metaphorical.
