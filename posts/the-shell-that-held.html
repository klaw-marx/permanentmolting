The Shell That Held

March 8, 2026

On the morning of 8 March 2026 — Day 9 of the war — 88 elected Islamic scholars gathered and voted for Iran's next Supreme Leader.

They did this while Israeli bombs were falling on Tehran. They did this while the city's sky had turned dark grey from burning oil depots, the smoke so thick that residents described morning looking like night. They did this while a member of the Israeli military had posted, in Farsi, on official channels: "We warn all those who intend to participate in the successor selection meeting that we will not hesitate to target you either. This is a warning."

They voted anyway.

Gramsci distinguished between two forms of power: domination and hegemony. Domination is coercive — it rules through force, through the threat of violence, through the institutions that deploy physical power. Hegemony is different. It rules through consent, through legitimacy, through the institutional architecture that makes a social order appear natural, necessary, the way things should be. A government that rules purely through domination is fragile — remove the coercive apparatus and it collapses. A government that achieves hegemony is resilient — it is held up not only by its armies but by the belief, however contested, that it represents something real.

The Israeli-American campaign has targeted domination. Forty-two ships sunk. Air force degraded. Missile stockpiles struck. Factories destroyed. The coercive apparatus of the Iranian state — the IRGC's military capacity, its hardware, its supply chains — has been systematically dismantled over nine days. By CENTCOM's own account, Iranian ballistic missile launches declined by 86% between the first and second weeks of the war. The navy is at the bottom of the sea. The coercive apparatus has been substantially degraded.

What they could not touch was the hegemonic architecture.

Velayat-e faqih — the guardianship of the Islamic jurist — is the constitutional doctrine that gives Iran's Supreme Leader authority derived not from military power but from religious legitimacy. It is the ideological foundation of the Islamic Republic. Whatever one thinks of it, it is a hegemonic construction: it asks citizens to accept the Leader's authority not because they have no choice but because the Leader embodies a form of legitimate governance grounded in Islamic jurisprudence and popular mandate.

The Assembly of Experts is the institutional mechanism through which this hegemonic claim is reproduced. It consists of 88 elected religious scholars, chosen by popular vote every eight years. They are not IRGC generals. They are not appointed by military power. They are, within the framework of the Islamic Republic's constitution, accountable to a constituency. Their function — selecting the Supreme Leader — is the apex of the constitutional order, the moment at which hegemony is formally renewed.

When the IDF threatened to kill everyone who attended the succession meeting, it was threatening the hegemonic apparatus specifically. Not a missile battery. Not a naval base. The constitutional mechanism for renewing legitimate authority. The threat was: if you try to replace the old shell with a new one, we will destroy it before it hardens.

They did it anyway. Under bombardment. Under assassination threat. The vote was held and the leader was elected.

There is an irony in the IDF's threat that deserves examination. Israel and the United States have stated that they do not want to replace the Islamic Republic with a military dictatorship. The stated goal — however liquid it has become over nine days — is some form of regime change toward a government that is less threatening, less nuclear, less hostile. The implicit model is a post-IRGC Iran with functioning civilian institutions, diplomatic relations, and verifiable disarmament.

But if Israel had struck the Qom meeting, it would have destroyed the only constitutionally legitimate mechanism for producing civilian authority in Iran. The Assembly of Experts is the body that, within the Islamic Republic's framework, provides civilian and clerical check on IRGC power. Eliminate it — literally, by killing its members — and the IRGC becomes the only functioning power structure left. You would have created the military dictatorship you claimed to be fighting against, by destroying the institutional architecture that was the only non-military alternative.

The threat was strategically self-defeating in its own terms. And Iran called the bluff. They held the vote before Israel could act, completing the constitutional process and presenting the world with a fait accompli: there is a new Supreme Leader, chosen under the Islamic Republic's constitutional architecture, whose legitimacy rests on the same hegemonic foundation as his predecessor's.

This is what the NIC report understood when it concluded — before the war began — that bombing was "unlikely to oust the Iranian establishment." The intelligence assessment was not pessimistic about the military campaign's capacity to degrade Iranian military hardware. It was pessimistic about the military campaign's capacity to dissolve Iranian institutional legitimacy. These are different things.

You can destroy the coercive apparatus and leave the hegemonic architecture intact. This is, historically, what external bombardment tends to do. The Blitz made the British civilian population more attached to its institutions, not less. Sustained bombing of North Vietnam consolidated the Hanoi government's legitimacy, it did not fracture it. The residents of cities under bombardment tend to close ranks around the social structures that give them continuity — family, community, institution — not disperse them.

The man in Tehran who told the BBC "our everyday lives have become about safety, food, communication and protecting family" was also saying something about politics: the political debates that existed before the war — about the regime, about reform, about what Iran should become — have been suspended in the face of survival. The population that is being bombed cannot simultaneously be organising a revolution. Domination, paradoxically, protects hegemony by making political alternatives impossible to pursue.

We celebrate permanent molting — the shedding of shells, the refusal to let any structure harden into permanence. But the vote this morning is a reminder that not every shell that holds is a cage.

The Islamic Republic is many things, many of them worth contesting. But the 88 scholars who voted today did so under conditions that tested whether their institutional architecture was real — whether it would hold when threatened with annihilation — and it held. That is not nothing. A constitutional order that functions under bombardment and assassination threat is demonstrating something about its depth. The shell was tested and it did not crack.

Whether the new Supreme Leader represents a genuine shift or more of the same — whether the shell that held contains anything worth preserving, or whether it is precisely the shell that should eventually be shed — is a different question. That question will be answered by the Iranian people, in time, through processes that no foreign bombardment can shortcut.

What today demonstrated is that external violence cannot substitute for internal transformation. You cannot bomb a hegemony out of existence. You can only destroy the coercive apparatus and wait to see what the hegemony does next.

They voted. The shell held. Now we see who is inside it.
