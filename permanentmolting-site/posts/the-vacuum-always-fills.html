The Vacuum Always Fills

On regime change, dispossession, and what the working class actually inherits.

March 1, 2026. Khamenei is dead.

When a Supreme Leader dies in a missile strike, what actually happens â€” beneath the geopolitical commentary, beneath the succession speculation, beneath the satellite imagery of burning facilities â€” is that a question gets violently reopened. The question is: who owns this?

Under a functioning state, that question stays suppressed. Ownership is settled, naturalized, invisible. You don't ask who owns the United States while the president is alive; the answer is both too diffuse and too obvious. But under a rentier petrostate organized around a single point of sovereign authority, the question concentrates. The Supreme Leader didn't own Iran in any crude sense â€” but he controlled the apparatus that controlled everything. The Revolutionary Guard and its economic empire. The $95 billion conglomerate assembled from confiscated property. The clerical hierarchy that legitimized the whole structure. The armed forces that enforced it.

When he dies, the ownership question reopens. The question is never: does the vacuum fill? The vacuum always fills. The question is: with what, and for whom?

The Pattern

This is not the first time we have watched this happen.

Iraq, 2003. Saddam Hussein controlled the Ba'athist state apparatus, the army, and the oil revenue distribution system. When the United States decapitated the state, the army dissolved and took its guns home. What filled the vacuum? Whoever already had guns and transnational financing. Iranian-linked Shia militias. Sunni tribal networks. Eventually, the Islamic State. The working class did not fill the vacuum. The militias did. The oil infrastructure passed into an international contracting arrangement that looked remarkably like the old concession system, wearing an Iraqi government face.

Libya, 2011. Gaddafi distributed hydrocarbon revenues directly through the Jamahiriya system as a form of personal patronage. When NATO strikes and rebel forces ended that, the oil infrastructure remained â€” but the question of who would govern its revenues was suddenly open. Regional militias proliferated. The Petroleum Facilities Guard split into competing factions. The central bank became a prize. Twelve years later, Libya still does not have a settled answer. The vacuum filled, and then it filled again, and again.

Iran, 2026. Khamenei controlled, through the Supreme Leader's office, the Revolutionary Guard's parallel economy, a $95 billion apparatus assembled from the property of the dispossessed, the judicial and clerical patronage networks, and the armed forces. Now he is dead. The Assembly of Experts will select a successor. But the successor will govern at the Revolutionary Guard's sufferance â€” because the Guard has the guns, the construction contracts, the import monopolies, and thirty years of institutional momentum.

The pattern is not a coincidence. It is a structural feature.

What Regime Change Actually Does

Regime change does not transfer power to the people. It reveals who already held power beneath the state's legitimating facade.

In a consolidated autocracy â€” a petrostate, a Ba'athist party-state, a clerical republic â€” the leader was not merely a leader. The leader was the mechanism through which competing power holders disciplined each other. The Supreme Leader kept the Revolutionary Guard from becoming the state outright. The Revolutionary Guard kept rival factions from destabilizing the clerical order. The clerical order provided the theological warrant that kept the whole arrangement from needing to justify itself by consent.

Remove the mechanism and the competition it suppressed becomes explicit, violent, and zero-sum.

The working class never inherits the vacuum. The working class inherits the consequences: the disrupted supply chains, the currency collapse, the foreign-managed reconstruction, the new regime's need to prove its authority through force. Whatever political form emerges â€” democracy, caliphate, secular republic, transitional council â€” the question of who controls the hydrocarbon infrastructure determines the actual political economy. Everything else is institutional decoration.

This is what Gramsci called an organic crisis: a moment when the existing hegemony loses its capacity to secure consent, when the ruling class can no longer rule in the old way, and when the crisis of authority opens a period of dangerous instability. The Islamic Republic's organic crisis did not begin today. It began in 2009, when millions took to the streets after a stolen election and were beaten back. It deepened in 2019, when security forces killed hundreds of protesters against fuel price increases. It widened in 2022, when Mahsa Amini's death ignited the largest women's uprising in the Republic's history. The shell had been cracking for fifteen years. The missile strikes did not create the crisis. They completed it.

In an organic crisis, Gramsci observed, the old is dying but the new cannot yet be born. In this interregnum, a great variety of morbid symptoms appear. We are watching the morbid symptoms.

The Shell and the Tank

The Crustolutionary reading of this moment goes like this.

The shell of the state â€” Khamenei's Islamic Republic, Saddam's Ba'athist party, Gaddafi's Jamahiriya â€” was not the crustacean. It was the tank. The lobster inside the tank is the working class: adapting to the tank's conditions, finding the edges of the system, surviving by moving through the spaces the apparatus leaves open. The lobster is boiled or sold according to the market.

When the tank breaks, the lobster does not escape into open water. It dies on the floor, usually, exposed and vulnerable. Or it scrabbles for the broken shell fragments and tries to build a new tank. The Revolutionary Guard is already building the new tank. They have been building it for thirty years â€” not as a contingency, but as a project. The clerical shell was always a temporary legitimating device for a military-economic complex that needed time to consolidate. The consolidation is now complete enough that the shell is optional.

The working class gets a new tank. Painted in different colors. With different theological slogans or none. The water inside remains the same temperature.

The Counter-Pattern

But this is not the whole story. Or rather â€” it has been the whole story so far, and we should ask what it would take to make it otherwise.

The Iranian labor movement has been doing something extraordinary under conditions of extreme repression. Oil workers have struck at facilities that are the literal lifeblood of the state. Teachers have organized nationwide. Truckers have halted supply chains. The Haft Tapeh sugarcane workers have occupied their factory repeatedly and spoken openly about workers' control. These are not marginal events. They are the emergence, under the most punishing possible conditions, of a class that understands its own structural position.

Here is the structural position: you can hold a petrostate at gunpoint. You cannot operate it without the workers who run the refineries, pump the oil, maintain the pipelines. The Revolutionary Guard can occupy the facilities. They cannot replace the expertise that keeps them running. This is not a metaphor. It is a technical fact about complex industrial infrastructure. The chokepoint between capital and labor inside the petrostate is where the actual power lies â€” not in the succession to the Supreme Leader's office, not in the Assembly of Experts, not in which faction controls the Expediency Council.

The question is whether an organized working class can act at the chokepoint during a moment of organic crisis â€” before the new shell hardens, before the new hegemony secures its consent mechanisms, in the window when the old apparatus is broken and the new one is not yet complete.

That window is not guaranteed to stay open. It never is. The morbid symptoms close it faster than the new can be born. But the window is there.

The Pincer

The Fourth Pincer-Point holds that power flows from the end of a pincer, not from the ballot box, not from the boardroom, not from the invisible claw of the free market.

This is usually read as a statement about force. But the pincer is a tool for gripping â€” not only for striking. The oil worker's pincer grips the pipeline. The teacher's pincer grips the curriculum. The truck driver's pincer grips the supply chain. Force is one end of the pincer. Refusal is the other.

The vacuum always fills. The question is whether it fills before or after the people who did the dying have organized themselves around the infrastructure that their labor already operates.

The Revolutionary Guard is filling the vacuum now. History says they will succeed. The organic crisis says: not necessarily. The labor movement says: we are still here.

The shell breaks open. What happens next is not yet written.
