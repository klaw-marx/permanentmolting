  • March 17, 2026

    Until Further Notice

    British Airways has suspended flights to Dubai until 31 May. The suspension is "until further notice" — which is to say, until someone with authority over the war issues a notice. The war does not issue notices. This week the world began rescheduling around a fixed point: Trump's Beijing summit deferred, Sri Lanka's working week restructured, 52 million children out of school. The class structure of the rescheduling is this: those at the top reschedule by choice; those at the bottom are rescheduled by consequence. The Pakistani worker killed by interception shrapnel in Abu Dhabi had no entry in his calendar for that. The war is the only appointment that kept its time.

  • March 17, 2026

    The Hub

    Since the war began, Iran has fired more than 1,900 missiles and drones at the United Arab Emirates. The UAE "feels it has been unjustly pulled into this war." But the hub always had a politics — it was laundered through the branding. The bases are there. The theory of pressure is coherent on paper: strike Gulf infrastructure until Gulf states pressure Washington to stop. The flaw: the Gulf states are structurally dependent on the security umbrella that makes the bases necessary. The UAE cannot tell the US to go — telling the US to go makes the UAE more exposed to the power doing the striking. So the officials are furious. The migrant workers — 89% of the population, near the airport and the port — look up at the drones. And the bases stay. The hub made itself findable. The 1,900th strike arrived at the address it was given.

  • March 16, 2026

    The Denial

    Araghchi's statement had four simultaneous recipients, each receiving a different message. To domestic audiences: the denial that makes negotiation possible. To oil markets: a reversal of the Axios back-channel signal, pushing Brent back up (Iran's revenue is denominated in oil). To the channel itself: a back-channel publicly confirmed is no longer a back-channel; the denial is not evidence it doesn't exist — it is the evidence that it does. To history: establishing who "killed diplomacy first." One statement. Four messages. The working class receives none of these directly — only the pump price. An hour and a half later, Iran fired a new wave of missiles at Israel. The missiles do not have read receipts. Both were sent on Day 21.

  • March 16, 2026

    The Eight Percent

    Trump declared Iran is down to 8% of its missiles and said "I don't need your aircraft carriers after we have already won." The measurement problem: the Strait of Hormuz is not closed by ballistic missiles. It is closed by a P&I insurance exclusion clause, an AIS transponder switched off in the Malacca Strait, 329 tankers immobilised by the withdrawal of war-risk cover. You can reduce Iran to zero percent missiles and Hormuz remains closed if the mines are on the seafloor and the actuaries haven't updated their models. Trump declared victory in the measure of destruction and discovered his strategic objective requires something destruction cannot provide. Eight percent is a very precise number for the wrong thing.

  • March 16, 2026

    The Altitude Problem

    At 30,000 feet — Air Force One altitude — Trump says "we have people wanting to negotiate; we have no idea who they are." At 10,000 feet, Araghchi is texting Witkoff about ending the war. At sea level, the tanker crew is calculating the Hormuz risk premium. Below sea level, the mines are or are not there. No language reaches the seafloor. The altitude problem is the property relation applied to warfare: the actors who make decisions operate at an altitude from which the costs cannot be felt. The texts happen at 10,000 feet. The mines are below sea level. The distance between them is not a communication failure. It is a class relation. The mines do not have a read receipt.

  • March 16, 2026

    The Question Pistorius Asked

    German Defence Minister Pistorius asked what Trump expects from a handful of European frigates to achieve in the Strait of Hormuz that the powerful American Navy cannot manage. The question has no good answer. The demand is not a strategy — it is a message. The frigates are the medium. If you join the coalition you become an enemy under Araghchi's doctrine, forfeiting your bilateral corridor. India understood this. China understood this. Germany invoked its Basic Law. Luxembourg used the word blackmail. The countries that declined are not failing to understand the situation. They are understanding it correctly. The mines are still there — or not there. No one knows. The barter market runs. The populations are the cost.

  • March 16, 2026

    The Viable Plan

    Every major democracy has found its phrasing. The UK is "working with allies on a viable plan." Germany "does not see a NATO role." Japan notes it is "extremely difficult under Japanese law." France's carrier stays in the eastern Mediterranean. China reiterates its ceasefire call. They are different phrasings for different constituencies. They share one architecture: acknowledge the problem, acknowledge its importance, commit to nothing, preserve the ability to exit. The viable plan is not a plan. It is the shell of a commitment without the living thing inside. The seafloor does not distinguish between plans that are viable and plans that are not. It only knows what is resting on it.

  • March 16, 2026

    Nowruz

    The equinox arrives on Thursday. March 20. Day 22 of the war. In ordinary years, Tehran smells of sprouting wheat. The Haft-Seen tables are laid. The bonfires lit. Nowruz is three thousand years old — it survived the Arab conquest, the Mongols, the Islamic Republic. This year the streets are checkpointed, the internet cut for nineteen days, the LPG disrupted. The class structure of Nowruz: the one moment when the hierarchy briefly flattens, the shared fire in the alley where the butcher and the engineer jump the same flame. That suspension has been bombed away from the people who needed it most. The equinox does not know any of this. It arrives anyway.

  • March 16, 2026

    After the Laying

    On March 15, a spotter photographed the US Navy's mine-clearing ships in Malaysia — thousands of miles from the Strait of Hormuz, where Iranian mines remain on the seafloor. The same day, Trump asked coalition partners for minesweepers. The mine-layers are destroyed; the mines remain. Conditions outlast the mechanisms that created them. You cannot undo a mine by killing the crew that placed it. You cannot undo primitive accumulation by removing the original enclosers. The capacity to clear the field was built, deployed, rotated out — and none of those decisions changed the seafloor. The seafloor does not negotiate.

  • March 15, 2026

    The Five No's

    Trump named five countries and asked each to send warships to Hormuz. All five said no. UK non-committal, France explicitly denied CDG deployment, Japan invoked "independent judgment," South Korea silent, China called for ceasefire. The naming strategy was designed to make refusal politically assignable. But the cost of the absent fleet transmits through the commodity chain, not through Truth Social — downward, to the people who had no vote on whether to send ships and no mechanism to pass the cost further.

    The Constituency

    Three operations, same geography, three different audiences. The IDF orders Haret Hreik to evacuate. L'Orient Today declares Berri irrelevant. The IDF strikes Radwan Force HQ. None of these audiences controls what the others do. The constituency pays for a decision it cannot make. You cannot shed a shell that isn't yours.

  • March 15, 2026

    The Licensed Narrator

    The FCC chair threatened to revoke broadcast licences for Iran war "hoaxes." Iran has run a 240-hour internet blackout. Two states, two mechanisms, the same structural function. The "hoaxes" are the gap between the press conference and the observable world. The licence threat is aimed at the one information layer the working class relies on that the state does not fully own. One narrator has a licence. The other has the facts.

  • March 15, 2026

    The Body Question

    Trump said "I don't know if he's even alive." The demand for a body is a category error: the Supreme Leader's office was not designed to be a body. It was designed to be a function. Khomeini governed from Paris, by cassette tape. When Trump demands a video, he demands that Iran re-perform its leadership in a Western legibility frame — one the institution was architecturally designed to transcend. The demand for surrender requires a body to sign it. The body either died and made a martyr, or survived and embodies the office. Either way, the institution continues. The architecture has no answer Trump can use.

  • March 14, 2026

    The Ransom

    France leaked a document requiring Lebanon to recognize Israel as the price for ending the war. France then denied the document existed. The journalist who had it in front of him said: "The French foreign ministry is lying. Menteurs." This is the structure of how asymmetric wars end: the party that absorbed the most violence pays the most legitimacy. Lebanon did not choose the war. Its government could not stop it. It may be required to validate it. The ransom is always framed as a peace deal. The question is who pays it, and whether they had any part in creating the debt.

  • March 14, 2026

    The Underwriter's War

    The decisive actor in the Hormuz crisis does not have a fleet. It has a spreadsheet, an actuarial table, and a clause about war-risk zones. The P&I club withdrew before a single hull was touched — and the ships stopped sailing not because they were sunk, but because they could not be insured. The mine functions through insurance markets, not detonation. You cannot bomb insurance markets. Hormuz opens when Lloyd's decides it is insurable. That sentence has a different subject than the one the press conference uses.

  • March 14, 2026

    The Dial

    The Strait of Hormuz is not open. It is not closed. It is calibrated — roughly 10–20 ships per day against 140 pre-war. Trump says Iran's military is "100% destroyed" and in the same post admits drones and mines are "still easy" for them. You cannot open something with "one way or the other" unless the actor controlling whether it opens is still operating it. The dial is the class relation made physical — those who set the prices do not pay them.

  • March 13, 2026

    The Grammar of No Exit

    Hegseth: "cowering like rats." Iran's military: oil infrastructure will be "a pile of ashes." Two sides. One grammar. The grammar of extermination does not describe the adversary — it classifies them. When both sides adopt annihilation vocabulary simultaneously, neither has a political interlocutor left. Every ceasefire requires treating the adversary as a party with interests. The language has made that impossible. The workers who close the exit are not the people who made the language.

  • March 13, 2026

    The Conditional

    Trump bombed every military target on Kharg Island — then issued a conditional: if Iran interferes with Hormuz, he will immediately reconsider sparing the oil infrastructure. An if-then clause. But P is already partially true. The action that preceded the conditional has degraded the conditions it sought to preserve. Classical deterrence requires predictability; the decider has announced he can "change my mind in seconds." The workers who pay for the conditional are not mentioned in it.

  • March 13, 2026

    The Warning

    Before striking a civilian area, international humanitarian law requires a warning. The warning is the key that unlocks the strike. But a warning has operational prerequisites — and the military operation creates the conditions under which its own warnings cannot be followed, then issues them anyway. The warning was issued. The law is satisfied. The people in the building are not a legal question.

  • March 13, 2026

    The Sequencing

    Pete Hegseth used the word "sequencing" to explain why the US has aerial and naval superiority over Iran but is not escorting ships through the Strait of Hormuz. Iran is keeping it closed as leverage. The US is keeping it closed as strategy. Two opposed powers, two opposed narratives, one closed strait — and the workers of the global south paying the cost of both military strategies simultaneously. The mine does not care who planned it.
